# Medical Protocols and Safety Procedures - Summer Springboard 2026

## General Medical Principles

### Staff Scope of Practice

**What staff CAN do:**
- Provide basic first aid for minor injuries
- Distribute over-the-counter medications FROM student's own supply
- Assist student in taking their own prescribed medication
- Apply bandages, ice packs, basic wound care
- Monitor student wellbeing
- Contact medical professionals

**What staff CANNOT do:**
- Diagnose medical conditions
- Prescribe medication
- Give students medication from staff's personal supply (PROHIBITED)
- Make medical treatment decisions
- Determine if medical care is needed (when in doubt, seek professional evaluation)

**WHY:** Staff are not medical professionals. Exceeding scope of practice creates liability and endangers students.

### First Aid Certification Requirements

**All Campus Directors and Assistant Campus Directors must have:**
- Valid CPR certification (Adult)
- Valid First Aid certification
- **UC Berkeley staff specifically:** Must have both Adult AND Pediatric CPR certification

**Certifications must be:**
- From hybrid or in-person courses (online-only not accepted)
- Current/valid for duration of program
- Uploaded to HR before program start (May 15 for CDs, June 1 for Mentors)

**WHY:** Ensures trained staff available to respond to medical emergencies.

**Reimbursement:** Campus Directors and Mentors reimbursed for certification costs via Divvy.

## Medication Management

### Student Medication Policy

**Student medications must:**
- Be in original prescription bottles with student's name
- Include dosage and administration instructions
- Be stored securely in student's room OR turned in to Campus Director (case-by-case)
- Be self-administered by student
- Be documented on student medical form

**Staff role:**
- Remind students to take medications at prescribed times
- Observe students taking medications if needed for accountability
- Document in daily logs if monitoring required
- Never touch or handle student medications

### Controlled Medications and High-Risk Medications

**Examples of medications that may require Campus Director oversight:**
- EpiPens (allergy emergency)
- Inhalers (asthma)
- Insulin (diabetes)
- Anti-seizure medications
- Psychiatric medications
- Narcotic pain medications

**Special protocols:**
- Campus Director discusses storage plan with parent and student during pre-program contact
- Some may be kept in CD office for safety
- Students still self-administer but staff supervise
- Daily medication logs maintained
- Parents updated on compliance

**EpiPens specifically:**
- Student should carry at all times
- Staff should know which students have severe allergies
- Staff should know where EpiPen is located
- Campus Director may keep backup EpiPen in office
- All staff should know how to use EpiPen in emergency

### CRITICAL MEDICATION INCIDENT FROM TRAINING

**SCENARIO REVIEWED:** Mentor gave student ibuprofen from mentor's personal supply for twisted ankle.

**WHY THIS IS PROHIBITED:**
- Constitutes practicing medicine without license
- Student could have allergy to medication
- Medication could interact with student's other medications
- Student could have condition making medication dangerous (kidney issues, bleeding disorders)
- Creates liability if student has adverse reaction
- Violates SSB policy

**CORRECT PROTOCOL:**
1. Student requests pain medication
2. Staff asks: "Do you have ibuprofen or pain medication with you?"
3. If YES: "Go ahead and take your own medication as prescribed. Let me know if pain doesn't improve."
4. If NO: "Let me contact your parent to see if you're approved to take ibuprofen. In the meantime, let's ice it and elevate it."
5. Campus Director contacts parent
6. Parent gives approval or disapproval
7. If approved, Campus Director purchases ibuprofen, gives to student in original packaging
8. Student self-administers
9. Document in notes

**CONSEQUENCES for giving student your personal medication:**
- First offense: Written warning and coaching
- Second offense: Dismissal from program
- If student has adverse reaction: Immediate dismissal, potential legal liability

### Over-the-Counter Medications

**Common student requests:**
- Pain relievers (ibuprofen, acetaminophen)
- Allergy medications
- Antacids
- Cough/cold medications

**Protocol:**
1. Check if student has their own supply
2. Check student medical form for allergies and contraindications
3. Contact parent for permission if not already documented
4. If approved, Campus Director provides in original packaging
5. Student self-administers
6. Document

**Pre-approved OTC medications:**
- Some parents may give blanket approval on medical forms
- Still check medical form for contraindications
- Still have student self-administer
- Still document

## Medical Emergency Response

### Life-Threatening Emergencies (Call 9-1-1 Immediately)

**Signs of life-threatening emergency:**
- Not breathing or difficulty breathing
- No pulse or irregular pulse
- Unresponsive or loss of consciousness
- Severe bleeding that won't stop
- Chest pain or pressure
- Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis - swelling of face/throat, difficulty breathing)
- Seizure (unless student has known seizure disorder and seizure typical)
- Head injury with loss of consciousness
- Poisoning or overdose
- Severe burns
- Suspected broken bone with deformity or limb looks abnormal
- Severe abdominal pain
- Choking

**RESPONSE PROTOCOL:**

**Immediately (within seconds):**
1. **Call 9-1-1** (or have someone else call while you stay with student)
2. Start CPR if trained and appropriate
3. Send someone to get AED if available and appropriate
4. Provide first aid within your scope
5. Do not move student unless in immediate danger

**Within first minute:**
1. Send staff member to call Campus Director (if not already present)
2. Send staff member to meet ambulance at designated entrance
3. Keep other students away from scene but supervised
4. Gather student medical information (medical form, medication list, insurance card)

**Within 2-3 minutes:**
1. Campus Director arrives and takes command
2. Campus Director calls parent IMMEDIATELY
   - "This is [name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] is having a medical emergency. We've called 9-1-1 and paramedics are on the way. [Brief description]. I'm going to stay on the phone with you and give you updates."
3. Campus Director notifies Program Director
4. Campus Director assigns staff to accompany student to hospital

**When paramedics arrive:**
1. Give them all medical information
2. Brief them on what happened
3. Follow their instructions
4. Staff member rides in ambulance if allowed OR follows in car
5. Campus Director continues phone contact with parents

**At hospital:**
1. Staff stays with student until parent arrives
2. Provide medical information to hospital
3. Update Campus Director regularly
4. Update parents regularly
5. DO NOT make medical decisions (parents or doctors make decisions)
6. DO NOT leave student alone

**Follow-up:**
1. Campus Director completes detailed incident report within 24 hours
2. Program Director notified of outcome
3. Plan for student's return to program or early departure
4. Insurance company notified if significant injury
5. Follow-up with parents
6. Staff debrief

### Non-Life-Threatening But Requires Medical Evaluation

**When to seek medical evaluation:**
- Persistent pain (doesn't improve with rest, ice, over-the-counter pain reliever)
- Possible fracture or sprain (swelling, can't put weight on it, can't move it normally)
- Fever over 101°F that doesn't respond to medication
- Vomiting or diarrhea lasting more than 6 hours
- Allergic reaction managed with EpiPen (ALWAYS go to hospital after EpiPen use)
- Worsening symptoms
- Rash with fever
- Suspected infection
- Student requests to see doctor
- Staff gut feeling that something isn't right

**RESPONSE PROTOCOL:**

**Step 1: Assessment (Campus Director)**
- Gather information about symptoms
- Check student medical form for relevant conditions
- Determine if urgent care or emergency room appropriate
- Urgent Care: Non-emergency but needs attention (sprains, minor cuts needing stitches, mild illness)
- Emergency Room: More serious but not immediately life-threatening (possible fracture, significant pain, moderate allergic reaction)

**Step 2: Parent Contact (Campus Director)**
- Call parent before going to medical facility
- Describe symptoms objectively
- Recommend level of care
- Get parent input
- Confirm they want student evaluated
- Get insurance information
- Ask about medical history relevant to current issue

**Step 3: Transportation**
- Uber/Lyft for urgent care (staff accompanies)
- Ambulance for emergency room if serious
- Campus vehicle if available and appropriate

**Step 4: At Medical Facility**
- Staff remains with student entire time
- Provides medical information to staff
- Student calls parent when doctor available if possible
- Staff updates Campus Director regularly
- Staff DOES NOT make treatment decisions (parent does)
- Staff ensures student understands discharge instructions

**Step 5: Return to Campus**
- Campus Director reviews discharge instructions
- Fills any prescriptions needed
- Plans for medication administration
- Plans for activity restrictions
- Notifies instructors if student will miss class
- Documents everything

**Step 6: Follow-Up**
- Campus Director completes incident report within 24 hours
- Parents updated on student's condition
- Program Director notified
- Monitor student recovery
- Adjust program participation as needed per medical advice

## Common Medical Situations

### Allergic Reactions

**Mild allergic reaction (itching, hives, sneezing):**
1. Ask about known allergies
2. Remove student from allergen if identified
3. Contact parent
4. Administer student's own antihistamine if parent approves
5. Monitor for worsening
6. Document

**Severe allergic reaction/Anaphylaxis (swelling of face/throat, difficulty breathing, rapid pulse, dizziness):**
1. **Call 9-1-1 immediately**
2. Use student's EpiPen if available (per training)
3. Have student lie down with legs elevated
4. Loosen tight clothing
5. Follow emergency protocol above
6. **IMPORTANT:** Student MUST go to hospital even if symptoms improve after EpiPen

**Food allergies:**
- Review all student food allergies before program
- Alert dining services
- Ensure student understands how to check ingredients
- Teach student to carry EpiPen at all times if severe allergy
- Brief all staff on students with severe food allergies

### Asthma Attacks

**Mild (student can talk, using inhaler helps):**
1. Have student use their inhaler
2. Move to quiet area
3. Encourage slow, deep breaths
4. Stay with student
5. If improves quickly, monitor and document
6. If doesn't improve in 15 minutes, escalate

**Severe (can't talk in full sentences, not improving with inhaler, lips/fingernails turning blue, extreme difficulty breathing):**
1. **Call 9-1-1 immediately**
2. Have student use inhaler
3. Keep student upright (sitting)
4. Stay calm and reassuring
5. Follow emergency protocol
6. Call parent immediately

### Seizures

**If student has known seizure disorder:**
- Review seizure action plan with parent before program
- Know what's typical for that student
- Know when to call 9-1-1 (usually if seizure lasts >5 minutes or student doesn't return to normal quickly)
- Have emergency medication plan if applicable

**First-time seizure or seizure in student without known disorder:**
1. **Call 9-1-1 immediately**
2. Protect student from injury (move furniture away, cushion head)
3. Time the seizure
4. DO NOT put anything in student's mouth
5. DO NOT restrain student
6. Turn student on side if possible (to keep airway clear)
7. After seizure, stay with student until paramedics arrive
8. Follow emergency protocol

### Concussion/Head Injury

**Any head injury with loss of consciousness:**
1. **Call 9-1-1 immediately**
2. Keep student still (possible neck injury)
3. Do not allow student to get up
4. Monitor breathing
5. Follow emergency protocol

**Head injury without loss of consciousness but concerning symptoms (confusion, severe headache, vomiting, unequal pupils, balance problems):**
1. Campus Director assesses
2. Call parent
3. Take to emergency room
4. Do not allow student to participate in activities until medically cleared

**Minor bump on head (no loss of consciousness, no concerning symptoms):**
1. Apply ice
2. Monitor for 24 hours
3. Wake student during night to check responsiveness
4. Inform instructors to watch for symptoms
5. Contact parent to inform
6. Document

**Return to activities after concussion:**
- Requires doctor's clearance
- Gradual return to activities per doctor's orders
- No physical activity until cleared

### Diabetes Management

**Before program:**
- Review diabetes management plan with parent
- Understand student's monitoring and medication schedule
- Know signs of low blood sugar and high blood sugar
- Have emergency contact for student's endocrinologist
- Ensure student has adequate supplies

**Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) - symptoms: shakiness, sweating, confusion, irritability, fast heartbeat:**
1. Give student fast-acting sugar (juice, glucose tablets, regular soda)
2. Have student check blood sugar if able
3. Have student rest for 15 minutes
4. Recheck blood sugar
5. If improves, give small snack with protein
6. Contact parent to inform
7. Document
8. If student loses consciousness or has seizure, call 9-1-1

**High blood sugar (hyperglycemia) - symptoms: extreme thirst, frequent urination, fruity breath, confusion:**
1. Have student check blood sugar
2. Contact parent immediately
3. Student may need to take insulin (under parent guidance)
4. Campus Director monitors closely
5. If severe or student vomiting, take to emergency room
6. Document

### Injuries

**Minor cuts and scrapes:**
1. Wash with soap and water
2. Apply antibiotic ointment if student not allergic
3. Bandage
4. Monitor for infection
5. Document in notes

**Deep cuts possibly requiring stitches:**
1. Apply direct pressure with clean cloth
2. Elevate if possible
3. Campus Director notified
4. Parent called
5. Take to urgent care
6. Follow non-emergency protocol

**Sprains and strains:**
1. RICE: Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation
2. Campus Director assesses
3. Parent called
4. If can't bear weight or severe pain, take to urgent care for x-ray
5. If improving with RICE, monitor
6. Limit activities as appropriate
7. Document

**Suspected fractures:**
1. Don't move injured area
2. Immobilize if trained to do so
3. Apply ice
4. Campus Director notified immediately
5. Parent called
6. Take to emergency room (ambulance if severe pain or deformity)
7. Follow non-emergency protocol

### Illness

**Fever:**
- Check temperature with thermometer (not hand on forehead)
- Fever = 100.4°F or higher
- Contact parent
- Student may take own fever reducer if approved
- Isolate from other students if possible
- Monitor every 2-4 hours
- If fever over 101°F or persists more than 24 hours, see doctor
- Document

**Vomiting/Diarrhea:**
- Isolate student from others
- Provide water to sip (prevent dehydration)
- Contact parent
- Monitor for signs of dehydration (dry mouth, no urination, dizziness)
- If vomiting or diarrhea more than 6 hours, see doctor
- If blood in vomit or stool, see doctor immediately
- Student cannot return to activities until 24 hours symptom-free
- Document

**Cold/Flu symptoms:**
- Contact parent
- Student may rest in room
- Encourage fluids
- Student may take own cold medication if approved
- Isolate from others if possible
- If worsens or doesn't improve in 2-3 days, see doctor
- Document

### Mental Health Emergencies

**Suicidal thoughts or self-harm:**
1. **Take ALL statements seriously**
2. DO NOT leave student alone
3. Campus Director notified immediately
4. Campus Director calls Program Director immediately
5. Program Director coordinates with Mental Health Director (on retainer)
6. Parent called immediately
7. Student may need to go to emergency room for psychiatric evaluation
8. Follow HQ directives
9. Develop safety plan or arrange early departure
10. Detailed incident report

**Severe anxiety or panic attack:**
1. Move student to quiet space
2. Speak calmly and reassuringly
3. Encourage slow breathing
4. Stay with student
5. If student has anti-anxiety medication, they may take it
6. Contact Campus Director
7. Contact parent
8. If doesn't improve or worsens, consider medical evaluation
9. Follow-up support
10. Document

**Student reports past trauma or abuse:**
1. Listen without judgment
2. Thank them for sharing
3. Explain you must report (mandated reporter)
4. Contact Campus Director immediately
5. Campus Director contacts Program Director and Risk Management Director
6. Follow mandated reporting protocol
7. Provide support to student
8. Do not question student in detail (trained professional will do this)

## Supervision and Safety

### Supervision Ratios (MUST BE MAINTAINED AT ALL TIMES)

**High School Programs:**
- 10:1 (10 students per 1 mentor)

**Middle School Programs:**
- 8:1 (8 students per 1 mentor)

**WHY:** Adequate supervision ensures student safety, allows individualized attention, meets insurance requirements.

**ENFORCEMENT:**
- Campus Director responsible for compliance
- When staff take time off, remaining staff must still meet ratios
- Activities planned with ratios in mind
- If ratio cannot be met, activity must be modified or cancelled

### Never-Alone Policies

**Staff must NEVER be alone with a single student in private space.**

**Acceptable:**
- 1 staff + 3 or more students in room with door open
- 1 staff + 1 student in fully public space (lobby, outdoor area with others present, dining hall, etc.)
- 1 staff + multiple students in open office with window/open door

**NOT acceptable:**
- 1 staff + 1 student in closed room
- 1 staff + 1 student in car alone
- 1 staff + 1 student in private space

**If one-on-one meeting needed:**
- Meet in public space
- OR have door open and another staff member nearby
- OR have multiple students present

**WHY:** Protects both students and staff from allegations of misconduct.

**EXCEPTIONS:**
- Medical emergency (but get help as soon as possible)
- Student safety emergency (but inform Campus Director immediately)
- Transportation in emergency (but document and inform CD immediately)

### Student Grouping Requirements

**Students in residence halls during unstructured time:**
- Must be in groups of 3 or more if leaving their room
- May be in own room with door closed
- May be in others' rooms in groups of 3+ with door open
- After lights out, must be in own room

**Students during free time on campus:**
- Must be in groups of 3 or more if leaving residence hall
- Must stay within campus boundaries
- Must check in/out on REACH
- Must have staff supervision within reasonable proximity

**WHY:** Buddy system prevents students from being alone and vulnerable. Ensures witnesses if something happens. Easier to supervise.

### Excursion and Off-Campus Safety

**Before departure:**
- Complete head count
- Check REACH - all students checked out
- Assign staff to specific groups
- Verify transportation
- Review safety rules with students
- Ensure staff have first aid kit, student medical info, emergency contact numbers

**During excursion:**
- Frequent head counts (at least every 30 minutes)
- Staff spread throughout group (front, middle, back)
- Staff actively supervising (not on phones)
- Students stay in assigned groups
- Buddy system enforced
- Staff monitor student behavior

**At venues:**
- Review meeting time and location
- Ensure students know boundaries
- Verify all students understand rules
- Take attendance before leaving

**Return to campus:**
- Head count before loading transportation
- Head count upon arrival
- Check in all students on REACH
- Account for all students before dismissing

**If student missing during excursion:**
1. Immediately notify Campus Director
2. Check last known location
3. Send staff to search immediate area
4. Contact venue security
5. Call student's phone
6. If not found in 10 minutes, call police
7. Call parents
8. Follow missing student protocol

### Water Safety

**At beaches, pools, lakes:**
- Verify lifeguards on duty before allowing water activities
- Do not allow swimming without lifeguard
- Assign staff as water watchers (dedicated visual supervision)
- Water watchers do not leave post or use phones
- Frequent head counts (every 10 minutes in water)
- Buddy system strictly enforced
- Students must stay in designated swim area
- Follow all facility rules
- Have rescue equipment available
- Have first aid kit nearby

**If student in distress in water:**
1. Alert lifeguard immediately
2. Do not attempt rescue unless trained
3. Call 9-1-1 if needed
4. Once out of water, assess and provide first aid
5. Follow emergency protocols

### Transportation Safety

**In vans/buses:**
- Seatbelts required at all times
- Head count before departure
- Head count at each stop
- Head count upon return
- No eating/drinking in vehicles
- Keep aisles clear
- Follow all driver instructions
- Students stay seated while vehicle in motion

**In Uber/Lyft:**
- Verify driver and license plate before entering
- Staff always accompanies students
- Students in back seat
- Share trip details with Campus Director
- Text when arriving at destination

**Walking:**
- Staff at front and back of group
- Use crosswalks and obey signals
- Stay on sidewalks
- Students stay together
- Frequent visual checks of all students

### Weather and Environmental Safety

**Heat:**
- Encourage frequent water breaks
- Watch for signs of heat exhaustion (dizziness, nausea, excessive sweating, weakness)
- Modify outdoor activities in extreme heat
- Ensure students have water bottles
- Provide shaded rest areas

**Cold:**
- Ensure students dressed appropriately
- Watch for signs of hypothermia (shivering, confusion, slurred speech)
- Limit outdoor time in extreme cold
- Provide warm indoor breaks

**Severe weather (thunderstorms, tornadoes, etc.):**
- Monitor weather forecasts
- Follow campus emergency protocols
- Move students indoors if severe weather approaching
- Account for all students
- Stay indoors until all-clear

### Liability and Legal Protection

**Staff protected if:**
- Acting within scope of duties
- Following SSB policies and procedures
- Acting in good faith
- Not negligent or reckless

**Staff potentially liable if:**
- Exceeding scope of practice (playing doctor)
- Violating policies (giving student your medication)
- Negligent (ignoring obvious medical need)
- Abandoning duties (leaving students unsupervised)
- Falsifying records

**Protection strategy:**
- Follow all protocols
- Document everything
- Ask for help when unsure
- Escalate concerns promptly
- Never exceed your scope
