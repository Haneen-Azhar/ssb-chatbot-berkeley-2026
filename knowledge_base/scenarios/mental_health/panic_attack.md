# Panic Attack - Mental Health Crisis

**Tags:** panic attack, anxiety attack, hyperventilating, cant breathe, chest pain, panic, anxiety, mental health crisis, freaking out, heart racing, dizzy, cant catch breath, breathing fast, shaking, trembling, scared, terrified, losing control

**⚠️ NOTE:** This protocol is based on limited content from director_training_2026.txt (lines 4310-4335) combined with clinical best practices. Full SSB protocols may exist in Staff Manual or POAs.

## Recognition Triggers
- Student hyperventilating or breathing rapidly
- Student says "I can't breathe"
- Student says "I need to leave" urgently
- Chest pain or tightness
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Trembling, shaking, or sweating
- Feeling of losing control or "going crazy"
- Fear of dying
- Numbness or tingling in hands/feet
- Rapid heartbeat (student may clutch chest)

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)
**CRITICAL: Panic attacks can look like heart attacks. Call 9-1-1 if:**
- Student has no history of panic attacks AND has chest pain
- Student is over 40 years old with chest pain
- Chest pain radiates to arm, jaw, or back
- Student loses consciousness
- Lips or face turn blue
- Breathing does not improve after 10 minutes
- Student has known heart condition

**When in doubt: Call 9-1-1. Better safe than sorry.**

### URGENT (SSB Helpline + CD + Close Monitoring)
- First-time panic attack (no history)
- Panic attack lasting longer than 20 minutes
- Multiple panic attacks in one day
- Student cannot be calmed or grounded
- Student expresses suicidal thoughts during/after attack
- Panic attack triggered by traumatic memory

### CONCERNING (Monitor + Support + Document)
- Student has history of panic attacks (check POA)
- Student responds to grounding techniques
- Attack subsides within 10-15 minutes
- Student can identify trigger
- Student has coping strategies that work

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response (First 60 seconds)

**From training (lines 4327-4331):**
1. **Stay calm** - Your reaction sets the tone
2. **Support student privately** - Get them away from the group
3. **Maintain supervision coverage** - Have another staff member take your group
4. **Assess need for escalation** - Medical emergency vs panic attack
5. **Communicate clearly and calmly** - Use slow, steady voice

**Detailed actions:**
- Approach calmly: "Hey, I'm here with you. You're safe."
- Move to quiet, private space (not a closed room - doorway open)
- Have another staff member cover your responsibilities
- Sit or stand at student's eye level
- **Assess if medical emergency:**
  - "Have you had a panic attack before?"
  - "Do you have any heart problems?"
  - "Does this feel like your other panic attacks?" (if they have history)

### Step 2: Grounding & De-escalation (Minutes 1-10)

**Physical Grounding:**

1. **Breathing regulation:**
   - "We're going to breathe together. Watch me."
   - Demonstrate: Breathe in for 4, hold for 4, out for 6
   - "In through your nose, out through your mouth"
   - Don't say "calm down" or "just breathe" - MODEL it instead

2. **5-4-3-2-1 Grounding Technique:**
   - "Tell me 5 things you can see right now"
   - "Tell me 4 things you can touch"
   - "Tell me 3 things you can hear"
   - "Tell me 2 things you can smell"
   - "Tell me 1 thing you can taste"
   - (Brings them back to present moment)

3. **Physical grounding:**
   - "Feel your feet on the floor"
   - "Press your hands together"
   - "Hold this cold water bottle"
   - Avoid touching student unless they ask

**Verbal Reassurance:**
- "You're having a panic attack. It will pass."
- "You're not dying. This is anxiety."
- "I'm staying with you until you feel better."
- "You're safe here."

### Step 3: Assessment & Escalation Decision (Minutes 10-15)

**Check POA if student has one:**
- Does student have panic disorder diagnosis?
- What does their POA say about panic attacks?
- What are their preferred coping strategies?
- Do they have medication? (Students self-manage meds)

**Assess severity:**

Call 9-1-1 if:
- Symptoms not improving after 10 minutes
- Chest pain + no panic attack history
- Student loses consciousness

Call SSB Helpline (+1.858.779.0555) + CD if:
- First panic attack ever
- Panic triggered by trauma
- Student mentions self-harm
- Multiple attacks in one day

Document + Monitor if:
- Student has panic history
- Symptoms improving
- Student using coping skills successfully

### Step 4: Notification & Documentation

**While attack is happening:**
- Text CD: "With [student name], panic attack, monitoring, will update"
- Have another staff member notify CD if you can't leave student

**After attack subsides:**
- **Incident Report required:** alerts@summerspringboard.com
- Include:
  - Time attack started and ended
  - Triggers (if known)
  - Grounding techniques used
  - Whether 9-1-1 was called
  - Student's history of panic attacks (from POA)
  - Follow-up plan

**CD decides parent notification:**
- First-time panic attack = parents always notified
- Student with panic history = CD discretion
- If 9-1-1 called = parents notified immediately

### Step 5: Follow-Up (Post-Attack Care)

**Immediate (next 1-2 hours):**
- Student should rest, not return to group activity immediately
- Offer water, quiet space
- Ask: "What usually helps you after a panic attack?"
- Don't leave student alone for at least 30 minutes
- Check: "Do you feel ready to rejoin activities, or do you need more time?"

**First 24 hours:**
- Check in with student 2-3 times
- "How are you feeling today?"
- Watch for avoidance behavior
- Monitor for additional attacks
- Remind student they can come to you anytime

**Ongoing:**
- Daily brief check-ins
- Coordinate with mentor for closer monitoring
- If attacks continue, CD + parents + professional help needed
- Student may need to withdraw if panic attacks interfere with program safety

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### During the attack:
"I can see you're really struggling right now. I'm here with you."

"This is a panic attack. It feels terrible, but it will pass. You're safe."

"Let's breathe together. Watch my hand. Breathe in... and out..."

"You're not in danger. Your body is having a fear response, but you're safe."

"I'm going to stay right here with you."

### When they can talk:
"What do you need right now?"

"Has this happened to you before?"

"Do you know what triggered this?"

"What usually helps you when you feel this way?"

### After the attack:
"That was really hard. You did a great job getting through it."

"Do you want to talk about what happened, or do you need quiet time?"

"I'm going to check in with you a few times today. Is that okay?"

## What NOT to Say

❌ "Calm down" (doesn't work, feels invalidating)
❌ "Just breathe" (they're trying, it's not that simple)
❌ "You're fine" (they don't feel fine)
❌ "Stop freaking out" (they can't control it)
❌ "It's all in your head" (dismissive)
❌ "You're overreacting" (invalidating)
❌ "What's wrong with you?" (shameful)
❌ "Snap out of it" (not possible)

## What TO Say

✅ "I'm here with you"
✅ "You're safe"
✅ "This will pass"
✅ "Breathe with me"
✅ "You're doing great"
✅ "I've got you"
✅ "You're not alone"

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Assuming it's "just anxiety"** - Could be medical emergency
2. **Telling them to calm down** - Increases panic
3. **Leaving them alone** - Increases fear
4. **Bringing them back to group immediately** - Need recovery time
5. **Not checking POA** - Student may have specific protocol
6. **Crowding them** - Give physical space
7. **Asking "why are you panicking?"** - They don't know, makes it worse
8. **Not documenting** - Every panic attack needs incident report
9. **Touching without permission** - Can escalate panic
10. **Talking too much** - Keep it simple and calm

## Differentiating: Panic Attack vs. Heart Attack

**Call 9-1-1 if you can't tell the difference.**

| Panic Attack | Heart Attack |
|--------------|--------------|
| Sudden onset, peaks in 10 min | Gradual onset, worsens over time |
| Sharp, stabbing chest pain | Pressure, squeezing, "elephant on chest" |
| Pain stays in one spot | Pain radiates to arm, jaw, back |
| Improves with grounding | Does not improve |
| Age typically <40 | Risk increases with age |
| Previous panic history | Previous heart problems |
| Triggered by stress/anxiety | Can happen at rest |

**When in doubt: Call 9-1-1**

## Panic Attack vs. Severe Anxiety

| Panic Attack | Severe Anxiety |
|--------------|----------------|
| Sudden, intense peak | Builds gradually |
| Lasts 5-20 minutes | Can last hours/days |
| Physical symptoms dominant | Worry/dread dominant |
| Fear of dying/losing control | Specific worries |
| Unprovoked or specific trigger | Ongoing stressor |

Both need support. Panic attack more urgent.

## Follow-Up Procedures

### Day 1 (day of attack):
- Check in every 2 hours
- Student can rest/skip activities if needed
- No excursions that day
- Quiet evening, early bed check

### Days 2-3:
- Daily mentor check-ins
- Watch for avoidance behavior
- Monitor sleep and eating
- Document any changes in mood

### Ongoing:
- If attacks continue, professional help needed
- CD coordinates with parents
- Student may need to leave program for treatment
- Not safe for student to stay if attacks are frequent/severe

## Red Flags for Escalation

**Immediate CD + Helpline notification if:**
- Student has panic attack before major activity (avoiding/anticipatory anxiety)
- Multiple panic attacks per day
- Student starts avoiding all activities
- Panic triggered by specific trauma memory
- Student mentions self-harm or suicidal thoughts
- Student refuses to eat or leave room after attack
- Attack lasts longer than 30 minutes

## Legal and Ethical Requirements

- **Incident Report mandatory** for all panic attacks
- **Parents informed if:** First attack, multiple attacks, or if impacts program participation
- **CD makes parent call**, not mentor
- **Medical evaluation required if:** Any uncertainty about heart attack vs panic attack
- **Student's POA is binding** - follow their crisis plan if they have one
- **Never force student** to return to triggering situation immediately
