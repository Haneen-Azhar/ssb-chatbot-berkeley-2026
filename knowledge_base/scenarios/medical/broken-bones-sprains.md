# Broken Bones and Sprains - Medical Emergency

**Tags:** broken bone, fracture, sprain, twisted ankle, hurt wrist, swollen, cant move, pain, injury, fall, accident, medical emergency, ice, immobilize, x-ray

## Recognition Triggers

Bone fractures and sprains are common injuries from falls, sports, or accidents. Proper assessment and immobilization prevent further damage and complications.

### Bone Fracture (Break) Signs

**Definite Fracture:**
- Deformity (abnormal angle or position)
- Bone visible through skin (open fracture)
- Inability to move or bear weight
- Severe pain at specific point
- Grinding or crunching sensation (crepitus)
- Swelling and bruising (immediate or rapid onset)
- Numbness or tingling below injury
- Limb appears shorter or rotated

**Possible Fracture:**
- Significant pain at injury site
- Swelling
- Tenderness to touch at specific point
- Limited range of motion
- Pain with movement or weight-bearing
- Bruising (may develop later)
- Student reports hearing/feeling a "snap" or "pop"

### Sprain (Ligament Injury) Signs

**Mild Sprain (Grade 1):**
- Pain and tenderness
- Mild swelling
- Can bear weight with discomfort
- Minimal bruising
- Full or near-full range of motion

**Moderate Sprain (Grade 2):**
- Moderate pain
- Noticeable swelling
- Bruising
- Difficult to bear weight
- Limited range of motion
- Joint may feel unstable

**Severe Sprain (Grade 3):**
- Severe pain
- Significant swelling
- Extensive bruising
- Cannot bear weight
- Very limited or no range of motion
- Joint feels unstable or "gives way"
- May be indistinguishable from fracture

### Common Injury Locations

**Upper Body:**
- Collarbone (clavicle) - from fall on shoulder or outstretched arm
- Wrist - from fall on outstretched hand
- Fingers - from ball sports, falls
- Shoulder dislocation - from fall or sports

**Lower Body:**
- Ankle sprain - most common
- Ankle fracture
- Knee injuries - ACL, MCL sprains
- Toe fractures
- Hip fracture (rare in teens but possible)

**Other:**
- Ribs - from impact or fall
- Nose - from impact
- Cheekbone or jaw - from impact

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)

**Call 911 if:**
- Open fracture (bone visible through skin)
- Severe deformity
- Suspected spine, neck, or skull fracture
- Suspected hip or pelvis fracture
- Limb is cold, blue, or numb (circulation compromised)
- Injury to chest with breathing difficulty (rib fracture)
- Loss of consciousness with injury
- Multiple injuries from high-impact trauma
- Severe pain and signs of shock
- Cannot move fingers/toes below injury

### URGENT (Immobilize + Urgent Care/ER Same Day)

**Urgent medical eval needed if:**
- Suspected fracture (pain, swelling, deformity, inability to bear weight)
- Severe sprain (grade 3)
- Joint dislocation
- Inability to bear weight after 10-15 minutes
- Severe swelling
- Significant deformity
- Numbness or tingling
- Joint instability
- Heard/felt a pop or snap

### NON-URGENT (RICE + Monitor + Call Doctor If Not Improving)

**Treat with first aid if:**
- Mild sprain (grade 1)
- Can bear weight with mild discomfort
- No deformity
- Minimal swelling
- Full or near-full range of motion
- Pain improving with rest

**Call doctor if:**
- Not improving in 2-3 days
- Swelling increasing
- Pain worsening
- Cannot bear weight by day 2-3

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response (First 60 seconds)

**Seconds 1-20:**
- Assess scene safety
- Tell student not to move injured area
- Ask what happened
- Ask where it hurts
- Observe for deformity, swelling, bruising

**Seconds 21-40:**
- Tell student to stay still
- Do NOT straighten deformity
- Support injured area in position found
- Call for help (get CD, call 911 if needed)

**Seconds 41-60:**
- Assess circulation below injury (can wiggle fingers/toes? Color normal? Warm?)
- Check sensation (can feel touch below injury?)
- Remove jewelry from injured limb if possible (before swelling)
- Prepare for immobilization

### Step 2: Medical Intervention

**Immobilization:**

**Purpose:**
- Prevent further injury
- Reduce pain
- Prevent closed fracture from becoming open fracture
- Protect blood vessels and nerves

**General Principles:**
- Immobilize joints above and below injury
- Splint in position found (do NOT straighten)
- Pad splint for comfort
- Secure but not tight (check circulation)

**Upper Extremity Immobilization:**

**Arm or Forearm:**
- Cardboard or SAM splint
- Pad with cloth
- Secure with tape or ace wrap
- Place arm in sling
- Bind sling to chest with another wrap

**Wrist or Hand:**
- Place rolled gauze or cloth in palm
- Splint to forearm
- Elevate in sling

**Fingers:**
- Tape injured finger to adjacent finger (buddy taping)
- Ice and elevate

**Collarbone:**
- Arm sling on affected side
- Support arm to chest
- Transport to ER

**Lower Extremity Immobilization:**

**Leg or Thigh:**
- DO NOT MOVE
- Call 911
- Pad between legs
- Splint both legs together if needed
- Keep student lying down

**Ankle or Foot:**
- Remove shoe if can do gently (before swelling)
- Pillow splint: wrap pillow around ankle, tape
- OR use rigid splint on sides of ankle
- Elevate
- Do NOT allow weight-bearing

**Knee:**
- Immobilize in position found
- Pad behind knee if bent
- Long splints on sides from hip to ankle
- Call 911 or transport carefully

**Toes:**
- Buddy tape to adjacent toe
- Hard-soled shoe or walking boot if available
- Elevate

**RICE Method (For Sprains):**

**R - Rest:**
- Stop activity immediately
- Do NOT use injured area
- Crutches if lower extremity

**I - Ice:**
- Apply ice pack (or bag of frozen vegetables)
- Wrapped in cloth (not directly on skin)
- 20 minutes on, 20 minutes off
- Repeat every 1-2 hours for first 24-48 hours

**C - Compression:**
- Ace wrap bandage
- Snug but not tight
- Start below injury, wrap toward heart
- Check circulation (toes/fingers should stay pink, warm, able to move)

**E - Elevation:**
- Raise injured area above heart level
- Reduces swelling
- Use pillows for support

**Pain Management:**
- Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) - reduces pain and inflammation
- Acetaminophen (Tylenol) - pain only
- Check for allergies first
- Follow package dosing for age/weight
- Parents can authorize OTC medication

### Step 3: Emergency Services

**Who to Call:**

**CALL 911 if:**
- Open fracture
- Severe deformity
- Suspected spine/neck injury
- Hip or pelvis fracture
- Chest injury with breathing difficulty
- Limb cold, blue, or numb
- Loss of consciousness
- High-impact trauma

**TRANSPORT TO URGENT CARE/ER if:**
- Suspected fracture
- Severe sprain
- Joint dislocation
- Inability to bear weight
- Significant swelling or deformity

**What to Say to 911:**
"We need an ambulance at [location]. A [age]-year-old has injured their [body part]. There is [deformity/open wound/severe swelling]. The student [can/cannot] move [area]. [He/she] has [normal/no] sensation and circulation below the injury. Student [is/is not] conscious and alert. Injury occurred at [time] from [mechanism - fall, sports injury, etc.]."

**Information to Have Ready:**
- Mechanism of injury (how it happened)
- Exact location of injury
- Visible deformity or swelling
- Pain level (1-10 scale)
- Can student move area?
- Sensation and circulation below injury
- Allergies
- Any other injuries

### Step 4: Documentation

**Immediate Documentation:**
- Time of injury
- How injury occurred (detailed)
- Location on body
- Observations (deformity, swelling, bruising, position)
- Student's reported pain level
- Ability to move and bear weight
- First aid provided (RICE, immobilization)
- Who provided care
- Decision to transport

**Incident Report:**
- Required for all fractures
- Required for sprains needing medical evaluation
- Include mechanism of injury
- Document scene (broken equipment, slippery floor, etc.)
- All care provided
- Parent communication
- Medical facility and treatment

**Photo Documentation:**
- Photo of scene/hazard (broken step, wet floor, etc.)
- NOT of student's injury
- May photo immobilization technique if relevant for training

### Step 5: Follow-Up

**0-2 Hours (Medical Evaluation):**
- Staff accompanies to urgent care/ER
- X-ray performed
- Diagnosis: fracture vs sprain vs nothing
- Treatment:
  - Fracture: cast, splint, or boot; orthopedic follow-up
  - Sprain: brace, boot, wrap; rest and ice; possible crutches
- Discharge instructions in writing
- Activity restrictions
- Follow-up appointment coordination
- Pain medication prescription if needed

**2-24 Hours (Acute Care):**
- Pain management
- Ice and elevate
- Keep cast/splint dry
- Crutch training if needed
- Modified schedule (no stairs if foot injury, no writing if hand injury)
- Accommodations for mobility
- Monitor for complications

**24+ Hours (Recovery Phase):**
- Follow medical instructions
- Attend follow-up appointments
- Physical therapy if ordered
- Gradual return to activities per doctor
- Cast care (keep dry, don't stick objects inside)
- Watch for complications

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### Immediately After Injury:
"[Name], don't move. I can see you've hurt your [body part]. I need you to stay very still so we don't make it worse. I'm going to support your [arm/leg] and call for help. Can you feel me touching your fingers/toes? Can you wiggle them? Good."

### Immobilizing:
"I'm going to put a splint on your [body part] to keep it from moving. This will help with the pain. I'm going to move you as little as possible. Tell me if anything I do hurts more."

### To 911:
"This is an emergency at [location]. A 16-year-old fell and has an obvious deformity to her left forearm. The bone appears to be at an abnormal angle. She has feeling and can move her fingers. The injury happened 10 minutes ago when she fell off playground equipment. We have immobilized the arm. We need an ambulance."

### To Parent (Fracture):
"This is [name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] fell today and injured [his/her] [body part]. We've immobilized it and are taking [him/her] to [hospital/urgent care] for X-rays. [He/she] is in pain but doing okay. It's possible [he/she] broke [his/her] [bone]. Can you meet us there or would you like me to call you after we see the doctor?"

### To Parent (Sprain):
"This is [name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] sprained [his/her] ankle today during [activity]. We've iced and elevated it and [he/she]'s resting now. [He/she] can bear some weight on it. We're going to urgent care to make sure it's not fractured. I'll call you after we see the doctor."

### After Cast Applied:
"The doctor says you have a [type of fracture] in your [bone]. The cast will stay on for [X weeks]. Here's what you need to know: Keep it dry - we'll get you a cast cover for showering. Don't put anything inside the cast even if it itches. We'll get you excused from activities that could damage the cast. You'll see an orthopedist in [timeframe]. Any questions?"

## What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Try to straighten deformity (can damage nerves/vessels)
- Move student with suspected spine injury
- Remove shoe if foot already very swollen
- Apply ice directly to skin (wrap in cloth)
- Wrap compression bandage too tight
- Give aspirin (ibuprofen okay)
- Let student "walk it off" if suspected fracture
- Transport student with severe injury in personal vehicle (call 911)
- Assume it's "just a sprain" if cannot bear weight
- Ignore numbness or color changes below injury

**COMMON MISTAKES:**
- Not immobilizing joints above and below fracture
- Moving injured student unnecessarily
- Delaying medical care hoping it will improve
- Applying heat in first 48 hours (ice only)
- Wrapping ace bandage too tightly (cuts off circulation)
- Not removing jewelry before swelling
- Dismissing injury because student is "walking on it"
- Not checking circulation, sensation, movement below injury

## Medical Information Needed

**From Student:**
- How did injury happen?
- Did you hear or feel a snap/pop/crack?
- Where exactly does it hurt?
- Pain level 1-10?
- Can you move it?
- Can you feel this (touch below injury)?
- Any previous injuries to this area?

**From Medical Forms:**
- Allergies (pain medication)
- Previous fractures or bone conditions
- Medications that affect healing
- Bone conditions (osteoporosis unlikely in teens but possible)

## After-Action Procedures

### 0-2 Hours (Diagnosis & Treatment):

**At Medical Facility:**
- X-rays performed
- Diagnosis provided
- Treatment applied (cast, splint, brace, boot)
- Discharge instructions in writing
- Pain medication prescribed if needed
- Follow-up appointment scheduled or instructions provided
- Work note/school note for accommodations

**Return to Campus:**
- Review discharge instructions
- Set up accommodation needs
- Ensure pain medication available
- Ice and elevation supplies
- Crutches if provided
- Cast care supplies (plastic bag for showering)

### 2-24 Hours (Acute Recovery):

**Student Care:**
- Pain management (medications per doctor)
- RICE continues for sprains
- Keep cast/splint dry and clean
- Elevate when resting
- Ice for sprains (not on cast)
- Crutch use if needed (check safety)
- Room on first floor if stairs problematic

**Accommodations:**
- Elevator access if needed
- Extra time between classes
- Note-taker if hand/wrist injury
- Seated activities
- Skip physical activities per doctor
- Dining hall assistance if crutches
- Bathroom assistance if needed (privacy maintained)

**Medical Follow-Up:**
- Orthopedic appointment scheduled
- Transportation arranged
- Parent coordination
- Insurance verification

**Monitoring:**
- Circulation checks (especially first 24 hours)
- Fingers/toes pink, warm, able to move
- If cast feels too tight, seek medical attention
- Watch for increased pain (not normal after first day)
- Watch for bad smell from cast (infection)

### 24+ Hours (Recovery & Rehabilitation):

**Cast Care:**
- Keep absolutely dry
- Cast cover for showering
- No swimming
- Don't stick objects inside (even if itchy)
- Don't break off pieces
- Don't write on cast without asking (markers can irritate skin)

**Activity Participation:**
- Modified per medical restrictions
- Creative inclusion in activities (scorekeeping, photography, etc.)
- Don't exclude unnecessarily
- Balance safety with normal experience

**Follow-Up Appointments:**
- Orthopedic visit as scheduled
- X-rays to check healing
- Cast removal (typically 4-8 weeks depending on bone)
- Physical therapy if ordered
- Clearance for return to full activities

**Sprain Recovery:**
- Gradual increase in activity
- May need brace for sports
- Physical therapy exercises
- Strengthening and balance training
- Full recovery 2-8 weeks depending on severity

**Prevention Discussion:**
- How did injury happen?
- Preventable?
- Hazard that needs addressing?
- Safety equipment needed? (wrist guards for skating, etc.)
- Facility review if environmental cause

### Debrief Questions (With Student):

1. How are you feeling?
2. Is your pain controlled?
3. Can you move your fingers/toes below the cast?
4. Do you understand how to care for your cast/splint?
5. Do you have questions about activity restrictions?
6. Do you need any accommodations for classes or activities?
7. How did this injury happen?
8. What can we do to prevent this in the future?

### Debrief Questions (With Staff):

1. Was the injury preventable?
2. Is there a hazard that needs fixing?
3. Was immobilization done correctly?
4. Was escalation appropriate (911 vs transport)?
5. What worked well in the response?
6. What could improve?
7. Do we need additional first aid training?
8. Are accommodations in place for the student?

## Medical References

**Types of Fractures:**
- **Closed:** Bone broken but skin intact
- **Open (Compound):** Bone breaks through skin (emergency)
- **Greenstick:** Partial break (common in children/teens - bones flexible)
- **Hairline:** Tiny crack
- **Comminuted:** Bone shattered (high impact)
- **Displaced:** Bone ends not aligned
- **Non-displaced:** Bone broken but aligned

**Common Fractures in Teens:**
- Collarbone (clavicle) - very common
- Wrist (Colles' fracture from fall on outstretched hand)
- Fingers
- Ankle
- Toes
- Nose
- Ribs (from impact sports)

**Sprain Grades:**
- **Grade 1 (Mild):** Ligament stretched, few fibers torn. Minimal swelling, can bear weight. Recovery 1-2 weeks.
- **Grade 2 (Moderate):** Partial ligament tear. Moderate swelling and bruising. Difficult to bear weight. Recovery 3-6 weeks.
- **Grade 3 (Severe):** Complete ligament tear. Severe swelling, bruising, instability. Cannot bear weight. May need surgery. Recovery 6-12 weeks.

**Healing Times:**
- **Fingers:** 3-4 weeks
- **Wrist:** 6-8 weeks
- **Forearm:** 6-8 weeks
- **Collarbone:** 6-8 weeks (often no cast, just sling)
- **Ankle:** 6-8 weeks
- **Leg:** 8-12 weeks

**Complications to Watch For:**
- **Compartment Syndrome:** Swelling inside cast cuts off circulation. EMERGENCY. Signs: severe pain, numbness, can't move fingers/toes, pale or blue color.
- **Infection (open fracture):** Fever, increased pain, redness, bad smell from cast.
- **Malunion:** Bone heals in wrong position (prevented by proper immobilization and follow-up).
- **Chronic instability (severe sprain):** Joint remains unstable. May need surgery or prolonged PT.

**When to Seek Immediate Medical Attention After Cast:**
- Fingers/toes blue, cold, or very swollen
- Can't move fingers/toes
- Numbness or tingling
- Severe pain not relieved by medication
- Cast feels too tight
- Cast breaks or gets very wet
- Bad smell from cast
- Fever

- First aid supplies for immobilization
- Crutches if available (or coordinate rental)
- Urgent care partnerships
- Transportation coordination
- Accommodation support for students with injuries
- 24/7 SSB Helpline for medical guidance
- Incident Reporting system
