# Heat Illness - Medical Emergency

**Tags:** heat stroke, heat exhaustion, overheating, too hot, dizzy, nausea, throwing up, heat cramps, dehydrated, sweating, medical emergency, hot weather, sunstroke

## Recognition Triggers

Heat illness occurs when the body cannot cool itself effectively. Ranges from minor heat cramps to life-threatening heat stroke. Most common during summer programs with outdoor activities.

### Types of Heat Illness

**Heat Cramps (Mild):**
- Painful muscle cramps or spasms
- Usually in legs, arms, or abdomen
- Heavy sweating during exercise
- Skin may be cool and moist
- Student alert and oriented

**Heat Exhaustion (Moderate - URGENT):**
- Heavy sweating
- Weakness or fatigue
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Nausea or vomiting
- Headache
- Pale, cool, clammy skin
- Fast, weak pulse
- Fainting or feeling faint
- Muscle cramps
- Student may be confused

**Heat Stroke (Severe - EMERGENCY):**
- High body temperature (103°F or higher)
- Hot, red, dry, or damp skin
- May or may not be sweating
- Fast, strong pulse (or weak pulse if severe)
- Confusion, disorientation, or bizarre behavior
- Slurred speech
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizures
- Throbbing headache
- Nausea or vomiting

**CRITICAL DIFFERENCE:**
- Heat Exhaustion: Sweating, cool/clammy skin, student oriented
- Heat Stroke: Often dry skin (not always), very hot skin, altered mental status

### Risk Factors

**Environmental:**
- High temperature (> 90°F)
- High humidity (sweat doesn't evaporate)
- Direct sun exposure
- No breeze
- Hot indoor spaces (no AC)

**Personal:**
- Not acclimated to heat
- Dehydration
- Obesity
- Certain medications (diuretics, antihistamines, some psych meds)
- Previous heat illness
- Sickle cell trait (increased risk)
- Viral illness (fever)

**Activity:**
- Vigorous exercise in heat
- Not enough rest breaks
- Wearing heavy clothing
- Inadequate water intake

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)

**Call 911 for Heat Stroke:**
- Temperature 103°F or higher
- Confusion, disorientation, or bizarre behavior
- Loss of consciousness or unresponsive
- Seizure
- Slurred speech
- Hot, dry skin (though may still be sweating)
- Stopped sweating in high heat
- Rapid or weak pulse with altered mental status

### URGENT (Cool Immediately + Monitor + Call SSB Helpline)

**Heat Exhaustion Requires Immediate Cooling:**
- Heavy sweating with weakness
- Dizziness or fainting
- Nausea or vomiting
- Headache
- Cool, clammy skin
- Fast, weak pulse
- Muscle cramps
- Student oriented but feeling very ill

**If Not Improving in 30 Minutes → Call 911**

### NON-URGENT (Hydrate + Rest + Cool Down)

**Heat Cramps:**
- Muscle cramps during/after exercise in heat
- Student alert and feeling mostly okay
- Sweating normally
- Responds to hydration and rest

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response (First 60 seconds)

**Seconds 1-20:**
- Move student to shade or air-conditioned space immediately
- Have student stop all activity
- Sit or lie student down
- Assess mental status (alert? confused? unconscious?)

**Seconds 21-40:**
- Check skin (hot and dry? cool and clammy?)
- Check for sweating
- Ask: "What's your name? Where are you? How do you feel?"
- Call for help (get CD, call 911 if signs of heat stroke)

**Seconds 41-60:**
- Start cooling measures
- Offer water if student alert
- Remove excess clothing
- Assess need for 911

### Step 2: Medical Intervention

**Cooling Measures:**

**For Heat Cramps:**
1. Stop activity
2. Move to cool place
3. Drink water or sports drink
4. Gentle stretching of cramped muscle
5. Rest until cramps resolve
6. Do not return to activity in heat that day

**For Heat Exhaustion:**
1. Move to cool, air-conditioned space
2. Lie student down, elevate legs slightly
3. Remove excess clothing
4. Cool with wet towels, fan, spray bottle
5. Offer sips of cool water (if not nauseated)
6. Apply ice packs to armpits, groin, neck (if available)
7. Monitor closely
8. If vomiting, stop oral fluids → call 911
9. If not improving in 30 minutes → call 911
10. If condition worsens → call 911

**For Heat Stroke (EMERGENCY):**
1. CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY
2. Move to cool place
3. Remove excess clothing
4. Cool aggressively:
   - Immerse in cold water if possible (tub, cold shower)
   - OR cover with wet towels and fan continuously
   - OR spray with cool water while fanning
   - Apply ice packs to armpits, groin, neck
5. Continue cooling until help arrives or temperature < 102°F
6. Do NOT give fluids (risk of choking if confused)
7. Monitor consciousness and breathing
8. Position on side if vomiting

**Temperature Taking:**
- Ideal: Rectal thermometer (most accurate for core temp)
- Acceptable: Oral or forehead thermometer
- Underarm less accurate
- Heat stroke diagnosis based on symptoms + high temp, not temp alone

**Hydration:**

**If Alert and Not Nauseated:**
- Cool water or sports drink
- Small sips (don't chug)
- 8 oz every 15-20 minutes
- Avoid caffeinated drinks
- Avoid very cold drinks (can cause stomach cramps)

**Do NOT Give Fluids If:**
- Confused or disoriented
- Unconscious
- Vomiting
- Difficulty swallowing

**What NOT to Do:**
- Do NOT give salt tablets
- Do NOT use rubbing alcohol to cool skin
- Do NOT give aspirin or acetaminophen (doesn't help heat illness)
- Do NOT allow return to heat that day even if feeling better

### Step 3: Emergency Services

**Who to Call:**

**CALL 911 for Heat Stroke:**
- Altered mental status (confusion, disorientation, bizarre behavior)
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizure
- Temperature ≥ 103°F
- Hot, dry skin (heat stroke)
- Heat exhaustion not improving in 30 minutes
- Vomiting and cannot keep fluids down

**What to Say to 911:**
"We need an ambulance at [location]. We have a [age]-year-old with [heat stroke/severe heat exhaustion]. [He/she] is [confused/unconscious/seizing]. Skin is [hot and dry/cool and clammy]. Student was [activity] in [temperature]° heat. We are cooling [him/her] now with [methods]. [His/her] temperature is [number]°F. We need immediate help."

**Information to Have Ready:**
- What student was doing (exercise type, duration)
- Environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, sun)
- How long in heat
- Symptoms and when they started
- Cooling measures already taken
- Student's temperature if available
- Medications student takes (some increase heat illness risk)
- Fluids consumed today
- Any previous heat illness

### Step 4: Documentation

**Immediate Documentation:**
- Time symptoms noticed
- Activity student was doing
- Environmental conditions (temp, humidity, sun)
- Symptoms observed
- Student's mental status
- Temperature if taken
- Cooling measures used
- Fluids given
- Student's response to treatment
- Time 911 called if applicable

**Incident Report:**
- Required for heat exhaustion or heat stroke
- Include environmental conditions
- All care provided
- Medical facility and treatment
- Prevention measures to implement

**Weather Monitoring:**
- Document temperature and humidity
- Heat index (feels like temperature)
- Used to inform future activity decisions

### Step 5: Follow-Up

**0-2 Hours (Acute Recovery):**

**If 911 Called:**
- ER evaluation
- IV fluids if dehydrated
- Continued cooling
- Lab work if severe
- Discharge when stable
- Rest instructions
- Return to activity instructions

**If Managed on Campus:**
- Student rests in cool environment
- Continued hydration
- Monitor temperature
- Watch for symptom return
- No activity rest of day
- Light meal when hungry

**2-24 Hours (Recovery Phase):**
- Rest in cool environment
- Continued hydration
- Light meals
- Monitor for delayed symptoms
- No exercise for 24 hours minimum
- No heat exposure

**24+ Hours (Return to Activity):**
- Medical clearance if heat stroke
- Gradual return to activity
- Avoid heat for several days
- Increased hydration
- More breaks
- Close monitoring
- May need several days to fully recover heat tolerance

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### Heat Exhaustion:
"[Name], you're showing signs of heat exhaustion. I need you to sit down here in the shade. We're going to get you cooled down. I'm going to wet this towel and put it on you. Take small sips of this water. You're going to feel better soon. Just rest."

### Heat Stroke (to bystanders):
"We need help now. [Name] call 911 and tell them we have heat stroke. [Name] get me all the ice and cold water you can find. [Name] help me move [student] to that air-conditioned building. Everyone else please step back."

### To 911:
"This is an emergency at [location]. A 17-year-old has heat stroke. She's confused and can barely stand. Her skin is very hot and she's not sweating. She was running outside in 95-degree heat for 30 minutes. We've moved her inside and are cooling her with wet towels and fans. Her temperature is 104°F. She needs an ambulance immediately."

### To Parent (Heat Exhaustion):
"This is [name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] got overheated today during outdoor activities. [He/she] had heat exhaustion symptoms - heavy sweating, dizziness, and nausea. We immediately moved [him/her] to air conditioning, gave fluids, and [he/she] is recovering well. [He/she] is resting now and feeling much better. We're keeping [him/her] out of activities for the rest of the day. I wanted to let you know."

### To Parent (Heat Stroke):
"This is [name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] suffered heat stroke today. We called 911 and [he/she] is being transported to [hospital]. [He/she] was confused and had a very high temperature. [He/she] is conscious now. I'm going with [him/her]. Can you meet us at the hospital? I'll update you as soon as we have more information."

### Prevention Talk (Group):
"Today is going to be hot. We need everyone to stay hydrated. Drink water before you're thirsty. Take breaks in the shade. If you start feeling dizzy, nauseous, or get a headache, tell a staff member immediately. Don't try to push through it. Heat illness is serious."

## What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Ignore early symptoms (heat cramps can progress to heat exhaustion/stroke)
- Give salt tablets
- Use alcohol to cool skin (can be absorbed and cause poisoning)
- Give aspirin or Tylenol (doesn't treat heat illness)
- Immerse unconscious person in water (drowning risk)
- Force fluids if vomiting or altered mental status
- Allow return to heat same day even if feeling better
- Dismiss as "just dehydrated" if altered mental status (heat stroke!)

**COMMON MISTAKES:**
- Not recognizing heat stroke (confused? = heat stroke = 911)
- Waiting too long to call 911 (heat stroke needs immediate ER)
- Not cooling aggressively enough for heat stroke (immerse in cold water if possible)
- Letting student "tough it out"
- Not monitoring environmental conditions
- Inadequate hydration breaks
- Not acclimating to heat gradually

## Medical Information Needed

**From Student:**
- How are you feeling?
- Do you have a headache? Dizzy? Nauseous?
- When did you last drink water?
- How much have you had to drink today?
- Have you eaten today?
- Do you feel hot or cold?
- Any previous heat illness?

**From Medical Forms:**
- Medications (some increase risk: diuretics, antihistamines, some psych meds)
- Sickle cell trait (increased risk)
- Heart conditions
- Diabetes (affects hydration)
- Cystic fibrosis (excessive salt loss in sweat)

**Environmental:**
- Current temperature
- Humidity
- Heat index
- Duration in heat
- Activity level

## Who to Call and When

### Immediate Emergency Contacts:

**1. 911** - For heat stroke (altered mental status, temp ≥103°F, unconscious, seizure)

**2. Campus Director** - For any heat illness beyond minor cramps

**3. Another Staff Member** - To assist with cooling, get supplies, call for help

### Urgent Contacts:

**4. SSB Helpline** - For heat exhaustion, guidance on escalation

**5. Parents** - After student stable, inform of heat illness

**6. Campus Health** - For evaluation if available

### Follow-Up Contacts:

**7. Student's Doctor** - If heat stroke occurred (clearance for return to activity)

**8. Athletic Trainer** - For return-to-play guidance

## After-Action Procedures

### 0-2 Hours (Acute Treatment):

**Heat Cramps:**
- Rest in cool place
- Hydrate
- Stretch gently
- No activity rest of day
- Can resume normal schedule next day if feeling well

**Heat Exhaustion:**
- Cool environment for several hours
- Oral hydration if tolerating
- Monitor for progression to heat stroke
- Rest
- If not improving → ER
- Parents notified
- Out of activities rest of day

**Heat Stroke:**
- ER evaluation (mandatory)
- IV fluids
- Continued cooling
- Lab work
- Observation
- Discharge instructions
- Medical clearance required for return to activity

### 2-24 Hours (Recovery):

**General Care:**
- Continued rest
- Cool environment
- Hydration (water, sports drinks)
- Light, cool foods
- No exercise
- No heat exposure
- Monitor temperature
- Watch for symptom return

**Warning Signs to Return to ER:**
- Confusion returns
- Vomiting
- High fever
- Severe headache
- Decreased urination (dehydration)
- Dizziness when standing

### 24+ Hours (Prevention & Return to Activity):

**After Heat Cramps:**
- Can return to activity next day if feeling well
- Increase hydration
- Take more breaks
- Modify intensity

**After Heat Exhaustion:**
- No strenuous activity for 24-48 hours
- Gradual return to exercise
- Avoid heat for several days
- Increased hydration and breaks
- Medical clearance if symptoms were severe

**After Heat Stroke:**
- Medical clearance REQUIRED before any physical activity
- May need 1-2 weeks off from strenuous activity
- Gradual return under medical supervision
- Heat sensitivity may last weeks-months
- Close monitoring

**Heat Acclimatization Protocol (After Recovery):**
- Days 1-2: Light activity, 20-30 minutes, cool times of day
- Days 3-4: Moderate activity, 30-45 minutes
- Days 5-7: Increase to normal intensity
- Weeks 2-3: Full activity with extra precautions
- Takes 10-14 days to fully acclimatize to heat

### Prevention Measures:

**Before Activity:**
- Check weather (temperature, humidity, heat index)
- Modify plans if extreme heat (> 95°F or heat index > 100°F)
- Move activities to cooler times (early morning, evening)
- Indoor alternatives
- Pre-hydrate (water 2 hours before)

**During Activity:**
- Hydration breaks every 15-20 minutes
- Shade breaks
- Rest breaks
- Monitor students for symptoms
- Modify intensity in high heat
- Shorten activity duration
- Light-colored, loose clothing
- Hats and sunscreen

**After Activity:**
- Rehydrate
- Cool down in air conditioning
- Monitor for delayed symptoms
- Shower or cool water
- Light snacks with salt (pretzels, crackers)

**High-Risk Day Modifications:**
- Heat index > 100°F: No strenuous outdoor activity
- Heat index 90-100°F: Limited activity with frequent breaks
- Heat index < 90°F: Normal activity with hydration breaks

### Debrief Questions (With Student):

1. How are you feeling now?
2. What were you doing when you started feeling bad?
3. How much water did you drink today before this happened?
4. Did you eat breakfast/lunch?
5. Have you had heat illness before?
6. Do you understand how to prevent this next time?
7. Any questions about returning to activities?

### Debrief Questions (With Staff):

1. What were the environmental conditions?
2. Did we monitor heat index before activity?
3. Were hydration breaks adequate?
4. Did we recognize symptoms quickly?
5. Was cooling aggressive enough?
6. Should this activity have been modified or moved indoors?
7. What can we do differently for the next hot day?
8. Do we need additional heat illness training?

## Key Policy Reminders

**Heat Illness is Preventable:**
- Monitor weather
- Modify activities in extreme heat
- Hydration breaks mandatory
- Recognize early symptoms

**Heat Stroke is Life-Threatening:**
- Altered mental status = heat stroke = 911
- Cool immediately while waiting for ambulance
- Can cause death or permanent brain damage

**No Same-Day Return:**
- Even if feeling better, no return to activity in heat
- Need 24 hours minimum
- Heat stroke needs medical clearance

**Hydration is Key:**
- Encourage drinking before thirsty
- Water breaks every 15-20 minutes
- Sports drinks for prolonged activity (> 1 hour)
- Monitor urine color (pale yellow = good hydration)

**Documentation:**
- IR for heat exhaustion or heat stroke
- Weather conditions documented
- Prevention measures implemented after incident

## Medical References

**Heat Index:**
- Combines temperature and humidity
- "Feels like" temperature
- > 100°F = dangerous for strenuous activity
- > 105°F = very dangerous
- > 110°F = extremely dangerous

**Hydration Guidelines:**
- Teens: 8-10 cups water per day minimum
- Add 1-2 cups per hour of activity
- More in heat
- Sports drinks if activity > 1 hour (replaces electrolytes)
- Avoid sugary drinks and caffeine (dehydrating)

**Urine Color Chart:**
- Pale yellow = well hydrated
- Dark yellow = mild dehydration
- Amber or brown = severe dehydration

**Recovery Time:**
- Heat cramps: Hours to 1 day
- Heat exhaustion: 1-3 days
- Heat stroke: 1-2 weeks or longer
- Heat sensitivity may last weeks-months after heat stroke

**Sickle Cell Trait:**
- 1 in 12 Black Americans carry trait
- Increased risk of exertional sickling in heat
- Can be life-threatening
- Requires extra precautions in heat

**Medications That Increase Risk:**
- Diuretics (water pills)
- Antihistamines (Benadryl, etc.)
- Some antidepressants and antipsychotics
- Stimulants (ADHD meds)
- Beta blockers (heart meds)
- Check medical forms

**SSB Resources:**
- Weather monitoring
- Hydration supplies
- Cooling supplies (fans, ice, spray bottles)
- Indoor activity alternatives
- 24/7 SSB Helpline for guidance
- Incident reporting system
- Staff training on heat illness recognition

**Important Notes:**
- Heat stroke is medical emergency requiring 911
- Young athletes often don't recognize their own limits
- Acclimatization takes 10-14 days (beginning of session higher risk)
- Previous heat illness increases risk
- Hot, humid days are highest risk
- Prevention is key - modify activities, hydrate, monitor
