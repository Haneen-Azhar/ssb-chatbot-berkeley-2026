# Diabetes Emergency - Medical Emergency

**Tags:** diabetes, diabetic, blood sugar, low blood sugar, high blood sugar, hypoglycemia, hyperglycemia, insulin, glucose, shaky, sweating, confused, dizzy, unconscious, diabetic emergency, glucagon, medical emergency

## Recognition Triggers

Diabetes emergencies fall into two categories: hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) and hyperglycemia (high blood sugar). Students with diabetes are typically self-managing but may need emergency support.

### Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar) - More Common Emergency

**Symptoms:**
- Shakiness, trembling
- Sweating
- Confusion, difficulty concentrating
- Dizziness, lightheadedness
- Hunger
- Irritability, mood changes
- Pale skin
- Fast heart rate
- Weakness, fatigue
- Headache
- Blurred vision
- Slurred speech
- Loss of consciousness (severe)

### Hyperglycemia (High Blood Sugar) - Slower Onset

**Symptoms:**
- Excessive thirst
- Frequent urination
- Fatigue
- Nausea or vomiting
- Fruity-smelling breath
- Shortness of breath
- Confusion
- Abdominal pain
- Loss of consciousness (severe)

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)

**Call 911 if student:**
- Is unconscious or unresponsive
- Having a seizure
- Cannot swallow safely
- Not improving after treatment (15-20 minutes)
- Severe confusion or altered mental status
- Unable to check blood sugar themselves

### URGENT (Treat + Monitor + Call SSB Helpline)

**Hypoglycemia requiring immediate treatment:**
- Blood sugar below 70 mg/dL (or student's threshold in POA)
- Student aware but symptomatic
- Student can swallow safely
- Needs supervision for treatment

**Hyperglycemia requiring urgent attention:**
- Blood sugar above 250 mg/dL persistently
- Ketones present
- Vomiting or unable to keep fluids down
- Symptoms not improving with student's usual management

### NON-URGENT (Student Self-Management + Staff Monitoring)

**Mild fluctuations:**
- Blood sugar 70-250 mg/dL
- Student feels fine
- Student managing independently per POA
- Routine monitoring

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response (First 60 seconds)

**For Suspected Hypoglycemia:**

**Seconds 1-20:**
- Ask student how they feel
- Ask if they've checked blood sugar recently
- Have student check blood sugar if able
- Stay calm and reassuring

**Seconds 21-40:**
- If blood sugar low or student symptomatic, give fast-acting sugar immediately
- Keep student seated or lying down
- Do not leave student alone

**Seconds 41-60:**
- Alert another staff member
- Get student's diabetes supplies if needed
- Prepare to call 911 if student deteriorates

**For Suspected Hyperglycemia:**

**Seconds 1-20:**
- Have student check blood sugar
- Ask about ketones if applicable
- Ask when student last took insulin
- Check if student feeling nauseous

**Seconds 21-60:**
- Contact CD immediately
- Review student's POA for specific instructions
- Prepare for possible urgent care visit

### Step 2: Medical Intervention

**Treating Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar):**

**The 15-15 Rule:**
1. Give 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates:
   - 4 glucose tablets
   - 4 ounces (1/2 cup) of juice
   - 4 ounces of regular soda (not diet)
   - 1 tablespoon of sugar or honey
   - Hard candy (check POA for student's preference)

2. Wait 15 minutes

3. Recheck blood sugar

4. If still below 70 mg/dL (or student's threshold), repeat

5. Once blood sugar normal, give complex carb snack (crackers, granola bar)

**If Student Unconscious (Hypoglycemia):**
- DO NOT give food or drink
- Call 911 immediately
- Position student on side (recovery position)
- Use glucagon if available and staff trained (check POA)
- Stay with student until help arrives

**Treating Hyperglycemia (High Blood Sugar):**
1. Student follows their insulin regimen per POA
2. Encourage water intake
3. Check for ketones if student has supplies
4. Monitor for worsening symptoms
5. Contact parents for guidance
6. Consider urgent care if not improving

**Glucagon Administration (if applicable):**
- Only if prescribed in student's POA
- Only if staff trained in administration
- For severe hypoglycemia with unconsciousness
- Call 911 before or immediately after administering
- Follow package instructions exactly
- Student will need medical evaluation even if they recover

### Step 3: Emergency Services

**Who to Call:**

**For Severe Hypoglycemia:**
1. **911** - If unconscious, seizing, or not improving
2. **SSB Helpline** - (888) 682-6671
3. **Campus Director** - Via batphone
4. **Student's Emergency Contact** - After medical intervention started
5. **Student's Doctor** - If number in POA for guidance

**For Hyperglycemia:**
1. **SSB Helpline** - For guidance on escalation
2. **Campus Director** - For coordination
3. **Student's Emergency Contact** - For parent guidance
4. **Student's Doctor** - May be able to advise remotely
5. **911 or Urgent Care** - If symptoms severe or worsening

**What to Say to 911:**
"We have a [age]-year-old with Type 1/Type 2 Diabetes experiencing [hypoglycemia/hyperglycemia]. Student is [conscious/unconscious]. Current blood sugar is [number] mg/dL. We have given [treatment]. Student [is/is not] improving. We are at [location]. Student uses [insulin pump/injections/medication name]."

**Information to Have Ready:**
- Student's full name and age
- Type of diabetes (Type 1 or Type 2)
- Current blood sugar reading
- Last time insulin taken
- Last meal/snack consumed
- Medications and doses (from POA)
- Student's typical blood sugar range
- Recent illness or unusual activity
- Insulin pump or CGM model if applicable

### Step 4: Documentation

**Immediate Documentation (0-30 minutes):**
- Time of symptoms observed
- Blood sugar reading(s)
- Treatment given (what, how much, when)
- Student's response to treatment
- Time of any calls (911, parents, SSB)
- Who was present
- Any unusual circumstances (missed meal, extra exercise, illness)

**Incident Report Requirements:**
- Required for ANY diabetes emergency requiring intervention
- Include all blood sugar readings
- Document parent communication
- Note any deviation from POA
- Submit to SSB HQ same day

**Ongoing Monitoring Log:**
- Blood sugar checks for next 24 hours
- Meals and snacks consumed
- Insulin doses taken
- Activity level
- How student feeling

### Step 5: Follow-Up

**0-2 Hours (Acute Phase):**
- Continue monitoring blood sugar every 15-30 minutes
- Ensure student eats regular meal if due
- Student stays with staff (no independent activity)
- Document all readings
- Update parents on student's status
- Medical clearance if 911 called

**2-24 Hours (Recovery Phase):**
- Blood sugar checks every 2-4 hours (or per POA)
- Extra snacks available
- Modified activity if recommended
- Mentor check-ins more frequent
- Communication with parents
- Review what triggered emergency

**24+ Hours (Prevention Phase):**
- Health Check with student and mentor
- Review POA together
- Discuss any changes to routine needed
- Ensure adequate supplies (glucose tablets, test strips, etc.)
- Coordination with dining services if meal-related
- Staff team briefed on enhanced monitoring

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### To Student During Hypoglycemia:
"[Name], your blood sugar is low. I need you to drink this juice right now. Sit down here with me. You're going to feel better in about 15 minutes. Just stay with me and sip this slowly."

### To Student During Hyperglycemia:
"[Name], your blood sugar is high. Have you taken your insulin? Let's check your POA together. I'm going to call your parents to let them know and get guidance. Do you have your supplies?"

### To 911 Operator (Unconscious Student):
"This is an emergency. I'm at [location]. We have a [age]-year-old with Type 1 Diabetes who is unconscious. Their blood sugar was [last known reading] at [time]. We have [given glucagon/positioned for safety]. Student uses an insulin pump. We need an ambulance immediately."

### To Parent (Non-Emergency):
"This is [your name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] had low blood sugar this morning. We followed their POA and gave [treatment]. Their blood sugar is now [number] and they're feeling much better. They're staying with staff today for extra monitoring. I wanted to let you know and see if you have any concerns or additional guidance."

### To Parent (Emergency):
"This is [your name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] is having a diabetes emergency. Their blood sugar is [number]. We have called 911 and they are being transported to [hospital]. [Student name] is [conscious/unconscious]. I am going with them to the hospital. Can you meet us there? I'll call you with updates."

### To Student After Incident:
"I'm glad you're feeling better. Let's talk about what happened so we can prevent this next time. Did you notice anything different about today? Missed a snack? More activity than usual? What can I do to support you better?"

## What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Give insulin (students self-manage insulin per POA)
- Give diet soda or sugar-free products for low blood sugar
- Force food or drink if student cannot swallow
- Inject glucagon unless trained and it's in POA
- Leave student alone during or after emergency
- Restrict student from program without medical cause
- Assume student will "tell you if something's wrong"
- Skip meals or snacks for diabetic students
- Ignore student's own assessment of their condition
- Give large amounts of sugar for low blood sugar (follow 15-15 rule)
- Panic or make student feel like a burden

**COMMON MISTAKES:**
- Not recognizing early symptoms of low blood sugar
- Confusing hypoglycemia confusion with behavioral issue
- Giving too much sugar and causing rebound high blood sugar
- Not having fast-acting glucose readily available
- Forgetting to give complex carb after treating low
- Not checking blood sugar when student "doesn't feel right"
- Failing to coordinate with dining services for meal timing
- Not ensuring refrigeration for insulin
- Allowing student to sleep through alarm for low blood sugar

## After-Action Procedures

### 0-2 Hours (Acute Response Phase):

**For Hypoglycemia:**
- Blood sugar normalized and stable
- Student eaten regular meal or substantial snack
- Monitoring every 15-30 minutes
- Student with staff at all times
- Parents updated
- IR submitted
- CD briefed

**For Hyperglycemia:**
- Medical consultation obtained (911, urgent care, or phone)
- Ketones checked if applicable
- Hydration ensured
- Insulin adjustment per doctor/parent guidance
- Monitoring every 30-60 minutes
- Plan for next 24 hours established

**If 911 Called:**
- Student transported to ER
- Staff accompanies student
- Bring student's medical info, supplies, insurance card
- Continuous parent communication
- CD coordinates with hospital
- Medical clearance before returning

### 2-24 Hours (Stabilization Phase):

**Student Care:**
- Modified schedule if recommended
- Extra snacks available
- More frequent blood sugar checks
- Buddy system for activities
- No independent activities until fully stable
- Emotional support and reassurance

**Equipment/Supplies:**
- Verify all equipment functioning
- Restock glucose tablets or fast-acting sugar
- Check insulin supply and refrigeration
- Ensure charging equipment working
- Backup supplies identified

**Staff Response:**
- Mentor debriefs with student
- What led to emergency?
- What can we do differently?
- Any POA adjustments needed?
- Team briefed on enhanced monitoring

**Administrative:**
- Complete Incident Report
- Document all communications
- Update student's file with notes
- Brief next shift on enhanced monitoring
- SSB HQ follow-up

### 24+ Hours (Prevention & Optimization Phase):

**With Student:**
- Comprehensive health check
- Review daily routine
- Meal timing and content
- Activity level changes
- Sleep quality
- Stress or illness factors
- Technology troubleshooting
- Confidence check - does student feel supported?

**With Staff:**
- Team debrief on response
- What worked well?
- What would we do differently?
- Any training gaps identified?
- Recognition for staff who responded well

**Program Adjustments:**
- Dining hall coordination for meal timing
- Snack access enhanced
- Activity modifications if needed
- Room check protocol (ensure alarms can wake student)
- Backup communication plan if student needs help overnight
- POA updates implemented

**Pattern Analysis:**
- If multiple emergencies, look for patterns
- Time of day
- Activity level
- Meal timing
- Sleep patterns
- Stress factors
- Consult with parents and student's doctor
- Adjust POA as needed

### Debrief Questions (With Student):

1. How are you feeling now?
2. What do you think led to this episode?
3. Did you notice warning signs?
4. What would help you prevent this next time?
5. Is there anything about your POA we should adjust?
6. What support do you need from the team?
7. Do you feel comfortable managing on program or do we need to make changes?

### Debrief Questions (With Staff):

1. Who first noticed something was wrong?
2. How quickly did we respond?
3. Did we have supplies readily available?
4. Was the POA clear and helpful?
5. What worked well in our response?
6. What would we do differently?
7. Do we need additional training on anything?
8. How can we better support this student going forward?

## Key Policy Reminders

**Student Self-Management:**
- Students with diabetes manage their own condition
- Staff provide support, monitoring, and emergency response
- Do not administer insulin (students do this themselves)
- Students may need to eat/drink in class - always permitted
- Students may need to check blood sugar anytime - always permitted

**Required Support:**
- Refrigeration for insulin (mini fridge if campus housing doesn't have)
- Dedicated outlet for device charging (near bed)
- 24/7 access to snacks
- Flexibility with meal timing
- Permission to miss activities if managing blood sugar
- Privacy for insulin administration if student prefers

**Overnight Protocol:**
- Student's alarm may go off for low blood sugar at night
- Student typically handles this independently (juice/crackers in room)
- Staff should be aware of alarm but not intervene unless student requests
- Room checks should not prevent student from hearing alarm
- Some students use CGM that alerts on phone/watch

**Equipment Issues:**
- Pump or CGM malfunction = contact parents immediately
- Student may need to switch to backup regimen
- Urgent care may be needed for pump issues
- Have contact info for pump/CGM technical support (in POA)

**Documentation Requirements:**
- IR for any diabetes emergency requiring staff intervention
- Document all blood sugar readings during incident
- Note any deviation from normal routine
- Parent communication documented
- Follow-up plan noted

**Medical Definitions:**
- **Hypoglycemia:** Low blood sugar, typically <70 mg/dL
- **Hyperglycemia:** High blood sugar, typically >180 mg/dL
- **Glucagon:** Emergency hormone injection for severe hypoglycemia
- **Insulin pump:** Device that delivers insulin continuously
- **CGM:** Continuous glucose monitor - tracks blood sugar in real-time
- **Ketones:** Dangerous byproduct when body can't use glucose (Type 1)
