# Anaphylaxis - Medical Emergency

**Tags:** allergic reaction, allergy, severe allergy, epipen, epinephrine, food allergy, bee sting, medication allergy, anaphylactic shock, hives, swelling, breathing emergency

## Recognition Triggers

Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction that typically occurs within minutes of exposure to an allergen (food, insect stings, medications).

**KEY RULE: Skin + Something Else = Anaphylaxis**

### Body Systems Affected

**Skin:**
- Hives
- Flushed or pale skin

**Respiratory System:**
- Wheezing, coughing
- Difficulty breathing

**Cardiovascular System:**
- Fast heart rate
- Low blood pressure
- Chest pain

**Gastrointestinal:**
- Abdominal pain
- Nausea or vomiting

**Neurological:**
- Confusion
- Dizziness or fainting

**Swelling:**
- Lips, tongue, or throat
- Eyes

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)

**ANY combination of skin reaction + another body system = ANAPHYLAXIS**

Call 911 immediately if you observe:
- Difficulty breathing or wheezing
- Swelling of lips, tongue, or throat
- Fast heart rate with low blood pressure
- Confusion, dizziness, or fainting
- Multiple body systems affected

### URGENT (Administer EpiPen + Call 911)

All anaphylaxis cases require:
1. Immediate epinephrine administration
2. Call to emergency services
3. Notification to SSB Helpline

### NON-URGENT

There is NO non-urgent anaphylaxis. If anaphylaxis is suspected, treat as emergency.

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response (First 60 seconds)

**Seconds 1-20:**
- Identify anaphylaxis symptoms (skin + other system)
- Ask student if they have their EpiPen
- Locate the EpiPen immediately

**Seconds 21-40:**
- Ask student to self-administer EpiPen if able
- If student cannot self-administer, staff will administer
- Student carries one EpiPen; their mentor carries the backup

**Seconds 41-60:**
- Have someone call 911 while you stay with student
- Keep student calm
- Position student lying down (unless breathing difficulty, then seated upright)

### Step 2: Medical Intervention

**EpiPen Administration:**
1. Remove EpiPen from carrier tube
2. Hold EpiPen with orange tip pointing downward
3. Remove blue safety cap by pulling straight up
4. Place orange tip against outer thigh (can go through clothing)
5. Push down hard until click is heard
6. Hold firmly in place for 3 seconds
7. Remove and massage injection area for 10 seconds

**Student Self-Administration Protocol:**
- Students prescribed epinephrine ALWAYS asked to self-administer first
- If unable, staff administers
- NEVER give personal medication from staff to student
- Only use student's prescribed EpiPen

**EpiPen Locations:**
- Student carries one at all times
- Mentor carries backup
- When mentor is "off," backup given to another staff member
- Students must inform staff where EpiPen is kept

### Step 3: Emergency Services

**Who to Call:**
1. **911** - Immediately after EpiPen administration
2. **SSB Helpline (24/7)** - (888) 682-6671 (confirm actual SSB helpline number)
3. **Campus Director/Program Director** - Via batphone or direct contact
4. **Student's Emergency Contact** - After medical intervention underway

**What to Say to 911:**
"We have a [age]-year-old experiencing anaphylaxis. EpiPen has been administered at [time]. Student is [conscious/breathing status]. We are at [exact location]. Allergen was [if known]. Student has history of [allergy type]."

**Information to Have Ready:**
- Student's full name and age
- Known allergies (check Plan of Action)
- Time of exposure
- Time of EpiPen administration
- Current symptoms
- Student's medical history (from POA)

### Step 4: Documentation

**Immediate Documentation (0-30 minutes):**
- Time of symptom onset
- Time of EpiPen administration
- Who administered (student or staff name)
- Which EpiPen used (student's primary or backup)
- Time 911 was called
- Symptoms observed
- Student's response to epinephrine

**Incident Report Requirements:**
- CD or designated staff fills out Incident Report
- Submit to SSB HQ immediately
- Include all timeline details
- Note all staff involved
- Document parent communication

**Photos/Evidence:**
- Food labels if food allergen
- Dining hall signage
- Any relevant environmental factors
- DO NOT photograph student during medical emergency

### Step 5: Follow-Up

**0-2 Hours (Emergency Phase):**
- Student transported to emergency room by ambulance
- Staff member accompanies student to hospital
- Continuous communication with SSB Helpline
- Parent/guardian notified and updated
- CD coordinates with hospital

**2-24 Hours (Recovery Phase):**
- Medical clearance required before returning to program
- Doctor's note specifying any activity restrictions
- Replacement EpiPen obtained if used
- Review of student's Plan of Action with student
- Check-in with student about emotional state
- Mentor debriefs with student about prevention

**24+ Hours (Prevention Phase):**
- Health Check with student and mentor
- Review allergen avoidance strategies
- Confirm EpiPen locations and accessibility
- Update Plan of Action if needed
- Staff team briefed on student's specific triggers
- Enhanced monitoring at meals

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### To Student During Emergency:
"[Name], I can see you're having an allergic reaction. Where is your EpiPen? Can you give it to yourself, or should I help you? We're calling 911 right now. You're going to be okay."

### To 911 Operator:
"This is an emergency. I'm calling from [location]. We have a [age]-year-old student experiencing anaphylaxis. Symptoms include [list observed symptoms]. We have administered epinephrine at [time]. Student is [conscious/breathing]. We need an ambulance immediately."

### To Parent/Guardian (After EpiPen Given):
"This is [your name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] had an allergic reaction to [allergen if known]. We administered their EpiPen at [time] and called 911. They are conscious and breathing. An ambulance is on the way to [hospital name]. I am staying with [student name] and will update you from the hospital. Can I confirm your contact number?"

### To SSB Helpline:
"This is [CD name] from [campus]. We have a medical emergency. Student [name] experienced anaphylaxis at [time]. EpiPen administered at [time]. 911 called at [time]. Student en route to [hospital]. Parent contacted at [time]. I need support with [specific need]."

### To Student After Incident (Next Day):
"How are you feeling today? I want to check in about what happened. Let's review your Plan of Action together to make sure we're all on the same page about keeping you safe. What support do you need from me and the team?"

## What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Delay EpiPen administration while "waiting to see if it gets worse"
- Give student antihistamines instead of epinephrine
- Use a staff member's personal EpiPen on a student (policy violation)
- Let student "walk it off" or go to their room alone
- Allow student to return to activity without 911 call
- Skip calling 911 even if student feels better after EpiPen
- Contact parents before administering life-saving medication
- Give student food or drink if having difficulty swallowing
- Leave student alone at any point
- Assume one dose of epinephrine is sufficient (medical evaluation required)

**COMMON MISTAKES:**
- Staff carrying student's EpiPen in office instead of with mentor
- Student forgetting EpiPen at dining hall
- Not reading food labels/dining hall signage carefully
- Assuming "mild" allergy won't become anaphylaxis
- Hesitating to administer EpiPen due to fear of doing it wrong

## Medical Information Needed

**Before Program (Check-In Day):**
- Student's Allergy Waiver + Plan of Action (POA) on file
- Review student's POA in Student Master List
- Confirm allergen specifics and severity
- Verify EpiPen prescription and expiration dates
- Identify if student has had prior anaphylaxis episodes

**Plan of Action Should Include:**
- Specific allergens
- Severity level (mild vs severe/anaphylactic)
- Symptoms student typically experiences
- Student's self-management ability
- Any additional medications (antihistamines, etc.)
- Emergency contact preferences
- Doctor contact information

**Health Check Requirements:**
- Mentor conducts Health Check on check-in day
- Discuss where student will keep EpiPen
- Confirm student knows how to self-administer
- Review dining hall procedures for allergen safety
- Establish communication plan if student feels symptoms

**Required Staff Training:**
- All staff complete Epinephrine training in Online Modules
- Review SSB Anaphylactic Policy in Staff Manual
- Familiarize with each allergic student's POA before program
- Practice EpiPen administration technique

## Who to Call and When

### Immediate Emergency Contacts (During Incident):

**1. 911** - Immediately after EpiPen given
- Never wait to call
- Call even if student improving
- Required for all anaphylaxis cases

**2. SSB Helpline (24/7)** - (888) 682-6671
- Call while waiting for ambulance
- Provides support and documentation guidance
- Escalates to executive team if needed

**3. Campus Director/Program Director**
- Via batphone (Google Voice number)
- Immediate notification
- CD may already be on scene

**4. Student's Emergency Contact**
- After medical intervention underway

### Secondary Contacts (Within 2 Hours):

**5. Conference Representative** (if applicable)
- CD notifies about campus incident

**6. Campus Police/Security**

**7. Medical Director** (via SSB HQ)
- For complex medical questions

**8. Student's Personal Physician**

## After-Action Procedures

### 0-2 Hours (Critical Response Phase):

**Immediate Actions:**
- Student transported to ER
- Staff accompanies student
- Bring student's medical information
- Bring student's insurance card
- Continuous parent communication
- IR submitted to SSB HQ
- CD briefs staff team

**Hospital Protocol:**
- Staff stays until parent arrives or medical clearance
- Obtain written discharge instructions
- Get doctor's note for return to program
- Coordinate transportation back to campus
- Document all medical advice

### 2-24 Hours (Stabilization Phase):

**Student Care:**
- Medical clearance before program return
- Replace used EpiPen immediately
- One-on-one check-in with CD
- Emotional support from mentor
- Peer support if student wants to share

**Staff Response:**
- Team debrief (separate from student)
- Identify what was front-loaded well
- Identify what was missing
- Process staff emotions
- Reinforce protocols

**Administrative:**
- Complete full Incident Report
- Document parent communications
- Update student's POA if needed
- Brief incoming shift staff
- Coordinate with SSB HQ on next steps

### 24+ Hours (Prevention & Learning Phase):

**With Student:**
- Review what happened (student perspective)
- What did student notice in themselves?
- What support do they need now?
- What do we want to remember for next time?
- Empowerment conversation (not blame)

**With Staff:**
- Separate facts, interpretations, and recommendations
- Did response match SSB's duty of care?
- What worked well?
- What would we do differently?
- Any needed follow-up with leadership or campus

**Program Adjustments:**
- Enhanced allergen awareness at all meals
- Dining hall staff re-briefed
- Student's peers educated (with student's permission)
- EpiPen accessibility confirmed daily
- Mentor check-ins more frequent

**Documentation Review:**
- Case file review with PD
- Any policy clarifications needed
- Staff training gaps identified
- Recognition for staff who responded well

### Debrief Questions (With Participants):

1. What happened?
2. What did you notice in yourself?
3. What support do you need now?
4. What do we want to remember for the next time?

### Debrief Questions (With Staff):

1. Separate facts, interpretations, and recommendations
2. Identify what was front-loaded well and what was missing
3. Ask whether the response matched SSB's duty of care and risk tolerance
4. Note any needed follow-up with leadership or host campus
