# Minor Medical Emergencies - Quick Reference Guide

**Tags:** minor injury, nosebleed, headache, stomachache, vomiting, throwing up, sick, first aid, fever, cold, flu, rash, bug bite, blister

## Overview

This playbook covers common minor medical situations that don't typically require emergency services but need proper first aid and monitoring. Know when to escalate.

---

## Nosebleeds

### Recognition
- Blood from one or both nostrils
- May have recent trauma, dry air, nose picking, or no clear cause

### Severity Assessment

**EMERGENCY (Call 911):**
- Bleeding won't stop after 30 minutes of pressure
- Large amount of blood lost
- Difficulty breathing
- Bleeding after head injury with other symptoms (confusion, vomiting)
- Blood thinners (check medical forms)
- Bleeding disorder (hemophilia)

**URGENT (Medical evaluation needed):**
- Frequent nosebleeds (several per week)
- Bleeding after 20-30 minutes
- Heavy bleeding
- Nosebleeds started after new medication

**NON-URGENT (First aid):**
- Occasional nosebleed
- Stops with pressure in < 20 minutes
- No other symptoms

### Treatment Protocol

**Step-by-Step:**
1. Have student sit upright, lean slightly forward (NOT back)
2. Pinch soft part of nose (just below bridge) firmly
3. Hold continuously for 10 minutes (don't peek!)
4. Breathe through mouth
5. Apply ice pack to bridge of nose
6. After bleeding stops, don't blow nose for several hours

**What NOT to Do:**
- Don't tilt head back (blood goes down throat → nausea/vomiting)
- Don't pack nose with tissue (can restart bleeding when removed)
- Don't have student lie down

**Prevention:**
- Humidifier in dry environments
- Saline nose spray
- Don't pick nose
- Trim fingernails

**Call SSB Helpline if:**
- Bleeding > 20 minutes
- Frequent recurrence
- Parents should be notified

## Stomach Issues (Nausea, Vomiting, Diarrhea)

### Recognition
- Nausea (feeling like going to vomit)
- Vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Stomach pain
- May have food poisoning, stomach virus, or other cause

### Severity Assessment

**EMERGENCY (Call 911):**
- Vomiting blood or "coffee ground" material
- Severe abdominal pain (can't move, severe cramping)
- Signs of severe dehydration (very dry mouth, no tears, no urination in 8+ hours, dizzy when standing)
- Rigid, board-like abdomen
- Vomiting after head injury
- Suspected appendicitis (right lower abdomen pain, fever, vomiting)

**URGENT (Same-day medical evaluation):**
- Vomiting > 12 hours
- Diarrhea > 24 hours
- Blood in stool (red or black)
- Signs of dehydration (dry mouth, dark urine, dizzy)
- Fever > 101°F with vomiting/diarrhea
- Unable to keep down any fluids
- Severe cramping
- Recent travel (possible parasites)

**NON-URGENT (Rest and monitor):**
- Mild nausea
- Vomiting 1-2 times, now feeling better
- Mild diarrhea
- Staying hydrated
- No fever or low-grade only
- Improving over time

### Treatment Protocol

**For Vomiting:**
1. Rest, no food for 1-2 hours
2. After 1 hour without vomiting: small sips of clear liquids
3. Start with 1 tablespoon every 15 minutes
4. Gradually increase if tolerating
5. After 4-6 hours: try bland foods (crackers, toast, rice, banana)
6. Avoid dairy, fatty foods, spicy foods for 24 hours

**For Diarrhea:**
1. Continue fluids (very important!)
2. Water, sports drinks, clear broth
3. BRAT diet: Bananas, Rice, Applesauce, Toast
4. Avoid dairy, fatty foods, caffeine, sugar
5. Replace electrolytes (sports drinks, Pedialyte)

**For Both:**
- Rest
- Easy access to bathroom
- Privacy
- Bland diet when ready
- Hand washing critical (prevent spread)

**Hydration Signs:**
- Good: Pale yellow urine, moist mouth, urinating regularly
- Dehydrated: Dark urine, dry mouth, no urination 6+ hours, dizzy

**When to Escalate:**
- Not improving after 12-24 hours
- Getting worse
- Signs of dehydration
- Severe pain
- Blood in vomit or stool

### Infection Control

**If Suspected Stomach Virus:**
- Student isolates in room or separate area
- Own bathroom if possible
- Hand washing mandatory
- Clean surfaces with bleach solution
- No food handling
- Disposable plates/utensils if eating
- Out of activities until 24 hours symptom-free
- Monitor other students for symptoms

**Food Poisoning vs Stomach Virus:**
- Food poisoning: Starts 1-6 hours after eating, multiple people sick who ate same food
- Stomach virus: Gradual onset, spreads person-to-person over days

## Blisters

### Recognition
- Fluid-filled bubble on skin
- Usually from friction (shoes, sports)
- May be painful

### Severity Assessment

**URGENT (Medical evaluation):**
- Very large blister (> 2 inches)
- Signs of infection (red, warm, pus, red streaks, fever)
- Blister from burn (see burn protocol)
- Diabetic student with blister on foot

**NON-URGENT (First aid):**
- Small to medium blister
- Clean, no infection
- From friction

### Treatment Protocol

**DO NOT Pop Blister (Usually):**
- Leave intact if possible (natural protection)
- Clean area with soap and water
- Cover with bandage or moleskin (cut hole for blister)
- Change bandage daily

**If Blister Pops on Its Own:**
1. Wash hands
2. Clean area with soap and water
3. Apply antibiotic ointment
4. Cover with bandage
5. Watch for infection

**Prevention:**
- Properly fitting shoes
- Break in new shoes before program
- Moisture-wicking socks
- Moleskin on friction areas
- Band-aids at first sign of rubbing

**When to Escalate:**
- Signs of infection (redness spreading, pus, warmth, red streaks, fever)
- Large blister interfering with walking
- Diabetic student (foot blisters dangerous for diabetics)

## Minor Burns

### Recognition
- Red, painful skin (1st degree)
- Blisters (2nd degree)
- May be from sun, hot object, hot liquid

### Severity Assessment

**EMERGENCY (Call 911):**
- Burns to face, hands, feet, genitals, or joints
- 3rd degree burn (white, charred, or leathery skin)
- Burn > 3 inches diameter
- Chemical or electrical burn
- Difficulty breathing (smoke inhalation)

**URGENT (Medical evaluation same day):**
- 2nd degree burn (blistering) > 2 inches
- Burn on child < 5 years old
- Uncertain about severity
- Signs of infection

**NON-URGENT (First aid):**
- Small 1st degree burn (red, painful, no blisters)
- Sunburn
- Small 2nd degree burn (< 2 inches)

### Treatment Protocol

**Immediate:**
1. Remove from heat source
2. Cool burn with cool (not cold) water for 10-20 minutes
3. Do NOT use ice (can damage tissue)
4. Remove jewelry before swelling

**After Cooling:**
1. Gently pat dry
2. Apply aloe vera gel (sunburn) or antibiotic ointment
3. Cover with loose, sterile bandage
4. Pain relief: Ibuprofen or acetaminophen

**What NOT to Do:**
- Don't apply ice
- Don't use butter, oil, or home remedies
- Don't pop blisters
- Don't use fluffy cotton (fibers stick to burn)

**Sunburn Prevention:**
- Sunscreen SPF 30+
- Reapply every 2 hours
- Protective clothing
- Shade during peak sun (10am-4pm)

## Headaches

### Recognition
- Student complains of head pain
- May have various causes (stress, dehydration, lack of sleep, illness, vision problems, etc.)

### Severity Assessment

**EMERGENCY (Call 911):**
- "Worst headache of my life"
- Sudden, severe headache (thunderclap)
- Headache after head injury with confusion/vomiting
- Headache with fever and stiff neck (possible meningitis)
- Headache with vision loss or weakness
- Headache with seizure

**URGENT (Medical evaluation):**
- Severe headache not relieved by medication
- Headache with fever
- Headache with vomiting (no head injury)
- Headache lasting > 24 hours
- Frequent headaches (several per week)
- Headache with vision changes

**NON-URGENT (Rest and OTC medication):**
- Mild to moderate headache
- No other symptoms
- Responds to rest and medication
- Occasional occurrence

### Treatment Protocol

1. Rest in quiet, dark room
2. Hydrate (water)
3. Ibuprofen or acetaminophen (check allergies, get parent authorization)
4. Cool compress on forehead
5. Avoid screens, bright lights, loud noise
6. Light snack if hungry (low blood sugar can cause headaches)

**Common Causes in Teens:**
- Dehydration (most common)
- Lack of sleep
- Stress/anxiety
- Skipped meals
- Too much screen time
- Caffeine withdrawal
- Migraines (if student has history)

**When to Escalate:**
- Not improving with rest and medication
- Worsening
- Other symptoms develop
- Interfering with daily activities

## Sore Throat

### Recognition
- Painful swallowing
- Scratchy throat
- May have cold, allergies, strep throat, or other cause

### Severity Assessment

**URGENT (Medical evaluation):**
- Difficulty breathing or swallowing
- Drooling (can't swallow saliva)
- Severe pain
- Fever > 101°F
- White patches on throat
- Swollen glands
- Rash with sore throat
- Suspected strep throat (contagious!)

**NON-URGENT (Rest and supportive care):**
- Mild soreness
- Can swallow
- No fever or low-grade only
- Associated with cold symptoms

### Treatment Protocol

1. Rest
2. Hydrate (warm liquids soothing: tea, soup)
3. Salt water gargle (1/2 tsp salt in 8 oz warm water)
4. Throat lozenges or hard candy
5. Ibuprofen or acetaminophen for pain
6. Avoid irritants (smoke, yelling)

**Strep Throat:**
- Needs antibiotic (doctor visit)
- Very contagious
- Symptoms: Severe sore throat, fever, white patches, swollen glands, no cough
- Isolate until on antibiotics for 24 hours

**When to Escalate:**
- Not improving in 3-4 days
- Worsening
- Fever develops
- Difficulty swallowing or breathing

## Minor Allergic Reactions (NOT Anaphylaxis)

### Recognition
- Mild hives or rash
- Itching
- Mild swelling
- Sneezing, runny nose
- Watery eyes
- NO difficulty breathing, NO throat swelling, NO vomiting

### Severity Assessment

**EMERGENCY (Use EpiPen, Call 911) - This is Anaphylaxis:**
- Difficulty breathing
- Swelling of lips, tongue, or throat
- Hives PLUS any other system (breathing, stomach, etc.)
- Dizziness or fainting
- See Anaphylaxis playbook

**NON-URGENT (Antihistamine):**
- Mild hives
- Itching
- Sneezing
- Watery eyes
- One system only (just skin OR just nose)

### Treatment Protocol

1. Remove from allergen if identified
2. Antihistamine (Benadryl, Zyrtec, etc.) - check allergies, get authorization
3. Monitor closely for progression (can become anaphylaxis)
4. If symptoms worsen or breathing affected → EpiPen + 911

**Common Allergens:**
- Pollen
- Dust
- Animals
- Insect bites (local reaction)
- Soaps/lotions
- Latex

**When to Escalate:**
- Symptoms spreading or worsening
- Breathing affected
- Throat feels tight
- Multiple body systems involved

## Insect Bites and Stings

### Recognition
- Bee, wasp, hornet sting
- Mosquito, tick, spider bite
- Redness, swelling, itching, pain at site

### Severity Assessment

**EMERGENCY (Call 911) - Anaphylaxis:**
- Difficulty breathing
- Swelling of face, lips, tongue
- Hives all over body
- Dizziness
- See Anaphylaxis playbook

**URGENT (Medical evaluation):**
- Tick embedded > 24 hours (Lyme disease risk)
- Suspected spider bite (brown recluse, black widow)
- Large area of redness/swelling (> 4 inches)
- Red streaks from bite
- Fever
- Severe pain

**NON-URGENT (First aid):**
- Local reaction only
- Mild swelling, redness, itching
- No systemic symptoms

### Treatment Protocol

**Bee/Wasp Sting:**
1. Remove stinger if present (scrape with card, don't pinch)
2. Wash area with soap and water
3. Ice pack for 10 minutes
4. Elevate if on arm/leg
5. Antihistamine if itchy
6. Pain relief: Ibuprofen or acetaminophen

**Tick Bite:**
1. Remove tick with tweezers (grab close to skin, pull straight up)
2. Don't twist or crush tick
3. Clean area with soap and water
4. Save tick in sealed bag (for ID if symptoms develop)
5. Watch for rash or fever over next 30 days
6. If bull's-eye rash develops → doctor (Lyme disease)

**Mosquito/Other Bites:**
1. Wash with soap and water
2. Ice if swollen
3. Anti-itch cream or calamine lotion
4. Antihistamine if very itchy
5. Don't scratch (can cause infection)

**Prevention:**
- Insect repellent (DEET)
- Long sleeves/pants in woods
- Check for ticks after outdoor activities
- Avoid scented lotions/perfumes (attract bees)

## Common Cold/Upper Respiratory Infection

### Recognition
- Runny or stuffy nose
- Sneezing
- Sore throat
- Cough
- Mild headache
- Fatigue
- Low-grade fever or no fever

### Severity Assessment

**URGENT (Medical evaluation):**
- Difficulty breathing
- Wheezing (may be asthma)
- Fever > 101°F for > 3 days
- Severe sore throat (strep?)
- Ear pain (ear infection)
- Sinus pain and fever (sinus infection)
- Symptoms lasting > 10 days
- Cough with green/yellow/bloody phlegm

**NON-URGENT (Rest and supportive care):**
- Typical cold symptoms
- Low-grade fever or none
- Able to participate in activities
- Improving or stable

### Treatment Protocol

**Supportive Care:**
1. Rest
2. Fluids (water, juice, tea, soup)
3. Humidifier or steam (hot shower)
4. Saline nasal spray
5. Throat lozenges
6. Ibuprofen/acetaminophen for aches and fever
7. Cough drops

**What Doesn't Help:**
- Antibiotics (cold is viral, antibiotics for bacteria only)
- Vitamin C (doesn't shorten cold)
- Cold medications for teens (check with parents first)

**Isolation:**
- Out of activities first 24 hours if fever
- Encourage hand washing
- Cover coughs/sneezes
- May continue with activities if feeling well enough

**When to Escalate:**
- Not improving after 7-10 days
- Getting worse instead of better
- Fever develops or gets higher
- Difficulty breathing

## Key Policies for All Minor Emergencies

**First Aid Kit:**
- Mentors and leadership carry first aid kits
- Larger kit in program office
- Stocked with: bandages, antibiotic ointment, pain relievers, antihistamines, cold packs, etc.

**Over-the-Counter Medications:**
- Parent authorization needed
- Check for allergies
- Follow package dosing
- Document administration

**When to Call SSB Helpline:**
- Unsure about severity
- Student not improving
- Parent needs to be notified
- Need guidance on escalation

**When to Call Parents:**
- Any illness requiring medical evaluation
- Persistent symptoms
- Student requests parent contact
- Missing multiple activities due to illness

**Incident Reports:**
- Required for medical facility visits
- Required for significant illness (vomiting > 12 hours, etc.)
- Required if affects program participation significantly

**Isolation for Contagious Illness:**
- Stomach virus: 24 hours symptom-free
- Strep throat: 24 hours on antibiotics
- Fever: 24 hours fever-free without medication
- Contagious rash: Per doctor clearance

**Student Room Rest:**
- Check on student every 1-2 hours
- Ensure hydration available
- Bathroom access
- Call button or phone to reach staff
- Not left alone if significantly ill

**Returning to Activities:**
- Fever-free 24 hours without medication
- Able to participate comfortably
- Contagious period passed
- Eating and drinking normally
- No vomiting/diarrhea for 24 hours

## Red Flags - When to Escalate ANY Minor Issue

Even minor issues can become serious. Call 911 or seek urgent care if:

- Symptoms rapidly worsening
- Difficulty breathing
- Altered mental status (confusion, disorientation)
- Severe pain
- Signs of dehydration
- High fever (> 103°F)
- Persistent vomiting (can't keep fluids down)
- Suspected serious infection (red streaks, severe swelling, fever)
- Student or staff significantly concerned
- When in doubt

**Trust Your Gut:**
If something feels wrong or you're worried, escalate. Better to err on side of caution.
