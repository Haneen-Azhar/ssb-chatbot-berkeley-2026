# Asthma Attack - Medical Emergency

**Tags:** asthma, asthma attack, cant breathe, wheezing, shortness of breath, inhaler, breathing problems, chest tight, coughing, respiratory distress, medical emergency, trouble breathing, gasping for air

## Recognition Triggers

Asthma is a chronic condition where airways become inflamed and narrowed, making breathing difficult. An asthma attack (exacerbation) occurs when symptoms worsen suddenly. Students with asthma typically have an Asthma Waiver on file.

### Common Symptoms

**Mild Asthma Symptoms:**
- Coughing (especially at night or with exercise)
- Wheezing (whistling sound when breathing)
- Shortness of breath
- Chest tightness
- Difficulty keeping up with peers during physical activity

**Moderate Asthma Attack:**
- Increased coughing and wheezing
- Trouble completing full sentences
- Breathing faster than normal
- Using shoulders and neck muscles to breathe
- Nostrils flaring
- Skin between ribs pulls in with each breath
- Anxiety or fear

**Severe Asthma Attack:**
- Extreme difficulty breathing
- Cannot speak more than single words
- Lips or fingernails turning blue
- Very fast or very slow breathing
- Exhaustion or confusion
- Peak flow reading in red zone (if student uses peak flow meter)
- Inhaler not providing relief
- Panic

### Asthma Triggers (what causes attacks):
- Allergens (pollen, dust, mold, animals)
- Exercise (especially in cold air)
- Cold air or weather changes
- Respiratory infections (cold, flu)
- Air pollution or smoke
- Strong odors or fumes
- Stress or strong emotions
- Certain medications

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)

**Call 911 if student:**
- Lips or face turning blue
- Severe difficulty breathing (can't speak at all)
- Leaning forward, can't lie down
- Extremely anxious or panicked
- Confused or drowsy (not enough oxygen)
- Inhaler not helping after 10-15 minutes
- Condition worsening rapidly
- You're unsure and concerned

### URGENT (Inhaler + Close Monitoring + Call SSB Helpline)

**Immediate inhaler use needed if:**
- Coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath
- Chest tightness
- Difficulty speaking in full sentences
- Visible breathing effort
- Peak flow in yellow or red zone (if applicable)

**Call SSB Helpline if:**
- First asthma attack on program
- Multiple inhaler uses in short time
- Symptoms not fully resolving
- Need guidance on escalation
- Student anxious about symptoms

### NON-URGENT (Student Self-Management + Staff Awareness)

**Normal asthma management:**
- Student using maintenance inhaler as prescribed (daily)
- No active symptoms
- Participating fully in activities
- Using rescue inhaler before exercise (if in POA)
- Student feeling normal

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response (First 60 seconds)

**Seconds 1-20:**
- Ask student: "Are you having trouble breathing? Do you need your inhaler?"
- Have student stop activity immediately
- Position student sitting upright (NOT lying down)
- Stay calm and reassuring

**Seconds 21-40:**
- Locate student's rescue inhaler (student should carry it)
- Ask student to use inhaler or assist if needed
- Loosen tight clothing
- Encourage student to stay calm and breathe slowly

**Seconds 41-60:**
- Move student to well-ventilated area away from trigger if known
- Alert another staff member
- Note the time for documentation
- Observe breathing pattern

### Step 2: Medical Intervention

**Rescue Inhaler Use:**

**Student Self-Administration (Preferred):**
1. Student shakes inhaler
2. Student exhales completely
3. Student puts inhaler to mouth, forms tight seal with lips
4. Student presses inhaler while breathing in slowly and deeply
5. Student holds breath for 10 seconds
6. Student waits 1 minute before second puff if needed

**Typical Dosing:**
- 2-4 puffs of rescue inhaler (albuterol)
- Can repeat every 20 minutes up to 3 times
- If no improvement after 3 doses or 1 hour, call 911

**If Student Using Spacer:**
- Some students use spacer device with inhaler
- Inhaler attaches to spacer
- Student breathes normally from spacer mouthpiece
- Makes medication more effective
- Follow student's POA for their specific device

**If Student Using Nebulizer:**
- Some students use nebulizer (breathing treatment machine)
- Check if available in student's supplies
- Requires electricity and takes 10-15 minutes
- Delivers same medication as inhaler, just different method
- Follow POA for specifics

**Staff Assistance:**
- Students typically self-administer inhalers
- Staff may need to retrieve inhaler from bag
- Staff may need to help position student
- Staff may need to remind steps if student panicking
- Provide calm, clear instructions

**Positioning:**
- Keep student sitting upright
- Leaning slightly forward can help breathing
- NEVER lie student down during asthma attack
- Support student's back with pillows if needed

**Environment:**
- Move away from trigger if known (smoke, strong perfume, allergen)
- Fresh air if indoors (open window)
- Avoid extreme cold air (can worsen asthma)

### Step 3: Emergency Services

**Who to Call:**

**CALL 911 if:**
- No improvement after 10-15 minutes
- Symptoms worsening
- Student can't speak
- Blue lips or face
- Student very anxious or panicked
- Chest pain
- You're uncertain

**After Calling 911:**
- Continue inhaler use every 5-10 minutes until help arrives
- Keep student sitting upright
- Loosen clothing
- Stay calm and reassure student
- Monitor consciousness and breathing

**Call SSB Helpline for:**
- Guidance if symptoms moderate but not emergency
- First asthma attack on program
- Student used multiple inhaler doses
- Parents need notification
- Return to activity questions

**What to Say to 911:**
"We need an ambulance at [location]. We have a [age]-year-old with asthma having a severe asthma attack. Student has used rescue inhaler [X] times with [no/minimal] improvement. Student [can/cannot] speak. [Lips are blue / Student is extremely anxious / other concerning sign]. Attack started at [time]. Student's inhaler is [medication name]. We need immediate help."

**Information to Have Ready:**
- Student's name and age
- Known asthma history (check POA)
- Asthma triggers (allergies, exercise, etc.)
- Medications (rescue inhaler, maintenance inhaler)
- How many puffs taken and when
- Any allergies
- Recent illness
- What student was doing when attack started (exercise, exposed to allergen, etc.)

### Step 4: Documentation

**Immediate Documentation:**
- Time symptoms began
- What student was doing (activity, location)
- Possible trigger
- Time of first inhaler use
- Number of puffs taken
- Time intervals between doses
- Student's response to each dose
- Peak flow readings if student uses meter
- Time 911 called if applicable
- Who was present

**Incident Report:**
- Required for asthma attacks requiring multiple inhaler uses
- Required if 911 called
- Include all timeline details
- Note all medications used
- Document parent communication
- Submit same day to SSB HQ

**Ongoing Log (if monitoring continues):**
- Peak flow readings (if student uses meter)
- Inhaler use times
- Symptom severity
- Activity level
- Trigger exposure

### Step 5: Follow-Up

**0-2 Hours (Recovery Phase):**
- Student rests quietly (no physical activity)
- Sitting upright or comfortable position
- Fluids offered (can help thin mucus)
- Monitor breathing continuously
- No return to activity for at least 1-2 hours after symptoms resolve
- Parents notified
- Medical evaluation if 911 called or symptoms severe

**2-24 Hours (Observation Phase):**
- Modified schedule - no vigorous exercise
- Avoid known triggers
- Continue rescue inhaler as needed
- Ensure maintenance inhaler taken if prescribed
- Monitor for any symptom return
- Adequate rest
- Stay hydrated
- Mentor check-ins

**24+ Hours (Return to Activities):**
- Gradual return to full activity
- Check with parents about activity clearance
- Review POA for pre-exercise inhaler use
- Trigger avoidance
- Peak flow monitoring if applicable
- Ensure student carrying inhaler at all times

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### During Asthma Attack:
"[Name], I can see you're having trouble breathing. Get your inhaler out right now. Sit down here. You're going to be okay. Take your inhaler just like you always do. Good. Now slow breaths. I'm right here with you. You're doing great."

### If Student Panicking:
"[Name], look at me. You've had asthma attacks before and you know what to do. I'm right here. Take your inhaler. Now breathe slowly with me. In through your nose... out through your mouth. You're doing it. Good."

### To 911 Operator:
"This is an emergency. We're at [location]. We have a 15-year-old having a severe asthma attack. She has used her rescue inhaler 4 times in the last 20 minutes with no improvement. She can only speak single words. Her lips are starting to look pale. The attack started at 3:15 PM. Her inhaler is albuterol. We need an ambulance immediately."

### To Parent (Moderate Attack, No 911):
"This is [your name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] had an asthma attack this afternoon at [time]. [He/she] used [his/her] rescue inhaler and symptoms improved within 15 minutes. [Student name] is resting comfortably now and breathing normally. We're keeping [him/her] out of physical activity for the rest of the day. I wanted to let you know. Does [student name]'s doctor want to know about asthma attacks?"

### To Parent (Severe Attack, 911 Called):
"This is [your name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] had a severe asthma attack at [time]. We used [his/her] rescue inhaler but [he/she] wasn't improving, so we called 911. [Student name] is being transported to [hospital] by ambulance. I'm going with [him/her]. [He/she] is conscious and receiving oxygen. Can you meet us at the hospital? I'll call you with updates."

### To Student After Recovery:
"I'm glad you're breathing better. That must have been scary. How are you feeling now? Let's talk about what might have triggered this. Were you running? Was there something in the air? What can we do to prevent this from happening again?"

### Before Exercise (if POA indicates):
"[Name], we're about to do [physical activity]. Does your POA say you should use your inhaler before exercise? Yes? Okay, go ahead and take it now. We'll wait for you. Do you have your rescue inhaler with you too? Great."

## What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Lie student down (makes breathing harder)
- Leave student alone
- Tell student to "calm down" or "just breathe" (they're trying!)
- Give food or drink during active attack
- Encourage physical activity to "work through it"
- Dismiss symptoms as "just anxiety"
- Take inhaler away or ration puffs
- Use someone else's inhaler
- Delay calling 911 hoping it will improve
- Let student go to room alone to "rest"

**COMMON MISTAKES:**
- Assuming all coughing is just a cold (could be asthma)
- Not recognizing exercise-induced asthma
- Letting student leave inhaler in dorm room during activities
- Exposing student to known triggers (smoke, strong perfumes, etc.)
- Not taking "mild" symptoms seriously (can escalate quickly)
- Waiting too long to use rescue inhaler
- Not having emergency plan for specific triggers

**DON'T ASSUME:**
- "If it were serious, they'd say something" - Students may minimize or not recognize severity
- "They used their inhaler, so they're fine now" - Monitor for continued improvement
- "Asthma only happens during exercise" - Many triggers exist
- "They're just out of shape" - Asthma is a medical condition, not fitness issue

## Medical Information Needed

**Before Program (Check-In Day):**
- Asthma Waiver on file (doctor-completed form)
- Student's Asthma POA or medical info
- Severity of asthma (mild, moderate, severe)
- Known triggers
- Medications:
  - Rescue inhaler (albuterol/ProAir/Ventolin, etc.)
  - Maintenance inhaler (if daily medication prescribed)
  - Other asthma medications
- When to use pre-exercise inhaler
- Peak flow meter zones if student uses one
- Emergency action plan specific to student
- Recent asthma control (any recent attacks?)
- Allergies that trigger asthma

**Plan of Action Should Include:**
- Asthma triggers (specific to student)
- Baseline breathing (what's normal for this student)
- Warning signs student experiences
- Rescue inhaler instructions
  - How many puffs
  - How often can repeat
  - When to call 911
- Pre-exercise inhaler use (yes/no, when)
- Activity restrictions if any
- Peak flow zones if applicable
  - Green zone = good
  - Yellow zone = caution
  - Red zone = emergency
- Who to call with questions
- Maintenance medication schedule

**Required Accommodations:**
- Inhaler accessible at all times
- Student may need to use inhaler before exercise
- May need to sit out if symptoms present
- Avoid exposure to triggers (smoke, allergens, etc.)
- May need indoor activity option if air quality poor
- Pre-exercise warm-up period
- Permission to stop activity if needed

**Health Check Requirements:**
- Mentor conducts Health Check on check-in day
- Discuss current asthma control
- Verify medications and how to use
- Identify triggers to avoid
- Plan for exercising safely
- Ensure student comfortable advocating for needs
- Confirm student will carry inhaler always

**Required Staff Training:**
- Recognize asthma attack symptoms
- Know when to call 911
- Understand trigger avoidance
- Review Asthma Waivers for students on team
- Basic inhaler assistance if needed
- Don't panic - stay calm

## Who to Call and When

### Immediate Emergency Contacts (During Attack):

**1. 911** - If:
- Severe difficulty breathing
- Blue lips/face
- Can't speak
- Inhaler not helping
- Rapidly worsening
- Student very anxious/confused

**2. Another Staff Member**
- To get student's inhaler
- To call CD
- To help keep student calm
- To prepare for 911 if needed

**3. Campus Director**
- Immediately when attack begins
- For escalation decisions
- Coordinate response

### Post-Attack Contacts (After Student Stable):

**4. SSB Helpline (24/7)** - (888) 682-6671
- For moderate attacks not requiring 911
- First attack on program
- Multiple inhaler uses
- Guidance on return to activities
- Parent communication support

**5. Student's Emergency Contact**
- After student breathing better
- Inform of attack and response
- Discuss any activity modifications
- Ask about doctor notification

**6. Student's Doctor** (if in POA)
- With parent permission
- Asthma control concerns
- Medication adjustment questions
- Increased attack frequency

### Follow-Up Contacts (24-48 Hours):

**7. Campus Health Services** (if available)
- Follow-up check
- Peak flow monitoring
- Activity clearance
- Supply coordination

**8. Campus Activities Coordinator**
- If activity modifications needed
- Trigger avoidance (e.g., avoid area with construction dust)
- Indoor alternatives if air quality poor

## After-Action Procedures

### 0-2 Hours (Immediate Recovery):

**Student Care:**
- Rest in quiet, comfortable area
- Sitting upright or position of comfort
- NO physical activity
- Water offered (sips okay once breathing better)
- Calm, low-stress environment
- Staff supervision continuously
- Monitor for symptom return

**If 911 Called:**
- Staff accompanies to hospital
- Bring student's inhaler, medications, POA, insurance card
- Stay until parent arrives or discharge
- Get discharge instructions in writing
- Doctor's note for return to activities
- Coordinate transportation back

**If 911 Not Called:**
- Continued monitoring for 1-2 hours
- Parents notified
- SSB Helpline contacted for guidance
- Document response and recovery
- Modified schedule for rest of day

**Medication:**
- Rescue inhaler available
- Can use every 4-6 hours if mild symptoms persist
- Ensure maintenance inhaler taken if prescribed
- Check inhaler supply (running low?)

### 2-24 Hours (Observation & Modified Activity):

**Student Support:**
- No vigorous exercise for 24 hours (or per doctor)
- Light activity okay if breathing normal
- Avoid triggers
- Extra rest
- Stay hydrated
- Indoor activities if air quality poor
- Carry rescue inhaler at all times

**Medical:**
- Peak flow monitoring if applicable
- Maintenance inhaler compliance
- Rescue inhaler use logged
- Watch for symptom return
- Medical follow-up if needed

**Environmental:**
- Identify and avoid trigger
- Room check (dust, mold, allergens?)
- Activity modifications (indoor vs outdoor)
- Air quality monitoring if relevant
- Coordinate with facilities if environmental trigger

**Emotional:**
- Check in on student's feelings
- Asthma attacks can be scary
- Reassure about participation
- Avoid stigma or over-restriction
- Empower student to self-advocate

### 24+ Hours (Return to Full Activities):

**With Student:**
- Health check conversation
- How are you feeling?
- What do you think triggered the attack?
- Did you use your pre-exercise inhaler? (if applicable)
- Do you feel ready to return to full activities?
- What support do you need?
- Is your POA working for you?

**With Parents:**
- Follow-up call
- Student's recovery
- Trigger identified?
- Any concerns about continuing program?
- Does doctor need to be informed?
- Any POA adjustments?

**With Staff:**
- Team debrief
- What happened?
- How did we respond?
- Was POA helpful?
- What worked well?
- What could improve?
- Any training needs?

**Program Adjustments:**
- Trigger avoidance strategies
- Activity modifications only if necessary
- Enhanced monitoring if indicated
- POA updates if needed
- Inhaler accessibility confirmed
- Communication plan reinforced

**Prevention Focus:**
- Pre-exercise inhaler use (if in POA)
- Trigger avoidance
- Gradual warm-up for exercise
- Stay hydrated
- Recognize early symptoms
- Student empowered to speak up

### Debrief Questions (With Student):

1. How are you feeling now?
2. Have you had asthma attacks like this before?
3. What do you think triggered it?
4. Did you notice any warning signs?
5. Do you usually use your inhaler before exercise? Did you this time?
6. Is there anything we can do to help prevent this?
7. Do you feel comfortable participating in activities?
8. What support would be helpful?

### Debrief Questions (With Staff):

1. Who first noticed the student in distress?
2. Did student have inhaler readily accessible?
3. How quickly did we respond?
4. Was the Asthma Waiver/POA helpful?
5. Did we call 911 at the right time (or should we have)?
6. What worked well?
7. What was challenging?
8. Do we need additional training or resources?
9. Can we prevent this from happening again?

## Key Policy Reminders

**Asthma Waivers:**
- Students with asthma typically have Asthma Waiver on file
- Doctor-completed form
- Describes severity and triggers
- May include emergency plan
- Review before program starts

**Inhaler Accessibility:**
- Student MUST carry rescue inhaler at all times
- Cannot be left in dorm room or office
- Keep in pocket, bag, or with mentor as backup
- Check expiration dates
- Ensure adequate supply for program duration

**Self-Management:**
- Students manage their own asthma
- Students administer own inhalers
- Staff provide support, monitoring, emergency response
- Don't take inhaler away or ration doses
- Trust student's assessment of symptoms

**Activity Participation:**
- Students with asthma can participate in most activities
- May need pre-exercise inhaler (check POA)
- Allow self-pacing
- Provide rest breaks
- Indoor alternative if air quality poor
- Don't exclude unnecessarily

**Trigger Avoidance:**
- Identify student's specific triggers
- Avoid smoke exposure (no smoking near students ever)
- Manage allergen exposure where possible
- Consider air quality on outdoor activity days
- Avoid strong perfumes/fragrances around asthmatic students
- Cold air can trigger - plan accordingly

**Documentation:**
- IR for asthma attacks requiring intervention
- Log all inhaler use during acute episode
- Document triggers identified
- Parent communication noted
- Follow-up plan documented

## Medical References

**Current Enrollment Statistics (2026):**
- Students with asthma have Asthma Waiver on file
- Described as requiring inhaler for asthma management
- Severity varies (mild, moderate, severe)
- Many have exercise-induced asthma
- Some have allergic asthma

**SSB Resources:**
- Asthma Waiver forms (doctor-completed)
- Student medical info in Reach
- Health Check protocol
- Incident Reporting system
- 24/7 SSB Helpline support

**Common Rescue Inhalers:**
- Albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin, Proventil)
- Usually blue in color
- "Rescue" or "quick-relief" inhaler
- Used as needed for symptoms
- Can use before exercise to prevent symptoms

**Common Maintenance Inhalers (Daily):**
- Advair, Flovent, Pulmicort, Qvar, others
- Usually NOT blue
- Prevent asthma attacks
- Taken daily whether student has symptoms or not
- NOT for emergency use

**Peak Flow Meter:**
- Some students use this device
- Measures how fast air exits lungs
- Green zone = good (80-100% of personal best)
- Yellow zone = caution (50-80%) - use rescue inhaler
- Red zone = emergency (<50%) - use rescue inhaler and call doctor/911

**Asthma Action Plan:**
- Many students have written plan from doctor
- Specifies zones (green, yellow, red)
- What to do in each zone
- When to call doctor
- When to call 911
- This should be incorporated into SSB POA

**Important Notes:**
- Asthma is a chronic condition but well-managed with medication
- Attacks can range from mild to life-threatening
- Quick action with rescue inhaler usually resolves symptoms
- Students with asthma can be fully active with proper management
- Not all coughing or shortness of breath is asthma (could be other causes)
- Each student's asthma is unique - follow their specific POA
