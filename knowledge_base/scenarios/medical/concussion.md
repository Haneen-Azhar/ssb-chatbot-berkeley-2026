# Concussion - Medical Emergency

**Tags:** concussion, head injury, hit head, headache, dizzy, confused, nausea, threw up after hitting head, memory loss, medical emergency, traumatic brain injury, TBI

## Recognition Triggers

A concussion is a brain injury caused by a blow to the head or violent shaking. Not all concussions involve loss of consciousness. Recognition and proper management prevent complications.

### Mechanisms of Injury (How Concussions Happen)

**Direct Impact to Head:**
- Fall and hit head
- Hit by object (ball, equipment)
- Head hits wall, floor, ground
- Collision with another person

**Whiplash/Shaking:**
- Sudden stop (car accident, ride malfunction)
- Violent shaking
- Blast injury (rare but possible)

**Any Situation With:**
- Fall from height > 3-4 feet
- Sports collision
- Fight or assault
- Motor vehicle accident
- Playground injury
- Any blow to the head

### Concussion Symptoms

**Immediate Symptoms (May Appear Right Away):**
- Loss of consciousness (even briefly)
- Confusion or disorientation
- "Seeing stars" or dazed look
- Memory loss (doesn't remember what happened)
- Slow to respond to questions
- Blank stare
- Vomiting
- Balance problems

**Physical Symptoms:**
- Headache
- Dizziness
- Nausea or vomiting
- Blurred or double vision
- Sensitivity to light or noise
- Feeling sluggish or foggy
- Balance problems
- Ringing in ears

**Cognitive Symptoms:**
- Difficulty concentrating
- Difficulty remembering
- Confusion
- Slowed thinking
- Repeating questions

**Emotional Symptoms:**
- Irritability
- Sadness
- More emotional than usual
- Nervousness or anxiety

**Sleep Symptoms:**
- Drowsiness
- Sleeping more than usual
- Sleeping less than usual
- Difficulty falling asleep

**RED FLAGS (Emergency Symptoms):**
- Loss of consciousness > 30 seconds
- Worsening headache
- Repeated vomiting
- Seizure
- Slurred speech
- One pupil larger than the other
- Weakness or numbness in arms/legs
- Increasing confusion or disorientation
- Unusual behavior
- Cannot be awakened

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)

**Call 911 if:**
- Unconscious for > 30 seconds
- Not breathing or abnormal breathing
- Seizure
- Clear or bloody fluid from nose or ears
- Unequal pupils
- Severe or worsening headache
- Repeated vomiting
- Slurred speech
- Weakness or numbness
- Loss of consciousness after initial recovery
- Very drowsy, cannot stay awake
- Unusual or combative behavior
- Confusion worsening
- Neck pain (possible spinal injury)
- Fall from > 5 feet
- High-speed impact

### URGENT (Medical Evaluation Same Day)

**Urgent care or ER visit if:**
- Brief loss of consciousness (< 30 seconds)
- Any symptoms of concussion
- Vomiting once or twice
- Moderate headache
- Disorientation or confusion
- Memory problems
- Balance issues
- Vision changes
- Hit head and on blood thinners
- Hit head and has bleeding disorder

### NON-URGENT (Monitor + Call Doctor If Symptoms Develop)

**Monitor at rest if:**
- Minor bump to head
- No symptoms
- Completely alert and acting normal
- No loss of consciousness
- No vomiting
- No headache

**IMPORTANT:** Symptoms can appear hours or days later. Continue monitoring for 24-48 hours.

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response (First 60 seconds)

**Seconds 1-10:**
- Assess scene safety
- Check if student conscious and breathing
- Tell student not to move (possible neck injury)
- Call for help

**Seconds 11-30:**
- If unconscious: Check breathing, call 911, stabilize head/neck
- If conscious: Ask what happened, where does it hurt, do you know where you are?
- Observe for symptoms
- Support head and neck (possible spinal injury)

**Seconds 31-60:**
- Keep student still (lying down)
- Do NOT move student unless necessary
- Observe level of consciousness
- Note pupil size (equal or unequal?)
- Prepare to call 911 if symptoms present

### Step 2: Medical Intervention

**Immediate Assessment:**

**Check Consciousness:**
- Is student awake and alert?
- Responds to questions?
- Knows who they are, where they are, what day it is?
- Remembers what happened?

**Check Pupils:**
- Equal size?
- Both react to light?
- Unequal pupils = emergency

**Check Movement:**
- Can student move all extremities?
- Any numbness or weakness?
- Any neck pain? (DO NOT MOVE if neck pain)

**Baseline Neuro Check:**
- Name?
- Where are you?
- What day is it?
- What happened?
- Count backwards from 20
- What's your address?
- Follow my finger with your eyes (smooth tracking?)

**If Any Symptoms Present:**
- Keep student lying down
- Stabilize head and neck
- Do NOT give food, drink, or medication
- Call 911 or arrange urgent medical eval
- Continuous monitoring

**If No Symptoms:**
- Monitor closely for at least 4-6 hours
- Student should not be alone
- No physical activity
- No screens (phone, computer, TV)
- Rest in quiet, dim environment
- Recheck every 15-30 minutes

**Concussion Protocol:**

**Immediate Actions:**
1. Remove from activity immediately (no return to play)
2. Medical evaluation (ER or urgent care same day)
3. No driving, biking, or operating equipment
4. Supervised at all times
5. Rest in quiet, dim room
6. No screens
7. No physical or cognitive exertion
8. Monitor for worsening symptoms

**Concussion Assessment Tools:**
- Some staff may be trained in SCAT5 (Sport Concussion Assessment Tool)
- Balance testing
- Memory testing
- Symptom checklist
- Use if trained; otherwise, arrange medical evaluation

### Step 3: Emergency Services

**Who to Call:**

**CALL 911 if:**
- Unconscious > 30 seconds
- Red flag symptoms (see above)
- Possible neck/spine injury
- High-impact trauma
- Student condition worsening

**TRANSPORT TO ER if:**
- Any concussion symptoms
- Brief loss of consciousness
- Confusion or disorientation
- Vomiting
- Headache

**What to Say to 911:**
"We need an ambulance at [location]. A [age]-year-old hit [his/her] head and [is unconscious / was unconscious for X seconds / is confused and vomiting]. The injury happened at [time] from [fall, collision, etc.]. [He/she] has [describe symptoms]. [He/she] [is/is not] responding appropriately. We are stabilizing [his/her] head and neck."

**Information to Have Ready:**
- Mechanism of injury (exactly how it happened)
- Loss of consciousness? How long?
- Current symptoms
- Changes since injury (better, worse, same?)
- Any vomiting?
- Headache severity?
- Pupil assessment
- Memory and orientation status
- Baseline behavior vs current

### Step 4: Documentation

**Immediate Documentation:**
- Exact time of injury
- Mechanism of injury (detailed description)
- Loss of consciousness? Duration?
- First symptoms and when they appeared
- All symptoms observed
- Baseline assessment results
- Pupil check results
- Student's orientation and memory
- Any changes over time
- Who witnessed injury

**Concussion Baseline (If Available):**
- Some sports programs do baseline concussion testing before season
- Helpful for comparison if student has baseline on file
- Not always available

**Incident Report:**
- Required for all head injuries
- Required for all suspected concussions
- Include detailed mechanism
- All symptoms and timeline
- Medical facility and diagnosis
- Return-to-play protocol started

**Ongoing Symptom Log:**
- Track symptoms daily
- Symptom severity (1-10 scale)
- Any new symptoms
- Activities attempted and response
- Sleep quality
- Headache patterns

### Step 5: Follow-Up

**0-2 Hours (Acute Phase):**
- Medical evaluation (ER or urgent care)
- Diagnosis confirmed or ruled out
- CT scan if concerning symptoms
- Discharge instructions
- Return-to-learn and return-to-play protocol explained
- No physical or cognitive exertion
- Parent notification and pickup or close monitoring on campus

**2-24 Hours (First Day):**
- Complete rest
- Dark, quiet room
- No screens (phone, TV, computer)
- No reading
- No physical activity
- Sleep encouraged
- Light meals if tolerated
- Supervised constantly
- Wake every 2-3 hours overnight to check on student (if on campus)
- If symptoms worsen, return to ER

**24-72 Hours (Days 2-3):**
- Continue rest
- Gradual return to cognitive activity if symptoms improving
- Light reading, quiet conversation okay if tolerated
- Still no screens or physical activity
- Monitor symptoms
- If symptoms return with activity, return to complete rest

**Days 3-7:**
- If symptoms improving: Gradual return to school/cognitive work
- 30 minutes of cognitive activity, rest, assess symptoms
- If no increase in symptoms, increase activity gradually
- Still no physical activity beyond walking
- Continue monitoring

**Week 2+:**
- If symptom-free at rest: Begin return-to-play protocol
- Must be cleared by doctor before any physical activity
- Gradual progression over 5-7 days
- Any symptom return: stop and drop back a level

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### Immediately After Injury:
"[Name], stay still. Don't move your head. You hit your head and I need to make sure you're okay. Can you hear me? Do you know where you are? I'm going to ask you some questions. What's your name? What day is it?"

### If Student Confused:
"[Name], you hit your head. You're at Summer Springboard. I'm [your name], a staff member. You're safe. We're going to get you checked out. Just stay still and rest. Don't try to get up."

### To 911:
"This is an emergency at [location]. A 16-year-old fell and hit her head on the floor. She was unconscious for about 20 seconds. She's awake now but confused and doesn't remember what happened. She vomited once. Her pupils are equal. The injury happened 10 minutes ago. We need an ambulance."

### To Parent (Concussion):
"This is [name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] hit [his/her] head today during [activity]. [He/she] has symptoms of a concussion including [list symptoms]. We're taking [him/her] to [hospital] for evaluation. [He/she] is conscious and talking but needs medical assessment. Can you meet us there?"

### After Diagnosis:
"The doctor confirmed you have a concussion. Here's what that means: Your brain needs to rest to heal. For the next few days, no physical activity, no screens, no reading. Just rest. We'll gradually add activities back as you feel better. This is temporary. Most concussions heal completely with proper rest. But it's really important you follow these instructions."

### Return-to-Play Explanation:
"You can't participate in physical activities until you're symptom-free and cleared by a doctor. I know that's frustrating, but it's for your safety. If you return to activities too soon, you could get a second concussion which is very dangerous. We'll find other ways for you to participate. You can still be part of the group."

## What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Move student if possible neck/spine injury
- Let student "shake it off" and return to activity
- Give pain medication before medical evaluation
- Allow screen time (phone, computer, TV, video games)
- Let student sleep without periodic checks (first 12 hours)
- Allow student to be alone
- Let student drive, bike, or operate equipment
- Dismiss symptoms because "they seem fine"
- Allow return to physical activity before medical clearance
- Rush recovery (brain needs time to heal)

**COMMON MISTAKES:**
- "They didn't lose consciousness so it's not a concussion" - FALSE. Most concussions don't involve LOC.
- "They seem fine now so they can play" - FALSE. Symptoms can be delayed or worsen.
- "A little activity won't hurt" - FALSE. Physical and cognitive rest critical for healing.
- "It's been a week, they're fine now" - Not necessarily. Recovery varies.
- Not recognizing delayed symptoms (can appear hours or days later)
- Allowing student to self-assess readiness (poor judgment common with concussions)
- Not getting medical clearance before return to sports

## Medical Information Needed

**From Student (If Able):**
- What happened?
- Did you black out?
- Do you remember everything?
- Where does your head hurt?
- Do you have any other pain?
- Any previous concussions?
- Are you on any medications?

**From Medical Forms:**
- History of concussions (increases risk of prolonged recovery)
- History of migraines (can complicate recovery)
- Medications (especially blood thinners)
- Bleeding disorders
- Previous brain injury or surgery

**Baseline Information (If Available):**
- Pre-season concussion baseline testing
- Student's normal personality and behavior
- Academic performance (to assess cognitive changes)

## After-Action Procedures

### 0-24 Hours (Acute Rest Phase):

**Complete Physical and Cognitive Rest:**
- Dark, quiet room
- No screens
- No reading
- No physical activity beyond bathroom
- Sleep encouraged
- Light meals if tolerated
- No noise or stimulation
- Supervised constantly

**Overnight Monitoring (If on Campus):**
- Wake student every 2-3 hours
- Check orientation (name, where they are)
- Check symptoms (headache, nausea)
- If worsening, return to ER

**If Parent Picks Up:**
- Give written monitoring instructions
- When to return to ER
- Symptom log sheet
- Return-to-learn and return-to-play protocols
- Follow-up instructions

### Days 2-7 (Gradual Return to Cognitive Activity):

**Progression (Only if Improving):**

**Day 2-3:**
- Continue physical rest
- Light cognitive activity if tolerated (15-30 minutes)
- Conversation, light reading
- If symptoms increase, stop and rest
- Still no screens

**Day 4-5:**
- Increase cognitive activity gradually (30-60 minutes)
- May try short screen time (15 minutes) if tolerated
- Light walking okay if no symptom increase
- Still no vigorous activity

**Day 6-7:**
- Return to school/class if symptom-free with cognitive work
- May need accommodations (see below)
- Still no physical education or sports
- Monitor symptoms

**Accommodations for Return-to-Learn:**
- Extra time on assignments
- Reduced workload
- Frequent breaks
- Dim lighting
- Quiet environment
- No standardized testing
- Excused from homework if symptoms increase
- Rest periods during day

### Week 2+ (Return-to-Play Protocol):

**Must Be Symptom-Free at Rest Before Starting**
**Must Have Doctor Clearance Before Starting**

**5-Step Progression (Minimum 24 Hours Each Step):**

**Step 1: Light Aerobic Activity**
- Walking, light jogging, stationary bike
- No resistance training
- 5-10 minutes
- If symptoms return, stop and rest 24 hours

**Step 2: Moderate Activity**
- Jogging, moderate intensity stationary bike
- 15-20 minutes
- Still no contact or jarring activities

**Step 3: Heavy Non-Contact Activity**
- Running, high-intensity stationary bike
- Resistance training
- Non-contact drills in sport
- 20-30 minutes

**Step 4: Full Contact Practice**
- Normal training with contact
- Full participation in practice

**Step 5: Return to Full Play**
- Normal game play
- Cleared for all activities

**If Symptoms Return at Any Step:**
- Stop immediately
- Rest 24 hours minimum
- Drop back to previous step
- May need medical re-evaluation

### Long-Term Monitoring:

**Second Impact Syndrome:**
- Getting a second concussion before first heals can be catastrophic
- Can cause severe brain swelling and death
- This is why no return to play until completely healed
- Watch for any head impact even after clearance

**Post-Concussion Syndrome:**
- Symptoms lasting > 3 months
- Headaches, dizziness, cognitive issues
- Needs specialist care (neurologist, sports medicine)
- May need physical therapy, vision therapy, accommodations

**Multiple Concussions:**
- Each concussion increases risk of future concussions
- Recovery takes longer with each subsequent concussion
- May need to consider discontinuing high-risk activities
- Specialist consultation critical

### Debrief Questions (With Student):

1. How are you feeling today?
2. Any headaches, dizziness, or other symptoms?
3. Did you sleep okay?
4. Are you able to concentrate?
5. Do you feel like yourself?
6. Have you tried any activities? How did you feel after?
7. Do you understand the return-to-play protocol?
8. Do you have questions about your recovery?

### Debrief Questions (With Staff):

1. How did the injury happen?
2. Was it preventable?
3. Is there a safety hazard that needs addressing?
4. Did we recognize concussion symptoms quickly?
5. Did we handle the immediate response appropriately?
6. Is the student receiving proper monitoring?
7. Are accommodations in place?
8. What can we do to prevent head injuries?

## Key Policy Reminders

**When in Doubt, Sit Them Out:**
- Any suspected concussion = removal from activity
- Medical evaluation required same day
- No return to play without medical clearance

**No Same-Day Return:**
- Even if symptoms resolve quickly, no return to activity same day
- Brain needs time to recover
- Minimum 24-48 hours rest

**Cognitive Rest is Critical:**
- Brain uses energy to heal
- Screens, reading, thinking all use brain energy
- Rest = healing

**Graduated Return:**
- Don't rush
- Each step minimum 24 hours
- Symptom return = stop and rest
- Medical clearance required

**Documentation:**
- IR for all head injuries
- Detailed symptom tracking
- Medical evaluations documented
- Return-to-play progression tracked

**Education:**
- Student and parent education on concussion
- Importance of reporting symptoms
- Long-term risks of repeated concussions
- Compliance with protocols

## Medical References

**Concussion Statistics:**
- 50% of concussions have NO loss of consciousness
- Symptoms can appear immediately or be delayed hours/days
- Most concussions resolve in 7-14 days with proper rest
- 10-15% have symptoms > 3 months (post-concussion syndrome)
- Second concussion before first heals is extremely dangerous

**Symptoms Timeline:**
- Immediate: LOC, confusion, dizziness, memory loss
- First 24 hours: Headache, nausea, fatigue, light/noise sensitivity
- Days 2-7: Cognitive symptoms, mood changes, sleep issues
- Delayed: Some symptoms may not appear for days

**Red Flags (Return to ER):**
- Worsening headache
- Vomiting repeatedly
- Seizure
- Increasing confusion
- Slurred speech
- Cannot wake up
- Unusual behavior
- One pupil larger than other
- Weakness or numbness

**Return-to-Play Timeline:**
- Minimum 1 week (if symptom-free after a few days)
- Average 2-3 weeks
- Longer for multiple concussions or prolonged symptoms
- Children and teens take longer to recover than adults
- Must be symptom-free before starting progression

**SSB Resources:**
- 24/7 SSB Helpline for immediate guidance
- Medical facility partnerships
- Incident reporting system
- Academic accommodations support
- Staff training on concussion recognition

**Important Notes:**
- Concussions are brain injuries - take seriously
- "Dings," "bell rung," "seeing stars" = concussion
- Every concussion is serious
- Proper management critical for full recovery
- Long-term effects possible with repeated concussions
- Prevention: proper equipment, safe play, supervision
