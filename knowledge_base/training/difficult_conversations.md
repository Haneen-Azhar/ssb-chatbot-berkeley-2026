## Disciplinary Conversations with Students

### Behavioral Process Overview

**Step 1: Official Warning**
- May be delivered verbally or in writing
- **Two staff members must be present**
- Document the incident

**During the Warning:**
- Clearly explain the behavior that violated program expectations
- Ensure the student understands the program rules and expectations
- Explain the consequences if the behavior continues
- Provide guidance on how the student can correct the behavior
- Contact parents or guardians to inform them

**Step 2: Written Behavioral Contract**
- Used when behavior doesn't improve after Official Warning OR serious misconduct occurs
- Contract developed by Campus Leadership in collaboration with PD
- PD will ALWAYS be involved

**Contract Must Include:**
- Behaviors that violated program expectations
- Expectations moving forward
- Consequences for any additional violations

**Must be Signed By:**
- The student
- The Campus Director (or designated leadership staff member)
- Additional staff member serving as witness

**Additional Steps:**
- Upload contract into an Incident Report (IR)
- Contact parents or guardians
- Remind of responsibility for arranging and covering cost of transportation home if dismissed

**Step 3: Dismissal from the Program**
- Violation of written contract or serious misconduct
- **PD or HQ will ALWAYS be involved**
- Dismissal decisions made in consultation with SSB HQ, PD, and Campus Leadership
- Students required to return home at parent/guardian expense
- Program tuition and fees are non-refundable
- Dismissal is final

## Conversation Scripts for Student Discipline

### Official Warning Conversation

**Opening:**
"[Student name], we need to talk about what happened [yesterday/this morning/etc.]. I've asked [other staff member] to join us because this is an official conversation about your behavior."

**Explain the Violation:**
"Here's what happened: [specific behavior]. This violates our program expectation that [specific rule from Code of Conduct]."

**Check Understanding:**
"Can you tell me in your own words what the expectation is and why it exists?"

**Explain Consequences:**
"If this behavior continues, the next step is a written behavioral contract. If there's another violation after that, you could be sent home from the program."

**Provide Guidance:**
"Here's what I need to see from you moving forward: [specific behavioral expectations]."

**Close with Support:**
"We want you to be successful here. Do you have any questions about what we've discussed?"

### Behavioral Contract Conversation

**Opening:**
"[Student name], we're meeting because the behavior we discussed in your official warning has continued [OR because a serious incident occurred]. We're going to create a written contract together."

**Review the Situation:**
"Let's review what's happened: [list specific incidents with dates/times]"

**Present the Contract:**
"This contract clearly states:
- The behaviors that violated our expectations
- What we expect from you going forward
- What will happen if there are any additional violations"

**Be Clear About Stakes:**
"I want you to understand that this is serious. If you violate this contract, you will be sent home from the program. Your parents will need to arrange and pay for your transportation home."

**Get Buy-In:**
"Do you understand everything in this contract? Do you have questions? Are you willing to commit to these expectations?"

**Sign Together:**
"We'll all sign this contract - you, me, and [witness staff member]."

## Parent Conversations

### Handling Upset Parent Calls

#### The Opening
"Hello [parent name], this is [your name], [your role] at [campus]. I understand you have some concerns. I want to hear what's going on."

#### Active Listening Phase
- Let them talk without interrupting
- Take notes
- Don't get defensive
- Don't make promises you can't keep

#### Validation Without Agreement
"I hear that you're concerned about [issue]. I understand why that would be upsetting."
- This validates their feelings without agreeing with their assessment

#### Fact Gathering
"Let me ask some questions so I fully understand the situation..."
- When did this happen?
- What specifically did [student] tell you?
- What outcome are you hoping for?

#### Response Framework
**What You Know:**
"Here's what I can tell you right now: [facts]"

**What You Need to Find Out:**
"I need to [gather more information/speak with staff/review records] before I can fully address this."

**Timeline:**
"I will [specific action] and follow up with you by [specific time]."

#### Never Say
- "Your student is lying"
- "That's not how it happened"
- "I don't know" (without a plan to find out)
- "That's just how things are"
- "I can't do anything about that"

#### Always Say
- "Let me look into this"
- "I'll follow up with you by [time]"
- "Student safety is our top priority"
- "I want to understand this fully"

### Calling Parents About Student Misconduct

#### Prepare First
- Have all facts documented
- Know exactly what happened, when, where, who was involved
- Have the student's file/POA available if relevant
- Know what the next steps are
- Have another staff member present if possible

#### The Structure

**1. Introduction**
"Hi [parent name], this is [your name] calling from Summer Springboard at [campus]. Do you have a few minutes to talk about [student name]?"

**2. State the Purpose**
"I'm calling because I need to let you know about a situation that happened today involving [student name]."

**3. Present the Facts**
"Here's what happened: [chronological account of events, stick to facts only]"

**4. Explain the Action Taken**
"Based on our code of conduct, we [issued official warning/created behavioral contract]. This is because [explain policy]."

**5. What Happens Next**
"Moving forward, we expect [specific expectations]. If [student name] [violates again], the consequence will be [next disciplinary step]."

**6. Open for Questions**
"Do you have any questions about what happened or what we expect going forward?"

**7. Partnership Close**
"We want [student name] to be successful here. Can we count on your support in reinforcing these expectations?"

## Staff Performance Conversations

### Coaching vs. Performance Management

#### When to Coach
**Coaching = First Response**

Ask yourself:
- Does the staff member understand the expectations?
- What support do they need?
- What tools/resources are missing?
- What leadership adjustments may help?
- Have expectations been clearly communicated?

**Use SSB Staff Coaching and Performance Management Report to document**

#### When It Becomes Performance Management

**Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) Needed When:**
- Issues continue after coaching
- Accountability conversations are documented
- Student/staff safety/experience is being impacted

**Indicators:**
- Repeated operational concerns
- Ongoing professional issues
- Failure to improve after coaching
- Multiple documented concerns

### Coaching Conversation Script

**Opening:**
"Hey [staff name], can we chat for a few minutes? I want to check in with you about [specific area]."

**Observation, Not Accusation:**
"I've noticed that [specific observable behavior]. Can you help me understand what's going on?"

**Listen:**
Let them explain. There may be legitimate reasons or challenges you're unaware of.

**Clarify Expectations:**
"Here's what I need to see: [specific expectations]"

**Offer Support:**
"What support do you need from me to make this happen?"
"Is there anything getting in the way that we can problem-solve together?"

**Set Timeline:**
"Let's check in again in [specific timeframe] to see how things are going."

**Document:**
Complete the coaching report immediately after the conversation.

### Performance Improvement Plan Conversation

**This is More Formal:**
Have another leadership team member present if possible.

**Opening:**
"[Staff name], we're meeting today to discuss some ongoing concerns about your performance. This is a formal conversation, and we'll be creating a Performance Improvement Plan together."

**Review Previous Conversations:**
"We've talked about [issues] on [dates]. Despite our conversations and the support we've offered, [specific behaviors] haven't improved."

**Explain PIP:**
"A Performance Improvement Plan clearly outlines:
- The specific areas that need improvement
- Exactly what success looks like
- The timeline for improvement
- The support we'll provide
- The consequences if improvement doesn't happen"

**Be Direct About Consequences:**
"I want to be clear: if these issues don't improve by [date], you may be sent home from the program."

**Document Together:**
Review the written PIP together, sign it, and give them a copy.

## Safety and Wellbeing Conversations

### When a Student Discloses Abuse or Harm

**Immediate Response:**
1. Thank them for trusting you
2. Listen without judgment
3. Don't promise confidentiality (you're a mandated reporter)
4. Don't investigate - that's not your role

**What to Say:**
"Thank you for telling me this. I'm glad you felt safe talking to me."

"I want you to know that I'm required to report this to [CD/HQ] because your safety is the most important thing."

"You did the right thing by telling someone. This is not your fault."

"What you've shared will only be shared with people who need to know to keep you safe."

**What NOT to Say:**
- "I promise I won't tell anyone"
- "Are you sure that's what happened?"
- "Why didn't you tell someone sooner?"
- Questions that feel like interrogation

**Immediate Actions:**
1. Report to CD immediately
2. CD reports to PD/HQ immediately
3. Document exactly what the student said (use quotes)
4. Do not question the student further
5. Maintain normal support and supervision

### Mental Health Crisis

**If a Student Expresses Suicidal Thoughts:**

**Stay Calm and Present:**
"I'm really glad you told me this. You're not alone."

**Assess Immediate Safety:**
- "Are you thinking about hurting yourself right now?"
- "Do you have a plan?"

**Don't Leave Them Alone:**
- Stay with them
- Have someone else get the CD immediately

**What to Say:**
"I care about you and we're going to get you the help you need."
"You're safe here. We're going to figure this out together."

**What NOT to Say:**
- "You don't really mean that"
- "You have so much to live for"
- "Think about your family"
- Anything that minimizes their feelings

**Escalate Immediately:**
This always requires CD/PD/HQ involvement and likely mental health professional support.

## Conversation Tips Across All Scenarios

### Before the Conversation

**Prepare:**
- Know the facts
- Have documentation ready
- Know what outcome you're seeking
- Choose the right time and place (private, not rushed)
- Have support (another staff member) if needed

### During the Conversation

**Use the LEAP Method:**
- **L**isten
- **E**mpathize
- **A**gree (on what you can)
- **P**artner (find common ground)

**Body Language:**
- Maintain open posture
- Make appropriate eye contact
- Keep voice calm and steady
- Don't cross arms or appear defensive

**Language Choices:**
- Use "I" statements: "I noticed..." "I'm concerned about..."
- Avoid "you always" or "you never"
- Be specific, not general
- Focus on behavior, not character

### After the Conversation

**Document:**
- What was said
- What was agreed upon
- Next steps
- Timeline for follow-up

**Follow Through:**
- Do what you said you'd do
- Check in at the agreed-upon time
- Continue to support the person

**Debrief:**
- Process with your supervisor or team
- Don't carry it alone
- Learn from each conversation

## Common Difficult Topics

### Student Homesickness

**Conversation:**
"I notice you seem down today. What's going on?"

**Listen** to their feelings.

**Validate:**
"It makes sense that you miss home. Being away is hard."

**Redirect:**
"What's one thing that might make today a little better?"

**Stay Connected:**
"Let's check in again tomorrow. I'm here if you need to talk."

### Student Conflict with Roommate

**Bring Both Students Together:**
"I know you're both frustrated. Let's figure this out together."

**Let Each Speak:**
"[Student A], tell me your perspective."
"[Student B], now you share yours."

**Find Common Ground:**
"You both want to be able to sleep and feel comfortable in your room, right?"

**Problem-Solve Together:**
"What are some solutions you can both agree on?"

**Set Clear Expectations:**
"Here's what I need from both of you..."

**Follow Up:**
"I'm going to check in with you both tomorrow to see how it's going."

### Staff Wanting to Quit

**Immediate Response:**
"I hear that you're overwhelmed. Let's talk about what's going on."

**Understand Root Cause:**
- Is it workload?
- Is it interpersonal conflict?
- Is it misaligned expectations?
- Is it burnout?

**Problem-Solve:**
"What would need to change for you to feel able to continue?"

**Be Realistic:**
"Here's what I can adjust... Here's what I can't change..."

**Set Timeline:**
"Can you commit to [end of session/end of week] while we work on solutions?"

**If They Still Want to Leave:**
- Don't take it personally
- Focus on smooth transition
- Protect student experience
- Document and report to PD

## Remember

1. **Preparation** prevents poor performance in difficult conversations
2. **Stay calm** - your energy affects the outcome
3. **Listen more** than you talk
4. **Document** everything
5. **Follow up** always
6. **Process** with your team afterward - don't carry it alone
7. **Learn** from each conversation to get better

Every difficult conversation is an opportunity to build trust, solve problems, and model healthy communication for students.
