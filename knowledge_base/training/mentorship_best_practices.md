# Mentorship Best Practices

## What Makes a Great Staff Member?

### The Six Core Roles

1. **Mediator**: Mediating conflict and maintaining a positive group dynamic
2. **Model**: Be a role model to your students
3. **Caretaker**: Take care of the physical and mental health of your students
4. **Listener**: Be an active, empathetic listener
5. **Facilitator**: Facilitate meaningful discussion and reflection
6. **Teammate**: Offer proactive assistance to your team members, for the good of the overall program

## Building Rapport with Students

### First Impressions Matter
- Your attitude and energy sets the standard from the jump
- Students are always watching and following your example
- The way you treat people determines whether you'll get their best effort, a perfunctory effort, or an effort to sabotage you

### Setting the Tone
Examples of tone setting:
- Being on time
- Communicating clearly and consistently
- Staying positive, even when things go in a different direction than planned
- Relationship building through intentional connection

### Active Listening (80/20 Rule)
- Use "I" statements to initiate conversations
- Use 80/20 rule (listening/talking)
- Pay attention to verbal and emotional cues: BE PRESENT
- Give feedback without interrupting:
  - Paraphrasing (repeating)
  - Reflecting (words to emotions)
  - Silence (allowing time to complete thoughts)

### Stay Curious, Not Judgmental
- "Tell me more about what led you to that conclusion?"
- Remind students of community agreements they signed
- Treat mistakes as pivots for educational growth, not just discipline

## Mentor Groups

### Purpose
- Foster deeper connections between students and staff
- Provide manageable group sizes (typically 10 students per mentor)
- Serve as functional safety measure for attendance and headcounts
- Create space for integration of experiences and reflection

### Best Practices

**Find the Right Space**
- Conducive to inclusivity and privacy
- Encourage students to sit within group, not outside of it

**Pay Attention to Ratios When Organizing**
- Residential vs Commuters
- Enrolled Courses
- Rooming Arrangements
- Gender and Age

**Never Skip Mentor Groups**
- These are crucial times for facilitating integration
- Essential for student counts and safety
- Opportunity to relay information in smaller groups

**Know Your Students**
- Understand key medical information (allergies, POAs, physical limitations, epi pens)
- Be aware of privileged information (mental health history, triggers, gender identity)
- Serve as trusted confidant to prevent escalation of issues

### First Mentor Group Session

Should include:
- Introductions and icebreakers
- Questions to engage students
- Clear explanation of what mentor groups are for
- Building initial trust and connection

**Important**: Do NOT disclose any information in a student's medical info to the full group. All information should be private and it is up to the student whether they would like to share. Some students may not want to disclose an ASD diagnosis or that they are trans.

## Student Engagement Strategies

### Model Self-Regulation
Students learn by watching you:
- Getting up with an alarm
- Going to gym
- Keeping a clean room
- Being on time
- Eating meals together
- Making friends through initiating, bonding, treating others with respect and kindness

### Show Empathy and Leadership
- Demonstrate positive ways to handle challenges
- Model how to navigate new environments
- Be patient with students who have varied experiences (some have traveled the world, some have never left home)

### Support Academic Engagement
As academic mentor liaison:
- Guide students to/from class early on
- Take daily attendance and learn who is in the class
- Interact with instructors to ensure students are in class, on time, and doing well
- Check in regularly with both students and instructors
- Attend and chaperone excursions
- Model networking opportunities during excursions
- Take photos in class and during excursions

## Recognizing Student Needs

### Types of Students You'll Encounter
- Some have traveled the world; some have never left home
- Some speak multiple languages; some have never been around so many new people
- Some have never woken up by their own alarm
- Some have never paid for things using a debit card
- Some have never had roommates or shared a room

### Your Role
Challenge yourself to find ways to regularly support students to help them:
- Grow and lean into new experiences
- Navigate daily challenges
- Build independence
- Create sense of belonging

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Sharing a Private Space with Students
NEVER:
- Share a room with a student
- Be alone with a student in a closed room without others present
- Touch a student when no one else is around
- Share your personal phone number (use Reach instead)

ACCEPTABLE:
- Being in a room with 3 other students present
- Accept a hug from a student in an open space with other students around

### Over-identifying with Students
- Maintain professional boundaries
- You are a mentor, not their peer
- Avoid creating exclusive relationships with certain students

### Not Escalating Concerns
- When in doubt, escalate
- Better to report and have it be nothing than miss something important
- You are a mandated reporter

## Working with Different Student Populations

### Neurodivergent Students
- Rely on program structure to lead the student
- Ensure itinerary is always written and visible
- Present important information in small chunks
- Allow more buffer time between activities
- Connect with students on best way to communicate need or support
- Make sure to give specific instructions and ask if there are questions
- Allow use of headphones or mental breaks
- Let students know to check in if feeling overwhelmed

### Students with Physical Health Conditions
- Do a Health Check on check-in day with CD present
- Review their POA and discuss any supports needed or unclear information
- All staff must read POAs for students with significant health conditions
- Students with mobility devices should have mentor supporting them side-by-side initially

### Students with Mental Health Needs
- SSB is not a therapeutic program - we cannot diagnose
- Use POA as a tool to support the student
- Create safe dialogue space about best way to support student
- Non-disclosure will continue to be a reality - handle with care
- Time away from home can trigger new issues

## Building Trust with Your Team

### Supporting Fellow Mentors
- Open communication
- Empowering those around you
- Learning about each other
- Offering proactive assistance for the good of the program

### Ways to Build Team Culture
- Shout-outs at daily meetings
- Team meals (breakfast together after attendance, Friday morning brunch)
- Team activities after weekend excursions
- Staff bonding moments (tie-dye t-shirts, raffle/movie nights)

## Professional Development

### What Mentors Gain from SSB
- Leadership skills
- Professional experience working with diverse groups
- Conflict resolution practice
- Communication skills
- Problem-solving under pressure
- Team collaboration experience

### Connecting Back to Goals
- First day and last day of session, tie back to personal goals
- Bring up goals throughout the program
- Regular check-ins with leadership about progress
- Reflecting on what you want to learn or gain from this experience
