# Student Engagement Strategies

## What Students Expect from SSB

### Core Experience Goals
- Experiencing campus culture
- Fine-tuning career exploration techniques
- Getting an experiential approach to an academic area of interest
- Having fun in a safe and caring environment
- Creating a sense of independence and belonging
- Making connections (friendships, networking)
- Learning skills (time management, decision making, getting out of comfort zone)

## First Impressions: Check-In Day

### Setting the Stage
Staff impact starts from the very first moment:
- Airport pickup and drop-off
- Check-in on campus
- Engaging all students in icebreaker activities
- First meals together
- Creating welcoming atmosphere

### The Importance of Energy
From the student perspective, mentors and leadership are leading the way to an epic experience. Your energy, attitude, and engagement set the tone for their entire program.

## Building Connection

### Initial Icebreakers

**First Mentor Group Activity:**
Should include:
- Introductions (name, where you're from, why you chose this program)
- Fun icebreaker that gets everyone laughing
- Questions to engage students
- Explanation of what mentor groups are for

**Example Icebreaker:**
"Arrange yourselves in one line by birthdate (month, date) without using words or verbal communication. You have three minutes!"

### Getting to Know Your Students

**About Me Exercise:**
Have students share:
- Where were you born
- What are you passionate about
- What grade you're in
- Favorite school experience
- Favorite hobby or recreation
- Career interests or subjects you want to study

**True Colors Activity:**
- Helps students discover personality styles
- Builds understanding of different types
- Creates common language for the group
- Acknowledges individuality while identifying commonalities

## Daily Engagement Touchpoints

### Morning Check-Ins
- Take attendance at breakfast
- Notice who's quiet, who's energized
- Quick emotional temperature checks
- "How are you feeling about today?"

### Academic Support
As academic track mentor:
- Guide students to/from class (especially early on)
- Take daily attendance
- Check in with instructors about student engagement
- Notice if students seem lost or confused
- Attend and support during excursions

### Meal Times
- Eat with students
- Rotate which students you sit with
- Use this time for informal conversation
- Notice social dynamics and who might be isolated
- Model healthy eating habits

### Transition Times
These are prime engagement opportunities:
- Walking to class
- Traveling to excursions
- Moving between activities
- Use this time for conversations, not just logistics

## Mentor Group Meetings

### Purpose
- Facilitate integration of experiences
- Ensure academic learning and social time are beneficial to growth
- Create space for reflection and processing
- Build deeper connections in smaller groups

### Structure
- Meet daily (sometimes throughout the day)
- Find space conducive to inclusivity and privacy
- Encourage students to sit within group, not outside of it
- Never skip mentor groups - they're crucial

### Discussion Topics

**Week 1 Focus:**
- Getting to know each other
- Setting group norms
- Understanding program expectations
- Building trust

**Week 2 Focus:**
- Reflecting on academic experience
- Processing social connections
- Preparing for college
- Thinking about next steps

### Engagement Questions

**For Academic Reflection:**
- "What surprised you in class this week?"
- "What's one thing you learned that you want to remember?"
- "How does this connect to your interests?"

**For Social/Emotional:**
- "What's been the highlight of your day?"
- "What's been challenging?"
- "Who have you connected with?"
- "What's something new you tried?"

**For Future Planning:**
- "How has this experience changed your thinking about college?"
- "What skills are you developing here?"
- "What do you want to do differently in the second week?"

## True You Sessions

### Purpose
Dedicated reflection session for students to:
- Pause and think about their Summer Springboard journey
- Explore thoughts on college, potential majors, career paths
- Reflect on social experiences
- Gain clarity and direction

### Why It Matters
- Helps students understand all pieces of the SSB experience (not just academic)
- Creates clarity and confidence around college decisions
- Students gain confidence articulating the value of their experience
- Prepares them for "in-the-car moment" with parents
- Shows value in shared self-reflection

### Your Role as Leadership
You are NOT leading the sessions. Mentors lead True You.

**You're Responsible For:**
- Training and preparing mentors to lead with confidence
- Ensuring all logistical and environmental needs are met
- Being available during True You to handle disruptions
- Allowing mentors and groups to stay focused and on task

### Activities

**Middle School True You:**
- Getting to know ourselves and each other
- True Colors personality assessment
- Writing a letter to our future self

**High School True You:**
- Reflecting on academic experience
- Social connections made
- College and career insights gained
- Personal growth moments

## Engaging Different Types of Students

### The Outgoing Student
- Give them leadership opportunities
- Channel their energy positively
- Use them to help quieter students
- Watch for dominating conversations

### The Quiet Student
- Create opportunities for one-on-one connection
- Don't force them to share in large groups
- Ask them directly for their thoughts in smaller settings
- Validate that quiet is okay

### The Homesick Student
- Acknowledge their feelings without dismissing
- Help them connect with others
- Point out moments of joy or connection
- Check in daily
- Encourage them to call home at designated times (not constantly)

### The "Too Cool" Student
- Don't take it personally
- Find what they care about
- Give them responsibility
- One-on-one conversations can reveal more

### The Student Who Doesn't Want to Be There
- Understand why (parent pressure, changed their mind, etc.)
- Find one thing they can enjoy
- Don't force fake enthusiasm
- Set minimum expectations for participation
- Document and communicate with leadership

## Clubs and Activities

### Purpose
Clubs offer students opportunity to:
- Socialize around an interest in smaller groups
- Meet students outside their mentor group and academic class
- Try new activities in low-pressure environment

### Best Practices

**Club Fair Night:**
- Make it fun and engaging
- Have mentors create marketing materials
- Use passport system for students to visit all tables
- Encourage students to step outside comfort zone

**During Club Time:**
- Be present and engaged
- Participate yourself (models enthusiasm)
- Notice who's connecting and who's isolated
- Allow student leadership to emerge
- Take photos

**Club Types:**
- Athletic (sports club, walking club, GT Trekkers)
- Creative (Art & Chill, Yearbook Club)
- Academic (Study Club)
- Social (Movie Club, Cafe Hoppers, Cooking Club)
- Program Support (Soiree Planning)

### Evening Activities vs. Clubs

**Clubs:**
- Smaller groups
- Non-academically focused
- Led by mentors
- Focused on core activity

**Evening Activities:**
- Whole group team building
- Broader socialization
- Planned and executed by leadership and mentors
- Examples: Movie Night, Karaoke, Flag Painting, Talent Show, Cultural Exchange Night

## Excursions

### Academic Excursions
- Connect to course content
- Professional environment exposure
- Networking opportunities

**Your Role:**
- Ensure students are engaged
- Model networking behavior
- "Did you notice how the professional introduced themselves?"
- Encourage questions
- Take photos
- Debrief afterward

### Weekend Excursions
- City exploration
- Cultural experiences
- Team bonding

**Engagement Strategies:**
- Let students have voice in free time choices (within boundaries)
- Create memorable moments
- Encourage students to step outside comfort zones
- Capture the experience (photos, reflections)

## Managing Group Dynamics

### Building Inclusion
- Notice cliques forming
- Intentionally mix up groups
- Create opportunities for different students to interact
- Address exclusion immediately

### Addressing Conflict
- Don't let tensions fester
- Address issues early while they're small
- Mediate when needed
- Teach conflict resolution skills
- Model healthy disagreement

### Celebrating Success
- Acknowledge growth and effort
- Public praise for positive behavior
- Create traditions (weekly awards, shout-outs)
- Photo walls or memory boards

## Signs a Student Needs More Support

### Behavioral Red Flags
- Withdrawing from activities
- Always alone
- Sleeping more than usual
- Not eating
- Sudden personality change
- Conflicts with multiple peers
- Rule-breaking behavior

### What to Do
1. Check in privately
2. Ask open-ended questions
3. Listen without judgment
4. Assess severity
5. Report to leadership
6. Document
7. Follow up

### When to Escalate
- Student expresses thoughts of self-harm
- Severe homesickness affecting functioning
- Bullying or harassment
- Health concerns
- Any safety issue

**Purpose:**
Helps students:
- Set intentions at the beginning
- Reflect at the end
- See their own growth

**Prompts:**
- "I came to Summer Springboard because..."
- "I chose to come to [campus] because..."
- "I hope to learn ___ about myself while I'm here"
- "One thing I am excited about this week is ___"
- "My goal this week is to ___"
- "My advice to myself this week is ___"

**Implementation:**
- Done in first True You session
- Students write to themselves
- Sealed in envelopes
- Opened on last day of program

## Engagement Tips from Alumni Staff

### What Worked Well
- Small campuses: allowed students to choose 2 clubs (gave more connection opportunities)
- Passport system for club fair (ensured students learned about all offerings)
- Creative clubs specific to location:
  - MIT: Kendall Explorers (local cafes/dessert spots), Ping Pong Tournament
  - UCSD: Board Game Club, Destress Club, Mystery/True Crime Club
  - Berkeley: Dungeons and Dragons
- Regular mentor group themes or competitions
- Student-led activities during free time
- Celebrating small wins publicly

### Common Mistakes
- Skipping mentor groups (students notice and connection suffers)
- Not addressing small conflicts (they become big ones)
- Forcing participation instead of encouraging it
- Not being present during activities (phones out, distracted)
- Focusing only on logistics, not relationships
- Treating all students the same instead of individualizing support

## Measuring Engagement

### Informal Assessment
- Are students showing up on time?
- Are they participating in discussions?
- Are they smiling and laughing?
- Are they connecting with peers?
- Are they asking questions?
- Are they trying new things?

### Formal Check-Ins
- Student pulse surveys (Day 3)
- One-on-one conversations with mentor
- Instructor feedback
- Parent feedback
- Final program evaluation

## Remember

**Students will forget what you said, forget what you did, but will never forget how you made them feel.**

Your engagement with students:
- Creates the foundation for their experience
- Models healthy relationships
- Builds trust that allows learning
- Makes SSB memorable and impactful
- Shapes how they see themselves

Every interaction is an opportunity to make a positive impact.
