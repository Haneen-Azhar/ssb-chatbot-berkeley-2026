## Program Overview

**Summer Springboard** is a pre-college summer enrichment program designed to provide students with an engaging and enriching summer experience that fosters their personal growth, academic exploration, and social development.

The program combines academic coursework with residential life experiences, college readiness workshops, recreational activities, and city exploration to give students a comprehensive taste of college life.

---

## Mission, Values & Goals

### Core Mission
Summer Springboard is committed to nurturing student curiosity and passion while empowering discovery of new interests and lifelong learning habits.

### Core Values

- **Inclusive learning environment** where everyone feels valued, challenged, and empowered to reach their full potential
- **Supportive community** built on respect, empathy, and resilience
- **Holistic development** combining academics with essential life skills

### Primary Goals

1. **Academic enrichment** - Students explore subjects relevant to college preparation and future studies
2. **Skill-building** - Development of critical thinking, problem-solving, communication, teamwork, leadership, and creativity
3. **Personal growth** - Self-discovery through exploring passions and strengths
4. **Social-emotional development** - Building meaningful peer connections and cultivating empathy and resilience
5. **Confidence building** - Boosting self-esteem through achievement and personal challenge

### Program Purpose

Summer Springboard aims to "inspire the leaders and innovators of tomorrow" by creating transformative experiences that combine academic knowledge with essential life preparation, ultimately preparing youth to "thrive in an ever-changing world."

## Staff Roles & Responsibilities

### Program Director

**Overview:**
The Program Director oversees campus operations, management, and program vision. They handle on-site setup, remote issue support, parent communication, and provide essential information to the Campus Director and Academic Manager.

**Pre-Summer Preparation:**
- Establish infrastructure for campus success, including the Summer Staff Portal and comprehensive Staff Binder
- Prepare pre-program training materials, allowing the Campus Director to lead while contributing on Terra policies
- Ensure access to all excursion confirmations linked in the Master Schedule

**During Summer Operations:**
- Provide behind-the-scenes support when field leadership has exhausted resources
- Conduct daily morning check-ins regarding on-call updates and incident reports
- Introduce Campus Directors and Academic Managers to conference representatives, then step back from direct communication
- Ensure instructor contact information is distributed; limit direct PD-instructor contact to curriculum and pay issues

**Key Relationships:**
- **With Campus Director:** Clear communication and collaboration. While the Campus Director manages day-to-day operations, the Program Director retains authority for major decisions and provides ongoing guidance on program quality and risk management.
- **With Academic Manager:** Supports instructor management through the Academic Manager, escalating issues such as curriculum concerns, instructor absences, or resignations.

### Campus Director (CD)

**Position Overview:**
The Campus Director serves as the senior on-site leader, holding "primary responsibility for student safety, staff supervision, and campus operations." They function as the main contact point for families, university partners, and headquarters staff.

**Core Responsibilities:**

**Leadership & Operations:**
- Direct all on-campus activities and ensure program quality standards are maintained
- Make timely decisions affecting students, staff, and day-to-day functioning
- Report complex issues to headquarters as needed

**Student & Staff Management:**
- Oversee residential staff including Academic Managers, Mentors, and Assistants
- Handle escalated student matters covering medical concerns, behavioral incidents, and emergencies
- Provide coaching, feedback, and performance oversight

**Logistics & Communication:**
- Coordinate housing, meals, transportation, and facility arrangements
- Manage campus supplies and budget tracking
- Communicate professionally with families regarding program or student concerns

**Key Partnerships:**
- **With Academic Manager:** The CD and Academic Manager function as "a cohesive team" requiring "open and proactive communication, mutual respect, and a commitment to the program's mission." Daily check-ins are strongly recommended.
- **With Resident Mentors:** The CD provides "guidance, support, and leadership" while mentors offer "transparent communication" and report issues immediately to the director.

### Academic Manager (AM)

**Position Overview:**
The Academic Manager serves as the second-in-command to the Campus Director. This individual "lives on campus, participates in the rotating on-call schedule, and shares responsibility for student supervision and well-being."

**Key Responsibilities:**

**Academic Leadership:**
The AM oversees academic scheduling, classroom logistics, and instructor coordination. They "develop, manage, and execute the academic schedule in collaboration with the Campus Director and instructors" while managing academic excursions and off-campus activities.

**Student Support:**
This role involves monitoring student engagement and addressing academic concerns. The AM works to "serve as a visible, accessible leader supporting student learning and engagement."

**Residential Leadership:**
As a live-on staff member, the Academic Manager shares responsibility for "student supervision, safety, and well-being" and participates in on-call coverage for after-hours support.

**Team Collaboration:**
The position requires close coordination with multiple teams: the Campus Director, Summer Program Assistant, mentors, and instructors to ensure cohesive program operations.

**Leadership Coverage:**
The AM "step[s] in for the Campus Director as needed to maintain operational continuity," including assisting with student conduct matters and escalated situations.

**Professional Requirements:**
The role demands completion of pre-program training, attendance at mandatory sessions, and familiarity with Summer Springboard policies and procedures.

### Resident Mentor & Alumni Mentor

**Overview:**
Mentors serve as the primary supervisors and support system for students, living on campus and participating in rotating on-call schedules.

### Teaching Assistant (TA)

**Overview:**
Teaching Assistants support instructors and enhance student learning experiences. They work Monday-Friday from 9:00 AM to 12:00 PM, plus Wednesday afternoon excursions.

**Key Responsibilities:**

**Classroom Support:**
- "Lecture Assistance: TAs may help during lectures by managing classroom equipment, distributing handouts, and facilitating discussions."
- Tracking student attendance and participation
- Leading discussion sections to deepen understanding of course material
- Supervising student projects and assessing their advancement
- Preparing and distributing course materials like notes and readings
- Coordinating class activities and guest lectures
- Acting as a communication bridge between students and instructors regarding course matters

**Attendance Duties:**
TAs must ensure attendance records are updated by 9:15 AM daily. They report late or missing students to assigned mentors, who then take action such as contacting families or checking dorm rooms.

**Additional Duties for TA-Mentors:**
Staff members serving dual roles as both TA and Mentor have expanded responsibilities:
- Supervise afternoon activities including workshops and projects
- Assist with evening clubs or group activities (2-3 times weekly)
- Support one full-day weekend excursion while supervising students
- Participate in staff meetings (on-campus residents)

### Summer Program Assistant (SPA)

**Position Overview:**
The SPA serves as "a key member of the on-site leadership team, supporting the daily operations, logistics, and student experience" of Summer Springboard programs. This residential position requires rotating on-call duties and reports to both the Campus Director and Academic Manager.

**Core Responsibilities:**

**Operations & Logistics:**
SPAs handle "setup, breakdown, and coordination of academic sessions, workshops, events, and excursions." They manage inventory, coordinate materials, and maintain records related to attendance and scheduling.

**Student Supervision:**
Staff members "supervise and engage with students during meals, activities, excursions, and free time" while modeling appropriate conduct and upholding program codes of conduct.

**Safety & On-Call Duties:**
The role includes "rotating supervision and on-call schedules, including evening, overnight, and weekend responsibilities as assigned."

**Team Communication:**
SPAs function as "a communication bridge between mentors, students, and campus leadership" while participating in staff meetings and contributing to collaborative team culture.

**Key Qualifications:**
The position demands strong organizational skills, adaptability, sound judgment in sensitive situations, and a genuine commitment to student-centered work in a residential setting.

### Instructor

**Core Duties:**
Instructors lead classroom sessions with students, including project work, track-specific guest speakers, and excursions. High school instructors teach 9 class periods of 3 hours each over two weeks, while middle school instructors teach 5 periods. Both groups must participate in required afternoon activities on specific days.

**First Day Expectations:**
On the opening Monday, instructors arrive at 9 AM to meet with campus leadership and their assigned mentor. The mentor provides parking passes, exchanges contact information, and sets up the classroom. Classes begin at 10 AM on day one, then shift to 9 AM start times for remaining sessions. All instructors use the same opening presentation and distribute worksheets asking students about their course expectations.

**Mentor Partnership:**
Each instructor receives an assigned mentor who serves as their program liaison. The mentor handles attendance, receives updates about student absences or behavioral concerns, and attends excursions. However, mentors don't provide classroom support or make last-minute materials—instructors must arrive fully prepared.

**Communication Protocols:**
Instructors contact their mentor/teaching assistant for student-related issues like absences, lateness, or behavior concerns. Academic questions, curriculum concerns, or requests to modify programming go directly to the Program Director.

**Session Completion:**
At program end, instructors must sign Letters of Participation for each student, typically writing a brief generic paragraph describing course content and learning outcomes.

## How to Run a Summer Program

### The Staff Summer Portal

The Staff Summer Portal is described as "the holy grail of links for staff to all important and frequently visited files."

**Four Main Categories:**

1. **Campus General Information** - Resources applicable across all sessions, including emergency plans, master schedules, QR codes for daily updates, supply lists, housing/meal assignments, and curriculum materials.

2. **Session-Specific Materials** - Documents and lists particular to individual program sessions: student folders with medical information, housing assignments, dietary restrictions, medication records, and participant certificates.

3. **CD/AM Access Only** - Restricted materials for Campus Director and Academic Manager roles: staff onboarding documents, emergency contact information, and phone directories.

4. **Post Session Review** - Documents prepared after program completion for headquarters and leadership review: staff evaluations, supply assessments, and transportation records.

### Classroom Expectations

**Schedule and Attendance:**
- **First Day**: Classes begin at 10am; instructors arrive at 9am for setup
- **Regular Days**: Classes run 9am-12pm (excluding excursion days)
- **Attendance**: Mentors take attendance every morning at 9am

**Instructor Preparation:**
Instructors receive an email one week before their first session with:
- Parking details
- Location of pre-program training (with a map of campus)
- Classroom location
- Mentor contact information and point-of-contact name

**Mentor Responsibilities:**
Mentors serve as classroom support by:
- Greeting instructors and facilitating campus orientation
- Exchanging contact information with teaching staff
- Ensuring students arrive by the designated start time
- Accompanying instructors on academic excursions

### Campus Boundaries

**Overview:**
Each campus provides boundary maps to students. At program start, mentors conduct orientation walks to familiarize students with permitted leisure areas.

**Movement Requirements:**

**Groups of Three Rule:**
Students must travel in groups of at least three people during off-campus activities within campus boundaries. If separated, students should return immediately to their residential college or the designated Emergency Checkpoint.

**Off-Campus Permission Protocol:**
Students cannot leave the program campus without parental authorization. The process requires:

- **Written approval only:** "A phone call from a parent or guardian is not sufficient to grant off-site permission."
- **REACH app submission:** Requests must be submitted through the REACH platform
- **Advance notice:** Minimum 24-hour notice required before any off-site activity
- **Campus Director involvement:** Students must coordinate with the Campus Director to initiate permission requests

The system emphasizes that only documented parental consent through the official REACH application satisfies permission requirements—verbal authorization is explicitly insufficient.

## Meetings & Scheduling

### Daily Meetings

**Purpose & Timing:**
The daily staff meeting serves as a critical routine touchpoint, ideally scheduled during student academic tracks. Meetings should be brief most days but include several in-depth sessions weekly.

**Core Agenda Components:**

1. **Updates & Action Items** - "Share updates for the day and review the schedule" to ensure team alignment on responsibilities and identify scheduling concerns.

2. **Student Check-In** - Staff discuss observations about student welfare, identify students of concern, document issues, and collaboratively develop support strategies.

3. **Program Planning** - The team reviews accomplishments, discusses challenges, and plans logistics including supplies, backup plans, and activity preparation/debriefing.

4. **Personal Check-In** - Staff reflection on team performance, individual wellbeing, sleep adequacy, and mutual appreciation for collective effort.

### Communication Principles

The recommended facilitation approach follows the "80-20 Rule": staff should speak roughly 80% of the time while facilitators speak 20%, using open-ended questions and active listening. The phrase "Tell me more" serves as a key coaching tool to maintain engagement and balance.

### Weekly Programming Meeting

Campus directors must complete a collaborative weekly report submitted to HQ supervisors, documenting campus conditions, student/staff concerns, successes, challenges, and support recommendations.

## Risk Management & Safety

### Core Risk Management Philosophy

Summer Springboard views risk management as "a culture and habit that we collectively embody" rather than merely a checklist. The organization emphasizes integrating safety considerations into daily operational decisions while taking calculated, strategic risks within sensible parameters.

### Five-Point Safety System

SSB implements a comprehensive safety framework:

1. **Safe Campus Environments**: Students reside in on-campus dorms with dedicated mentors and 24-hour campus police protection.

2. **Expert Staff Supervision**: Residential teams include trained personnel with CPR/First Aid certification and conflict resolution skills. Staff-to-student ratio maintains one mentor per 10 students.

3. **Student Code of Conduct**: All participants must follow established behavioral expectations that promote safety and community respect.

4. **Off-Campus Safety**: Excursions utilize licensed drivers and safe vehicles. Supervision maintains "at least 8-to-1" student-to-staff ratios with predetermined free-time boundaries.

5. **Student Accountability**: Students receive safety briefings at program start and understand that rule violations may result in removal from the program.

### Key Responsibilities

The manual outlines that program staff members must "collect information, monitor, investigate and evaluate injury, illness and near miss incidents." Management regularly reports on policies addressing near misses, changes, and safety developments to the Board of Directors annually.

### Safety Goals

The organization prioritizes preventing fatalities and serious injuries while designing protocols for emergency response and crisis management.

### Incident Reporting Procedures

**Definition of an Incident:**
SSB considers an incident to be "an occurrence of an action that is dangerous, or results in need for medical treatment, hospital visits, medical or mental health emergency, student behavioral issues or disciplinary actions taken by staff, property damage, near miss (close calls) or other loss (or potential loss)."

**Four-Step Incident Handling Process:**

**Step 1: Stabilize the Incident**
Address the situation according to your training level while prioritizing the safety and security of all students involved.

**Step 2: Categorize the Incident**
Classification determines reporting urgency:

- **Non-Urgent**: Close calls and minor issues that don't escalate but warrant documentation
- **Urgent**: Code of Conduct violations, threats, behavioral disruptions, or potentially dangerous situations
- **Emergency**: Incidents causing or risking serious injury or death

**Step 3: Communicate Details**
Essential information to provide includes:

- "Who is affected? What took place, when, where and why?"
- Current management steps underway
- External communications made (parents, police, media)
- Impact on group dynamics and individual wellbeing
- Specific assistance needed and uncertainties requiring guidance
- Follow-up plans and responsible parties

**Step 4: Submit Documentation**

Submit incident reports through one of three methods:

1. Complete the [online Incident Report form](https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLSdvScv2Kfcxbkh2N81ukoJLocuTXmVt1d1xrjpqX5q_Rk-IKg/viewform)
2. Email alerts@summerspringboard.com
3. Send a photo of written documentation to your Program Director

**Follow-Up Responsibilities:**
- Program Directors contact Campus Directors daily to monitor case status
- Campus Directors serve as liaisons between mentors and Program Directors
- Cases mentioning "case closed" require no further follow-up

## SSB Policies

### Staff Code of Conduct

**Safety and Supervision:**
- Staff must maintain high engagement and supervision throughout sessions
- Required staff-to-student ratios: 1:10 (high school), 1:8 (middle school)
- Distinction made between student unstructured time and staff personal time; mentors supervise both

**Campus Boundaries and Student Movement:**
- Staff must enforce established campus boundaries during all program hours
- "Boundary maps are also provided and available in the Staff Hub"
- Students must travel in groups of at least three; no solo movement permitted
- Staff monitor compliance and assist students who struggle finding peers
- Pre-program training includes mandatory boundary walk led by campus leadership

### Personal Time During Sessions

- Staff may take 1–2 evenings off per session (typically 6:00 PM to 8:00 AM)
- Time-off schedules must maintain required supervision ratios
- Staff taking time off must remain reachable by phone and stay within two hours of campus
- Campus Leadership rotates on-call coverage throughout sessions
- Staff remain program representatives even during personal time

### Personal Time Between Sessions

- Time off typically runs from session conclusion Friday through Sunday
- Staff must remain reachable by phone and within two-hour proximity to campus
- Staff must inform leadership of any travel plans and expected return times
- Split-campus teams have modified schedules prioritizing acclimation to new locations

### Time-Off & Between-Session Protocols

**During Programming:**

**Personal Time Allocation:**
Staff receive 1–2 evenings off per session, typically 6:00 PM to 8:00 AM. The policy states: "Personal time is intended to allow staff to rest, recharge, and attend to personal needs while ensuring that student supervision, safety, and program operations remain fully supported."

**Coverage Requirements:**
Campus leadership must develop written schedules maintaining required supervision ratios (1:10 for high school, 1:8 for middle school). Time off is distributed equitably without compromising programming.

**Staff Obligations During Personal Time:**
Staff must remain phone-accessible, stay within two hours of campus, and inform leadership of travel plans. The manual emphasizes: "Staff members remain representatives of Summer Springboard throughout the duration of the program" and must maintain professional conduct even during personal hours.

**Between Sessions:**

**Standard Break Period:**
Time off typically runs from Friday evening (after debriefing) through Sunday. Staff must remain reachable and within two hours of campus unless they notify leadership of travel plans.

**Multi-Campus Teams:**
Teams relocating between campuses have reduced personal time, as preparation for new student arrival takes priority, though rest remains encouraged.

### Back-to-Back Session Coverage

**Minimum Requirements:**
Two mentors minimum supervise students during interim weekends, handling meals, transportation, and activities.

**Compensation:**
Assigned mentors receive one additional night off (6:00 PM–8:00 AM) as compensation, scheduled with leadership approval.

**Assignment Process:**
Volunteers are solicited first; insufficient volunteers trigger fair rotational assignment with a maximum of one weekend per cycle unless voluntary.

## Parent Management

### Core Philosophy
SSB maintains responsibility for keeping parents informed about their children's well-being, especially regarding mental health concerns. The organization recognizes that "predicting human behavior is a near-impossible task" and that parents may respond differently to disclosure requests about their child's mental health background.

### Parent Response Patterns
Parents typically fall into three categories:
1. **Transparent communicators** (~33%) who provide accurate, helpful information
2. **Non-disclosers** (~33%) who offer little substance, sometimes due to family denial or fear of program rejection
3. **Comprehensive sharers** (~33%) who detail everything, yet their children often thrive without incident

### Effective Communication Guidelines

When discussing student mental health concerns with parents, staff should:

- **Lead with observation**: Use "concrete, specific language about how their son or daughter is behaving" rather than diagnoses
- **Focus on impact**: Document observable behaviors and explain how they affect other program participants
- **Gather context**: Ask about similar patterns at home and successful management strategies
- **Listen actively**: "We can often get less resistance and more buy-in from parents if we do as much listening as we do talking"
- **Remain diplomatic**: Avoid defensiveness while staying calm and factual

### When Early Dismissal Occurs
In rare cases, HQ may recommend sending a student home early after consulting with program staff and a psychological consultant. SSB typically recommends professional evaluation or treatment facility placement upon return home, though implementation remains the family's decision.

## Middle School Program

### Program Duration & Structure
Unlike high school programs lasting two weeks, middle school offerings operate for **"one week"** with check-in on Sunday afternoon and Friday afternoon dismissal. Both residential and commuter formats are available.

### Age Group & Supervision Intensity
Participants range from 12-14 years old. The younger demographic necessitates heightened oversight compared to high school cohorts. "Staff members are present at all times to provide supervision and support," with **minimal unstructured free time** built into daily schedules.

### Key Safety Protocols

**Movement Restrictions:** Students cannot travel independently. They must operate in **"groups of at least three"** on campus and during excursions. Separated students must return immediately to residence halls or designated emergency checkpoints.

**Permission Requirements:** Off-campus departures require **"prior written permission from a parent or guardian,"** a stricter policy than typical high school programming.

**Support Structure:** Each participant receives assignment to a **"Mentor Group"** providing regular check-ins, guidance, and event accompaniment.

### Campus Locations
Middle school programs operate at UC Berkeley, Georgetown University, and UC San Diego—smaller than the broader high school network.

### Program Components
Both residential and commuter tracks include orientations, campus tours, guest speakers, academic skills labs, and site visits, though residential students access additional evening activities and full meal service.

## Conflict Management

### Key Strategies for Mediating Student Conflicts

**Recommended Approach:**
The document advises using a **combined method** rather than extreme positions. Staff should:
- Conduct separate initial conversations with each student
- Hold a facilitated dialogue once both parties are ready
- Avoid letting conflicts fester or resolving them without direct communication between parties

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Shuttle Mediation Only**
Speaking exclusively with students separately means they never improve their own communication skills and may not reach genuine resolution.

**Mistake 2: Joint Sessions Throughout**
Conversely, keeping students together for the entire process allows dominant personalities to control outcomes.

**Mistake 3: Inaction**
Ignoring conflicts or hoping time resolves them typically backfires, as unaddressed tensions escalate.

**Mistake 4: Directive Facilitation**
Telling students what to do disempowers them and misses opportunities for skill-building.

### The LARA Method

The framework consists of four components:
- **Listen**: Practice active listening with appropriate nonverbal cues
- **Affirm**: Acknowledge feelings and underlying needs
- **Respond**: Address interests raised by the student
- **Add**: Offer information or options without imposing solutions

### "I Statements" Tool

The recommended formula: *"I feel [emotion] when [behavior] because [impact], so I'm hoping [solution]."*

This approach centers the speaker's experience rather than attacking or blaming the other party.

## Program Module

### Core Philosophy

The module emphasizes that "learning depends heavily on where, how, whom you are with and what's around you." SSB values creating summer experiences that prioritize safety, inclusion, and student respect.

### Student Experience Assessment

The program evaluates success through:
- End-of-session surveys where students rate their experiences
- Evaluation across multiple dimensions: academics, excursions, electives, residential life, and staff interactions
- Learning outcomes used to inform programming improvements for future sessions

### Staff's Critical Role

Staff members function as essential leaders who:
- Represent Summer Springboard's brand and mission
- Model professional behavior and high standards
- Drive program safety, inclusion, and fun through "strategic communication and teamwork"
- Maintain professionalism across all stakeholder interactions

The document stresses that while specific responsibilities may vary by campus and role, all staff must serve as leaders bringing "a high level of professionalism, enthusiasm, and attention to safety throughout the whole program day."
