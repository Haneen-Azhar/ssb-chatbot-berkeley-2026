# De-Escalation Techniques and Communication Skills

## Core Principles: The 3 C's

### Care
"I care about the situation, its urgency, and your wellbeing. It's my top priority."

### Compassion & Empathy
Understand the perspective of someone in a foreign area without family. Put yourself in their shoes.

### Control
Clearly communicate that you have resources and can provide help. Students/parents/staff need to know you're capable of handling the situation.

## Engagement Strategies Framework

### Four-Phase Approach

| Phase | Action Item | Leader's Objective |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Listen** | Active Silence | Understand the root emotion before reacting |
| **Bridge** | Validate Impact | Acknowledge how the student/parent feels without conceding policy |
| **Pivot** | Redirect to Values | Move the conversation back to the program's inclusion mission |
| **Repair** | Actionable Steps | Define exactly what happens next to restore safety |

## De-Escalation with Students

### When a Student is Upset

**Initial Response:**
- Stay calm and present
- Move to a private space if possible
- Use active listening (80/20 rule)
- Don't interrupt - let them express their feelings

**Conversation Starters:**
- "Tell me more about what led you to that conclusion?"
- "Help me understand what you're experiencing right now"
- "What would make this situation better for you?"
- "I hear that you're feeling [emotion]. That makes sense given [situation]."

### When a Student is Angry or Agitated

**Stay Curious:**
- "Tell me more about what led you to that conclusion?"
- Ask open-ended questions to understand the root cause
- Avoid defensive responses

**Remind of Group Norms:**
- Reference the community agreement they signed
- "We all agreed to treat each other with respect"
- "Let's take a moment to reset"

**Treat as Learning Moments:**
- Mistakes are pivots for educational growth, not just discipline
- Focus on the "why" behind behavior, not just the "what"

### Managing Panic Attacks or Emotional Crisis

**Priorities:**
1. Stay calm yourself (your energy affects theirs)
2. Support student privately
3. Maintain supervision coverage for other students
4. Assess need for escalation
5. Communicate clearly and calmly

**What to Say:**
- "You're safe. I'm here with you."
- "Let's focus on your breathing together"
- "It's okay to feel this way. We'll get through this."
- Avoid: "Calm down" or "You're fine" (invalidating)

## De-Escalation with Parents

### Managing Angry Parent Calls

**Professional Neutrality:**
- Keep your voice calm and steady to de-escalate high emotions
- Never match their emotional intensity
- Take a breath before responding

**Find Shared Interest:**
- "We both want your student to have a successful, safe summer"
- "I understand your concern - your student's wellbeing is our priority too"
- Focus on common ground

**Use Policy as Shield:**
- Lean on pre-established program guidelines to maintain boundaries
- "Our policy is designed to ensure all students..."
- "For the safety of all participants, we..."

**Never Speculate or Overpromise:**
- Stick to facts you know
- "Here's what I can tell you right now..."
- "I need to gather more information before I can answer that"
- "I will follow up with you by [specific time] once I have more details"

**The Pivot to Shared Values Technique:**
- Focus on the goal of preparing students for a diverse global workforce
- Emphasize exposure to multiple perspectives as educational value
- "Part of this experience is learning to navigate different viewpoints"

## De-Escalation with Staff

### When a Staff Member is Struggling

**Check In Privately and Supportively:**
- "I noticed you seem [observation]. How are you doing?"
- "What support do you need right now?"
- Create space for honest conversation

**Assess Burnout:**
- Are they getting enough rest?
- Are they overwhelmed with responsibilities?
- Do they need schedule adjustments?

**Problem-Solve Together:**
- "What would help make this more manageable?"
- Brainstorm workload/support strategies together
- Don't just listen - take action

**When to Escalate:**
- If performance or student care may be impacted
- If they express inability to continue
- If safety concerns arise

### Managing Staff Conflicts

**Ten Minutes Before Programming - Staff Member Breakdown:**

**What to Do FIRST:**
1. Support the staff member calmly
2. Maintain student supervision (reassign coverage immediately)
3. Assess immediate safety/functionality
4. Reassign coverage as needed
5. Escalate to CD/PD appropriately

**Key Principle:** Supporting your team is part of protecting the student experience.

## Difficult Conversation Scripts

### Addressing Microaggressions

**Student to Student:**
When you overhear: "Wow, your hair is softer than I thought"

**Response:**
- Pull student aside privately
- "I heard you say [quote]. Can you tell me what you meant by that?"
- "Impact matters more than intent. Even if you didn't mean harm, that comment can make someone feel [effect]"
- "How might you approach that differently next time?"

### Addressing Inappropriate Behavior

**When a student refers to another student as 'ADD' to mock their energy:**

**Response:**
- Address immediately but calmly
- "That's not appropriate here. We don't use medical conditions or diagnoses as insults"
- "Let's create space where everyone feels safe and respected"
- Explain why it's harmful: "Using 'ADD' as a joke minimizes what people with ADHD actually experience"

### Addressing Boundary Violations

**When students are in wrong rooms or breaking curfew:**

**Response:**
- State the policy clearly
- "You know the rule about being in other students' rooms after bed checks"
- Give them a chance to explain, but hold firm on consequences
- "I understand you were trying to support your friend, but the policy exists for everyone's safety"
- Explain next steps clearly

## Responding to Divisive Topics

### Safety Through Dialogue Philosophy

**Risk Appetite vs. Tolerance:**
- High "risk appetite" = lean into tough topics to prevent them from becoming toxic undercurrents
- Low "risk tolerance" = zero tolerance for exclusion or harassment
- Tolerate the discomfort of growth while maintaining a no-tolerance policy for harm

**Three Key Questions:**

1. **Avoid Divisiveness:** Is this crucial to student safety or merely an external political debate?
2. **Uphold Integrity:** You don't need to agree with everyone to respect them
3. **Safety Anchor:** "How does this discussion affect the safety and belonging of our community?"

### Handling Political or Sensitive Topics

**The Strategy:**
- Remain neutral as a staff member
- Don't impose your values on the dialogue
- Redirect to program values when needed

**Quick Phrases:**
- "That comment isn't appropriate here"
- "Let's create space where everyone feels safe"
- "That's not something we joke about"
- "I'm not going to engage with that topic, but I'm happy to talk about [redirect to program values]"

## Impact vs. Intent Framework

### Key Principle
In leadership, **Impact trumps Intent**. A student might not intend to be exclusionary, but the impact on the peer group creates an unsafe environment.

### How to Address

**Leader Response:**
"I appreciate that your intent was not to harm, but we must address the impact this has had on our group dynamic."

**Focus On:**
- How the behavior affected others
- What the community agreement says
- How to repair and move forward

**Avoid:**
- Dismissing because "they didn't mean it"
- Only focusing on intent
- Ignoring impact on other students

## When a Student Doesn't Feel Safe Because of Another Student's Identity

**Example:** "A parent says their child 'doesn't feel safe' because of another student's identity"

### Response Strategy

**Professional Neutrality:**
- Keep voice calm and steady
- Don't engage with bias or prejudice

**Shared Interest:**
- "We both want your student to have a successful, safe summer"

**Policy as Shield:**
- "Our program is designed to be inclusive of all students"
- "We do not make rooming or programming decisions based on other students' identities"
- "All students have agreed to our code of conduct which includes respecting all identities"

**Pivot to Shared Values:**
- "Part of preparing for college and the workforce is learning to work with people from all backgrounds"
- "Exposure to diversity is part of the educational value of this program"

## Crisis De-Escalation

### Missing Student Scenario

**What to Do FIRST:**
1. Stay calm (panic spreads)
2. Confirm information quickly (who saw them last, when, where)
3. Escalate immediately to CD/PD
4. Organize response roles (who searches where)
5. Prioritize student safety over everything else

**Key Principle:** In emergency situations, calm organization is more important than speed without structure.

### Medical Emergency

**Priorities:**
1. Secure the scene
2. Call for or seek emergency support needs
3. Ensure human safety first
4. Stay within scope of practice (don't exceed your training)

### Student Panic or Distress

**Response:**
- Support student privately
- Stay calm and present
- Assess need for medical/mental health support
- Never minimize their feelings
- Document and communicate to leadership

## Documentation and Communication

### Why It Matters
- Emotional parents need calm leadership
- Staff professionalism helps de-escalate situations
- Documentation protects everyone
- Creates record for patterns and follow-up

### What to Document
- Exact time and date of incident
- Who was involved
- What was said (use quotes when possible)
- What actions were taken
- Who was notified
- Follow-up needed

### Objective Tone
- State facts, not opinions
- "Student was shouting" NOT "Student was crazy"
- "Student appeared upset, with tears visible" NOT "Student was being dramatic"

## Self-Care for De-Escalation Work

### Leadership Checklist

**Self-Check:**
- Are my personal biases influencing my reaction to this "divisive" topic?

**Impact Review:**
- Who was affected by this interaction, and how can we repair it?

**Support:**
- Debrief with your leadership peers to maintain your own emotional resilience
- Don't carry everything alone
- Process difficult situations with your team

### Remember
You can't pour from an empty cup. Taking care of yourself allows you to take care of your students and staff effectively.
