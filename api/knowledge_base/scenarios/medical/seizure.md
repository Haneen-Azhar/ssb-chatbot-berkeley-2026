# Seizure - Medical Emergency

**Tags:** seizure, epilepsy, convulsion, shaking, jerking, unconscious, tonic-clonic, absence seizure, medical emergency, fitting, spasm, passed out, unresponsive, twitching, foaming at mouth, lost consciousness

## Recognition Triggers

A seizure is abnormal electrical activity in the brain that can cause changes in behavior, movements, feelings, and consciousness. Students with seizure disorders typically have a Physical Health POA on file.

### Types of Seizures

**Generalized Tonic-Clonic (Grand Mal) - Most Recognizable:**
- Loss of consciousness
- Body stiffening (tonic phase)
- Jerking movements (clonic phase)
- May cry out
- May lose bladder/bowel control
- Confusion after seizure ends

**Absence Seizure (Petit Mal):**
- Brief loss of awareness (seconds)
- Staring blankly
- May have subtle movements (eye blinking, lip smacking)
- Suddenly stops activity then resumes
- No memory of episode

**Focal (Partial) Seizure:**
- Remains conscious or partially aware
- Unusual movements in one body part
- Strange sensations (tingling, smells, tastes)
- Emotional changes
- Confusion or inability to respond

**Complex Focal Seizure:**
- Altered consciousness
- Repetitive movements (picking, chewing, walking)
- Appears awake but not responsive
- Confusion during and after

### Warning Signs (Aura):
Some students experience warning signs before seizure:
- Strange smell or taste
- Visual changes
- Déjà vu feeling
- Anxiety or fear
- Tingling sensation
- Student may say "I feel strange" or "It's happening"

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)

**Call 911 if:**
- First-time seizure with no known seizure disorder
- Seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes
- Second seizure follows immediately after first
- Student injured during seizure
- Seizure occurs in water
- Student has difficulty breathing after seizure
- Student doesn't regain consciousness after seizure ends
- Student is pregnant
- Student has diabetes or other medical condition
- You're unsure - when in doubt, call 911

### URGENT (Follow POA + Call SSB Helpline)

**Call SSB Helpline after seizure if:**
- Student has known seizure disorder
- Seizure lasted less than 5 minutes
- Student recovering normally per their POA
- Need guidance on return to activities
- Parents need to be notified
- Medication dosage question

### NON-URGENT (Monitor + Document)

**For students with controlled seizure disorders:**
- No seizure occurred
- Student feeling normal
- Taking medication as prescribed
- Regular health monitoring

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response - DURING SEIZURE (First 60 seconds)

**YOUR PRIMARY GOALS:**
1. Keep student safe
2. Time the seizure
3. Observe what happens
4. Stay calm

**Seconds 1-10:**
- Note the time (when seizure started)
- Stay with the student
- Call for help (have someone get CD and call 911 if needed)
- Do NOT restrain or hold student down

**Seconds 11-30:**
- Clear area of hard or sharp objects
- Cushion student's head (jacket, towel, your hands)
- Turn student on their side if possible (recovery position)
- Loosen tight clothing around neck

**Seconds 31-60:**
- DO NOT put anything in student's mouth
- DO NOT give food, water, or medication during seizure
- Keep observing and timing
- Speak calmly to student even if they seem unconscious
- Keep others calm and give student privacy

### Step 2: Medical Intervention

**During Seizure (Continuing):**

**Safety Measures:**
- Protect head from hitting ground
- Remove glasses if student wearing them
- Remove or loosen restrictive clothing
- Move furniture or objects away
- If student wearing backpack, remove it gently
- Shield from onlookers (ask others to step back)

**DO NOT:**
- Put anything in mouth (no water, no spoon, nothing)
- Try to stop movements or hold student down
- Move student unless in immediate danger
- Give oral medication during seizure

**Observe and Document:**
- What time did seizure start?
- What was student doing before seizure?
- Did student have warning (aura)?
- What body parts were involved?
- What type of movements?
- Eyes open or closed? Direction of gaze?
- Loss of bladder/bowel control?
- Breathing pattern
- Skin color changes
- Duration of seizure

**After Seizure Ends:**

**Immediate Post-Seizure (0-5 minutes):**
- Check for breathing (should resume normally)
- Keep student on their side (recovery position)
- Check for injuries
- Stay with student - DO NOT leave alone
- Speak calmly and reassuringly
- Student will likely be confused - this is normal
- Provide privacy and comfort

**Recovery Phase (5-30 minutes):**
- Student may be confused, sleepy, or agitated
- Allow student to rest
- Do not give food/drink until fully awake and alert
- Monitor breathing and consciousness
- Keep student comfortable and calm
- Answer questions (student may ask same questions repeatedly)
- Reassure that they are safe

**Emergency Medication (Rescue Medication):**
- Some students have rescue medication prescribed (like Diastat/rectal diazepam or nasal midazolam)
- Check student's POA for specific instructions
- Typically given if seizure lasts > 5 minutes
- Follow exact administration instructions in POA
- Call 911 before or immediately after giving rescue medication
- Only staff trained in administration should give rescue meds

### Step 3: Emergency Services

**Who to Call:**

**CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY if:**
- First seizure ever (no known seizure disorder)
- Seizure > 5 minutes
- Multiple seizures without recovery in between
- Injury during seizure
- Breathing problems after seizure
- Student unconscious 10+ minutes after seizure

**DO NOT CALL 911 if:**
- Student has known seizure disorder
- Seizure < 5 minutes
- Student recovering normally per POA
- INSTEAD: Call SSB Helpline for guidance

**Other Contacts:**
1. **SSB Helpline (24/7)** - (888) 682-6671
2. **Campus Director** - Immediately
3. **Student's Emergency Contact** - After student safe and stable
4. **Student's Neurologist** - If number in POA for questions

**What to Say to 911:**
"We need an ambulance at [exact location]. We have a [age]-year-old who [had their first seizure / has a seizure disorder] and just had a seizure. The seizure [is still happening / lasted X minutes / ended at time]. Student is [conscious/unconscious]. [Any injuries observed]. Student takes [medication name from POA]. We need immediate medical assistance."

**Information to Have Ready:**
- Student's name and age
- Known seizure disorder? (check POA)
- Seizure start and end time
- Type of seizure observed
- Any injuries
- Current consciousness level
- Medications student takes (from POA)
- Student's neurologist contact (if in POA)
- Recent illness or changes
- Allergies

### Step 4: Documentation

**Critical Information to Record:**

**During Seizure:**
- Start time (exact)
- End time (exact)
- Total duration
- Location where it occurred
- Activity before seizure
- Warning signs student mentioned (if any)

**Seizure Characteristics:**
- Type of movements (jerking, stiffening, subtle)
- Body parts involved (whole body, one side, just face)
- Eyes (open/closed, direction of gaze)
- Consciousness level
- Sounds made (crying out, etc.)
- Color changes (pale, blue lips, flushed)
- Breathing pattern
- Loss of bladder/bowel control

**After Seizure:**
- How long until student responsive
- Confusion level
- Any injuries discovered
- Student's own description of how they feel
- How long until back to normal

**Incident Report:**
- Required for ALL seizures
- Include complete timeline
- Attach observations from multiple witnesses if available
- Document all communications
- Note adherence to or deviation from POA
- Submit to SSB HQ same day

### Step 5: Follow-Up

**0-2 Hours (Immediate Recovery):**
- Student rests in quiet, safe place
- Staff supervision at all times
- No activities requiring alertness (swimming, heights, machines)
- Small sips of water once fully alert
- Light food if student wants and fully alert
- Parents notified and updated
- Medical evaluation if 911 called

**2-24 Hours (Extended Recovery):**
- Modified schedule per POA or doctor recommendation
- Avoid triggers (flashing lights, excessive screen time, sleep deprivation)
- Ensure medication taken as prescribed
- Extra rest time
- Buddy system for all activities
- No swimming or potentially dangerous activities
- Mentor frequent check-ins
- Monitor for additional seizures

**24+ Hours (Return to Normal):**
- Health Check with student and mentor
- Review what may have triggered seizure
- Medication compliance check
- Sleep and stress assessment
- Return to full activities if cleared
- Updated POA if needed
- Ongoing support and monitoring

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### During Seizure (to bystanders):
"Everyone please step back and give [student name] space and privacy. They are having a seizure and they will be okay. I need someone to call [CD name] and tell them to come to [location] immediately. Everyone else, please continue to [other room/activity]."

### After Seizure (to student):
"[Name], you're okay. You're safe. You had a seizure but it's over now. I'm [your name], I'm staying right here with you. You're at [location]. You're going to feel sleepy/confused for a little while and that's normal. Just rest. You're safe."

### To Student (if they keep asking same questions):
Patiently answer each time, even if repetitive:
"You had a seizure. It's over now. You're okay. You're at [location]. I'm [name] from your Summer Springboard program. You're safe. Your parents have been called."

### To 911 Operator:
"We need an ambulance at [specific location]. A 16-year-old student just had a seizure lasting 6 minutes. The seizure has stopped but the student is still unconscious. The seizure started at 2:47 PM and ended at 2:53 PM. We are with the student now providing first aid. The student has a known seizure disorder and takes Keppra. No visible injuries."

### To Parent (after student stable):
"This is [your name] from Summer Springboard. I'm calling to let you know that [student name] had a seizure today at [time]. The seizure lasted [duration]. We followed [his/her] Plan of Action. [Student name] is now resting comfortably and [is/is not] back to normal. [We did/did not] call 911. [If 911: Student was transported to [hospital] and we are there now.] [Student name] is safe and being monitored. What questions do you have?"

### To Parent (if 911 called):
"This is [your name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] had a seizure at [time] that lasted [duration]. We called 911 because [reason per protocol]. [Student name] is being transported to [hospital name] by ambulance. I am going with [him/her]. [Student name] is [current status]. Can you meet us at the hospital? I will call you with updates every [timeframe]."

### To Student Next Day:
"How are you feeling today? I want to check in about yesterday. Do you remember what happened? [Listen] That must have been [scary/frustrating/etc.]. You handled it really well. Let's go over your POA together to make sure we're all doing everything we can to prevent this from happening again. What do you need from me and the team?"

## What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Put anything in student's mouth (fingers, spoon, wallet, etc.)
- Try to stop the seizure or hold student still
- Move student unless in immediate danger (fire, water, etc.)
- Give food, water, or medication during seizure
- Slap or shake student to "wake them up"
- Throw water on student's face
- Perform CPR during seizure (unless student not breathing after seizure ends)
- Crowd around student (give privacy)
- Take photos or videos (protect privacy)
- Leave student alone during or immediately after seizure
- Let student drive, swim, or operate equipment until fully recovered and cleared

**COMMON MYTHS:**
- ❌ "Put something in their mouth so they don't swallow their tongue" - FALSE. You cannot swallow your tongue. Putting objects in mouth can cause injury.
- ❌ "Hold them down to stop the seizure" - FALSE. You cannot stop a seizure. Restraining can cause injury.
- ❌ "Seizures always mean epilepsy" - FALSE. Single seizures can have many causes.
- ❌ "You can die from a seizure" - Rare, but prolonged seizures are dangerous. That's why we call 911 for seizures > 5 minutes.

**COMMON MISTAKES:**
- Not timing the seizure (critical information)
- Moving student too soon
- Giving medication too soon
- Not checking for injuries after
- Letting student resume activities before fully recovered
- Not documenting details immediately (memory fades)
- Embarrassing student later by discussing publicly
- Treating student differently after seizure

## Medical Information Needed

**Before Program (Check-In Day):**
- Student's Seizure POA (Physical Health POA)
- Type of seizure disorder (epilepsy, other)
- Seizure triggers (flashing lights, stress, sleep deprivation, illness)
- Typical seizure type and duration
- Frequency of seizures
- Last seizure date
- Medications and dosing schedule
- Rescue medication protocol (if applicable)
- When to call 911 (may differ from standard protocol)
- Post-seizure recovery pattern
- Activity restrictions if any
- Neurologist contact information

**Plan of Action Should Include:**
- Seizure type(s) student experiences
- Warning signs (aura) if any
- What to do during seizure
- How long seizures typically last
- When to call 911 (specific to this student)
- Rescue medication protocol
  - When to give
  - How to give
  - Who is trained
- Post-seizure care instructions
- How long until student can return to activities
- Triggers to avoid
- Medication schedule
- Who to notify
- Emergency contacts

**Required Accommodations:**
- Medication storage (may need refrigeration)
- Medication reminders if needed
- Avoid known triggers (seizure disorder may be photosensitive - avoid strobe lights, rapid flashing)
- Sleep schedule consistency (sleep deprivation can trigger seizures)
- Stress management support
- Supervision for swimming or heights if recommended
- Private space for post-seizure recovery

**Health Check Requirements:**
- CD and Mentor conduct Health Check on check-in day
- Review seizure POA in detail
- Discuss student's comfort with disclosing to peers
- Confirm medication schedule and reminders
- Identify triggers to avoid
- Plan for what to do if seizure occurs during specific activities
- Ensure student feels supported and not restricted unnecessarily
- Student teaches staff about their specific seizure pattern

**Required Staff Training:**
- All staff should know seizure first aid basics
- Staff working closely with student with seizure disorder MUST review POA
- Practice rescue medication administration if applicable
- Know when to call 911
- Understand post-seizure care
- Emotional support for student after seizure

## After-Action Procedures

### 0-2 Hours (Immediate Post-Seizure):

**Student Care:**
- Rest in quiet, low-stimulation environment
- Side-lying position until fully alert
- Staff supervision constantly
- Water and light snack once fully alert
- No activities requiring alertness
- Bathroom assistance if needed (may be unsteady)
- Emotional support and reassurance

**If 911 Called:**
- Staff accompanies to hospital
- Bring student's medical info, POA, insurance card, medications
- Stay until parent arrives or hospital discharges
- Get written discharge instructions
- Doctor's note for return to program
- Coordinate transportation back to campus

**If 911 Not Called:**
- Parents notified
- SSB Helpline contacted for guidance
- Student monitored continuously
- IR submitted
- CD determines modified schedule

**Staff Actions:**
- Document everything immediately (details fade)
- Collect observations from witnesses
- Photograph any injuries (not student's face)
- Secure any rescue medication used for replacement
- Prepare IR
- Brief team on student's status

### 2-24 Hours (Recovery & Monitoring):

**Student Support:**
- Modified schedule - no high-risk activities
- Extra rest periods
- Medication adherence checked
- Adequate sleep (sleep deprivation is common trigger)
- Avoid seizure triggers (flashing lights, stress, screens)
- Buddy system for all activities
- Frequent mentor check-ins
- Privacy respected - student chooses disclosure to peers

**Medical:**
- Check with parents about medication
- Any dose adjustments needed?
- When can student return to full activities?
- Replacement rescue medication if used

**Emotional:**
- Student may feel embarrassed, frustrated, scared
- Normalize feelings
- Peer education if student wants (demystify seizures)
- Maintain normal expectations where safe
- Don't over-restrict or treat differently

**Administrative:**
- Complete Incident Report with all details
- Parent communication documented
- Team briefed
- Next shift informed
- SSB HQ updated

### 24+ Hours (Return to Activities & Prevention):

**With Student:**
- Comprehensive health check
- How are you feeling?
- Any injury pain or lingering confusion?
- What might have triggered this seizure?
  - Missed medication?
  - Poor sleep?
  - Stress?
  - Illness?
  - Flashing lights?
- What can we do to prevent next time?
- Are you comfortable with your POA or does it need updates?
- What do you need from the team?

**With Parents:**
- Follow-up call
- Student's recovery status
- Discuss any concerns
- POA adjustments needed?
- Neurologist follow-up planned?
- Comfort level with student continuing on program

**With Staff:**
- Team debrief
- What happened?
- How did we respond?
- What worked well?
- What would we do differently?
- Was POA helpful and clear?
- Any training gaps?
- Recognition for calm, effective response

**Program Adjustments:**
- Trigger avoidance (e.g., if photosensitive, avoid strobe lights at evening activities)
- Sleep schedule enforcement
- Medication reminder system if helpful
- Stress reduction support
- Activity modifications only if medically necessary
- POA updates implemented
- Enhanced communication with parents

**Pattern Monitoring:**
- If seizures more frequent than usual, investigate
- Medication compliance
- Sleep quality
- Stress level
- Illness or hormonal changes
- Contact neurologist with parent
- Adjust POA as needed
- Consider if program environment sustainable for student

### Debrief Questions (With Student):

1. How are you feeling today - physically and emotionally?
2. Do you remember what happened before the seizure?
3. Did you have any warning signs?
4. What do you think might have triggered it?
5. Is there anything you want to tell me about how you felt during recovery?
6. What can I and the team do to support you?
7. Do you want to talk to your friends about what happened, or would you prefer privacy?
8. Are you comfortable continuing on the program?

### Debrief Questions (With Staff):

1. Who first noticed the seizure?
2. How long did it take to secure the area?
3. Did we have the student's POA readily accessible?
4. Was the decision to call/not call 911 appropriate?
5. How did we handle bystanders and privacy?
6. What worked well in our response?
7. What was challenging?
8. Do we need additional training or resources?
9. How are staff feeling? Any need for support?

## Key Policy Reminders

**Seizure First Aid Basics:**
- Stay calm, stay with student, time seizure
- Protect from injury, cushion head, turn on side
- NOTHING in mouth
- Call 911 if > 5 minutes or first-time seizure
- Recovery position after seizure
- Monitor breathing and consciousness

**Student Privacy:**
- Seizure disorders are medical conditions, not defining characteristics
- Don't discuss student's seizure publicly
- Student controls disclosure to peers
- Protect dignity during and after seizure
- Don't treat student differently because of seizure disorder

**Medication Management:**
- Student manages own medication
- Staff may provide reminders if in POA
- Rescue medication only in emergencies per POA
- Only trained staff give rescue medication
- Never skip or alter medication without parent/doctor approval

**Activity Participation:**
- Students with seizure disorders can participate fully in most activities
- Restrictions only if specifically in POA or doctor recommendation
- Common sense safety (buddy for swimming, supervision for heights)
- Don't over-restrict out of fear
- Balance safety with normal experience

**Documentation:**
- IR required for all seizures
- Document objectively, factually
- Include timeline, observations, interventions
- Parent communication noted
- Follow-up plan documented

**Communication:**
- Parents notified after every seizure
- SSB HQ informed
- Medical consultation if questions
- Team briefed as appropriate
- Student's privacy protected

## Medical References

**Seizure Disorder Statistics:**
- POA type: Physical Health POA - Seizure
- Students with seizure disorders have individualized POAs
- Common types: epilepsy (various types), seizure disorders from other causes
- Most are well-controlled with medication
- Seizures on program are typically due to: medication non-compliance, sleep deprivation, stress, illness, or breakthrough despite treatment

**SSB Resources:**
- Student POA Folder (Summer Staff Portal)
- Health Check protocol for check-in day
- Incident Reporting system
- 24/7 SSB Helpline support
- Medical Director consultation via SSB HQ

**When Seizures Are Medical Emergencies:**
- Status epilepticus: seizure > 5 minutes or repeated seizures without recovery
- Can cause brain damage or death
- Requires immediate 911 and often ICU care
- This is why timing is critical

**Common Seizure Medications (students may take):**
- Keppra (levetiracetam)
- Lamictal (lamotrigine)
- Depakote (valproic acid)
- Tegretol (carbamazepine)
- Dilantin (phenytoin)
- Topamax (topiramate)
- Others - see student's POA

**Rescue Medications (emergency use):**
- Diastat (rectal diazepam)
- Nasal midazolam
- Given for prolonged seizures per POA
- Staff training required
- Call 911 when giving rescue medication

**Important Notes:**
- Most seizures stop on their own in < 2 minutes
- Student cannot control or stop a seizure
- Seizures are not usually painful (student unconscious)
- Confusion and tiredness after seizure are normal
- Students with seizure disorders can live full, active lives
- Having a seizure disorder doesn't mean you have constant seizures - many are well-controlled
