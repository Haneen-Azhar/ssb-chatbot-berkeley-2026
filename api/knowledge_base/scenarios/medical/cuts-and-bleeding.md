# Cuts and Bleeding - Medical Emergency

**Tags:** cut, cuts, bleeding, blood, wound, laceration, gash, scrape, injury, bandage, stitches, pressure, hemorrhage, medical emergency, first aid

## Recognition Triggers

Cuts and bleeding injuries range from minor scrapes to severe lacerations requiring emergency care. Proper assessment and response prevent complications like infection or excessive blood loss.

### Types of Bleeding

**Minor Bleeding:**
- Small cuts, scrapes, or abrasions
- Bleeding stops with light pressure in 5-10 minutes
- Shallow wound
- Clean edges
- No signs of deep tissue damage

**Moderate Bleeding:**
- Deeper cut
- Bleeding slows but doesn't stop easily
- May need pressure for 10-20 minutes
- Wound edges may gape open
- May need medical glue or butterfly bandages
- May need stitches

**Severe Bleeding:**
- Deep laceration
- Blood spurting or flowing continuously
- Bleeding doesn't slow with pressure
- Large or deep wound
- Visible muscle, fat, or bone
- Foreign object embedded in wound
- Caused by puncture (stab, impalement)

### Location Matters

**Head/Face Wounds:**
- Bleed heavily even if minor (lots of blood vessels)
- May look worse than they are
- Scalp wounds bleed profusely
- Face wounds may need stitches for cosmetic reasons
- Potential concussion if trauma involved

**Hand/Finger Wounds:**
- Risk of tendon or nerve damage
- May affect function if deep
- Joints or creases need evaluation

**Abdominal/Chest Wounds:**
- Always serious
- Internal injury possible
- Call 911

## Severity Assessment

### IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Call 9-1-1)

**Call 911 if:**
- Blood spurting from wound (arterial bleeding)
- Bleeding doesn't stop after 10 minutes of direct pressure
- Blood soaking through multiple bandages
- Deep laceration (can see muscle, fat, or bone)
- Puncture wound to chest, abdomen, head, or neck
- Foreign object embedded in wound (don't remove)
- Amputation or near-amputation
- Signs of shock (pale, sweaty, weak, rapid pulse, confusion)
- Head injury with bleeding and altered consciousness
- Bleeding from fall > 3-4 feet or high-impact trauma
- Animal bite (especially wild animal or large dog)
- Human bite that breaks skin

### URGENT (First Aid + Urgent Care Same Day)

**Urgent care visit needed if:**
- Wound longer than 1/2 inch
- Wound edges gaping open
- Deep cut (even if bleeding controlled)
- Cut on face (may need stitches for minimal scarring)
- Cut over a joint
- Cut on hand possibly involving tendons
- Cannot clean wound thoroughly (dirt, debris embedded)
- Caused by dirty object (rusty metal, soil, etc.)
- Tetanus shot needed (unsure of vaccination status)
- Possible infection (redness, warmth, pus, red streaks)
- Numbness or tingling around wound (nerve damage?)

### NON-URGENT (First Aid + Monitor)

**Treat with first aid kit if:**
- Small, shallow cut
- Bleeding stops with pressure in < 10 minutes
- Wound edges come together naturally
- Clean wound
- No signs of deep damage
- Scrapes and abrasions
- Paper cuts, minor kitchen cuts

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Immediate Response (First 60 seconds)

**Seconds 1-10:**
- Assess scene safety (broken glass, sharp objects, ongoing danger)
- Put on gloves (universal precautions for blood exposure)
- Assess student for consciousness and breathing
- Note mechanism of injury (how did it happen?)

**Seconds 11-30:**
- Apply direct pressure to wound with clean cloth or gauze
- Elevate injured area above heart if possible
- Keep student calm and seated/lying down
- Have someone get first aid kit

**Seconds 31-60:**
- Continue firm, steady pressure
- Do NOT lift bandage to check (releases clot)
- Add more bandages on top if bleeding through
- Assess for shock (pale, sweaty, weak pulse)
- Call for additional help if needed

### Step 2: Medical Intervention

**Bleeding Control:**

**Step 1: Apply Direct Pressure**
- Use clean gauze, cloth, or even your gloved hand
- Press firmly directly on wound
- Don't let go for at least 5-10 minutes (don't peek!)
- If blood soaks through, add more bandages ON TOP
- Don't remove first bandage (disrupts clotting)

**Step 2: Elevate**
- If arm or leg injury, raise above heart level
- Helps reduce blood flow to area
- Continue pressure while elevating

**Step 3: Pressure Points (if severe bleeding continues)**
- For arm: pressure on brachial artery (inner upper arm)
- For leg: pressure on femoral artery (groin)
- Use only if direct pressure not working
- This is advanced - call 911 first

**Wound Cleaning (For Minor Wounds Only):**

**After Bleeding Stops:**
1. Wash your hands and put on fresh gloves
2. Rinse wound with clean running water for 5 minutes
3. Gently clean around wound with soap (not in wound)
4. Remove small debris with clean tweezers
5. Pat dry with clean gauze
6. Apply antibiotic ointment if no allergy
7. Cover with adhesive bandage or sterile dressing

**DO NOT Clean:**
- Wounds that need stitches (will be cleaned at hospital/urgent care)
- Puncture wounds (need medical evaluation)
- Deep wounds (risk of pushing bacteria deeper)

**Bandaging:**

**For Minor Cuts:**
- Adhesive bandage (Band-Aid)
- Change daily or if wet/dirty
- Keep clean and dry

**For Larger Wounds:**
- Non-stick gauze pad over wound
- Wrap with rolled gauze or tape to secure
- Snug but not tight (should not cut off circulation)
- Change bandage daily
- Watch for signs of infection

**Special Situations:**

**Nosebleed:**
- Sit upright, lean slightly forward (not back!)
- Pinch soft part of nose for 10 minutes continuously
- Apply ice to bridge of nose
- Don't blow nose after
- If continues > 20 minutes, call for medical help

**Embedded Object:**
- DO NOT REMOVE object
- Stabilize object with bulky dressings around it
- Control bleeding around object with pressure
- Call 911
- Keep student still

**Amputated Part:**
- Control bleeding from stump with pressure and elevation
- Call 911 immediately
- Rinse amputated part with clean water (don't scrub)
- Wrap in clean, moist cloth
- Place in sealed plastic bag
- Place bag on ice (don't put part directly on ice)
- Transport to hospital with student

### Step 3: Emergency Services

**Who to Call:**

**CALL 911 for:**
- Severe bleeding not controlled with pressure
- Deep lacerations
- Puncture wounds to torso, head, neck
- Embedded objects
- Amputation
- Signs of shock
- Bleeding with head injury and altered consciousness

**CALL SSB Helpline:**
- For urgent care coordination
- Moderate wounds needing evaluation
- Need transportation to urgent care
- Parent notification support
- Insurance questions

**What to Say to 911:**
"We need an ambulance at [location]. We have a [age]-year-old with [severe bleeding from arm/deep laceration/etc.]. We are applying pressure but bleeding is not stopping. [He/she] [is/is not] conscious and alert. Injury occurred at [time] from [mechanism - fell on glass/cut with knife/etc.]. We need immediate help."

**Information to Have Ready:**
- How injury occurred
- When injury occurred
- Student's tetanus vaccination status (if known from medical forms)
- Allergies (medication allergies important)
- Any bleeding disorders or medications that affect clotting
- Current student status (conscious, alert, in shock, etc.)

### Step 4: Documentation

**Immediate Documentation:**
- Time of injury
- How injury occurred (detailed description)
- Location on body (be specific)
- Extent of bleeding (minor, moderate, severe)
- First aid provided (pressure, bandaging, etc.)
- Time bleeding stopped
- Who provided care
- Who was present

**Incident Report:**
- Required for any injury requiring urgent care or ER
- Required for significant bleeding even if only first aid
- Include mechanism of injury
- Document all care provided
- Parent communication
- Photos of scene if relevant (broken glass, etc.) - not of student's injury

**Photo Documentation:**
- Photo of scene that caused injury (broken equipment, hazard)
- DO NOT photograph student's open wound (privacy concern)
- May photograph bandaged wound if relevant for documentation

### Step 5: Follow-Up

**0-2 Hours (Immediate Care):**
- If urgent care/ER: Staff accompanies student
- Bring insurance card, medical info, parent contact
- Get discharge instructions in writing
- Obtain wound care instructions
- Get prescription if antibiotics ordered
- Coordinate return transportation

**Wound Care Instructions Typically Include:**
- How often to change bandage
- How to clean wound
- When stitches/staples removed (usually 7-14 days)
- Signs of infection to watch for
- Activity restrictions (keep dry, no swimming, etc.)
- Follow-up appointment timing

**2-24 Hours (Monitoring Phase):**
- Check wound per medical instructions
- Watch for signs of infection
- Ensure pain managed (Tylenol/ibuprofen if allowed)
- Activity modifications per doctor
- Keep wound clean and dry
- Bandage changes as instructed

**24+ Hours (Ongoing Care):**
- Daily wound checks
- Follow discharge instructions
- Watch for infection signs
- Coordinate stitch/staple removal if applicable
- Return to normal activities per medical clearance

## What to Say (Exact Scripts)

### During Injury:
"[Name], I can see you're bleeding. I need you to sit down right here. I'm going to put pressure on it to stop the bleeding. It might hurt but I need to press firmly. You're going to be okay. Just breathe."

### To Student in Shock:
"[Name], I need you to lie down. I'm going to elevate your legs. You're safe. Help is coming. Just focus on my voice. You're doing great."

### To 911:
"This is an emergency. We're at [location]. A 17-year-old cut his hand on broken glass and there's severe bleeding. We've been applying pressure for 10 minutes but blood is still soaking through the bandages. He's conscious but looking very pale. The injury happened at 2:30 PM. We need an ambulance immediately."

### To Parent (Urgent Care Level):
"This is [name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] cut [his/her] [body part] this afternoon. We've stopped the bleeding and cleaned the wound, but it's deep enough that [he/she] needs stitches. We're taking [him/her] to [urgent care/ER] now. [He/she] is alert and doing fine, just needs medical attention for the wound. Can you meet us there or would you like me to stay with [him/her]?"

### To Parent (Emergency - 911 Called):
"This is [name] from Summer Springboard. [Student name] has had an injury with significant bleeding. We called 911 and [he/she] is being transported to [hospital]. [He/she] is conscious. I'm going with [him/her]. The injury is [brief description]. Can you meet us at the hospital? I'll update you as soon as we know more."

### To Student After Stitches:
"The doctor did a great job. Your wound is closed up now. Here's what we need to do: Keep it clean and dry. No swimming for [X days]. We'll change the bandage [frequency]. The stitches come out in [X days]. If you notice any redness, swelling, or pus, tell me immediately. Any questions?"

## What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Remove embedded objects (stabilize and call 911)
- Use a tourniquet unless life-threatening hemorrhage (last resort)
- Peek under bandage while bleeding (disrupts clot)
- Clean wounds that need stitches (hospital will clean)
- Give aspirin (worsens bleeding) - ibuprofen okay
- Apply ointment to wounds needing stitches
- Let student shower/swim with open wound
- Ignore signs of infection
- Remove bandages too soon
- Use hydrogen peroxide or rubbing alcohol on wounds (damages tissue)

**COMMON MISTAKES:**
- Not wearing gloves (blood exposure risk)
- Lifting bandage repeatedly to "check" (prevents clotting)
- Not applying enough pressure
- Removing first layer of soaked bandage (add on top instead)
- Cleaning too vigorously (causes more bleeding)
- Using old or dirty cloth for pressure
- Not elevating injury
- Sending student to room alone after injury
- Not documenting minor injuries (can become infected later)

## Medical Information Needed

**From Student Medical Forms:**
- Tetanus vaccination status
- Last tetanus shot date (needs booster if > 5 years for dirty wound, > 10 years for clean wound)
- Allergies (especially to antibiotics, lidocaine)
- Bleeding disorders (hemophilia, von Willebrand disease, etc.)
- Medications that affect clotting (aspirin, warfarin, etc.)
- Previous scarring issues (keloid formation)

**Ask Student:**
- How did injury happen?
- When did it happen?
- Any numbness or tingling?
- Can you move [affected area]?
- Pain level?
- When was your last tetanus shot?

### Immediate Emergency Contacts:

**1. 911** - For severe bleeding, deep wounds, embedded objects, signs of shock

**2. Campus Director** - Immediately for any significant injury

**3. Another Staff Member** - To get first aid kit, assist with care, call for help

### Urgent Contacts (After Stabilized):

**4. SSB Helpline** - For urgent care coordination, transportation, guidance

**5. Parents** - After injury stabilized but before transport to medical facility if time allows

**6. Urgent Care or ER** - Call ahead if transporting (check wait times, insurance acceptance)

### Follow-Up Contacts:

**7. Campus Health Services** - For wound checks, bandage changes, stitch removal coordination

**8. Student's Doctor** - For infection concerns, healing problems, with parent permission

## After-Action Procedures

### 0-2 Hours (Immediate Care):

**If Medical Facility:**
- Staff accompanies student
- Bring insurance card, parent contact, medical info
- Stay through treatment
- Get written discharge instructions
- Understand wound care
- Get prescription filled if needed
- Return to campus

**If First Aid Only:**
- Student rests
- Monitor for pain, bleeding, shock
- Elevate if possible
- Ice for swelling (not directly on skin)
- Parents notified
- IR submitted

### 2-24 Hours (Wound Care):

**Daily Care:**
- Change bandage per instructions
- Clean as directed
- Watch for infection signs
- Pain management
- Activity modifications (keep dry, limit use, etc.)

**Signs of Infection (Call Doctor if Present):**
- Increased pain (after first 2 days)
- Redness spreading from wound
- Red streaks going up arm/leg from wound
- Warmth around wound
- Pus or cloudy drainage
- Fever
- Swelling
- Bad odor from wound

**Activity Restrictions:**
- No swimming until healed (chlorine irritation + infection risk)
- No soaking (bath, hot tub)
- Limit physical activity if on hand/foot/joint
- Keep dry per doctor's instructions
- Modified activities if needed

### 24+ Hours (Healing & Prevention):

**Ongoing Wound Care:**
- Follow discharge instructions
- May transition from bandage to leaving open to air (per doctor)
- Scar minimization (per doctor - may recommend vitamin E, silicone sheets)
- Sun protection on healing wound (can darken scar)

**Stitch/Staple Removal:**
- Typically 7-14 days depending on location
- Face: 5-7 days
- Scalp: 7-10 days
- Arms/legs: 10-14 days
- Coordinate with parents for removal (can be done at urgent care, doctor, or campus health)

**Return to Activities:**
- Per medical clearance
- Consider location (hand injury may limit writing, sports)
- Swimming only after fully healed
- Contact sports only after cleared

**Prevention Discussion:**
- How did injury happen?
- Preventable?
- Hazard that needs fixing?
- Student education (knife safety, glass handling, etc.)
- Facility review (sharp edges, broken equipment)

### Debrief Questions (With Student):

1. How are you feeling?
2. Is your pain controlled?
3. Do you understand how to care for your wound?
4. Any signs of infection we discussed?
5. Do you have questions about activity restrictions?
6. How did this injury happen?
7. What can we do to prevent this in the future?

### Debrief Questions (With Staff):

1. Was first aid kit readily accessible?
2. Did staff have gloves?
3. Was injury preventable?
4. Was scene safe?
5. Is there a hazard that needs addressing?
6. Was escalation appropriate (911 vs urgent care vs first aid)?
7. What worked well?
8. What could improve?
9. Do we need additional first aid training?

## Key Policy Reminders

**Universal Precautions:**
- Always wear gloves when handling blood/body fluids
- Treat all blood as potentially infectious
- Wash hands before and after care
- Dispose of bloody materials in sealed bag
- Clean contaminated surfaces with bleach solution

**First Aid Kit Accessibility:**
- Mentors carry first aid kits
- Leadership carries first aid kits
- Large kit in program office
- Check expiration dates on supplies
- Restock after use

**Incident Reporting:**
- IR for any injury requiring medical attention
- IR for significant bleeding even if only first aid
- IR for any injury from facility/equipment issue
- Document thoroughly
- Submit same day

**Staff Limitations:**
- Provide first aid only
- Do not diagnose
- Do not stitch or perform medical procedures
- Do not give prescription medications
- Escalate appropriately

**Parent Communication:**
- Notify for any injury requiring medical attention
- Notify even for minor injuries if significant (parent preference varies)
- Factual information
- Calm tone
- Clear next steps

## Medical References

**Tetanus Vaccination:**
- Needed for dirty wounds if last shot > 5 years ago
- Needed for clean wounds if last shot > 10 years ago
- "Dirty" = soil, rust, animal bite, deep puncture
- Students usually had tetanus booster around age 11-12 (Tdap)
- Check medical forms for vaccination history

**Stitches vs. Glue vs. Butterfly Bandages:**
- Stitches: Deep cuts, high-tension areas, wounds needing strength
- Medical glue: Shallow cuts with clean edges, low-tension areas (often used on face)
- Butterfly bandages (Steri-Strips): Small cuts with edges that align well, low tension
- Doctor decides based on wound characteristics

**When Stitches Needed:**
- Wound > 1/2 inch long
- Wound edges gaping open
- Deep wound
- Over a joint
- On face (for minimal scarring)
- Can see fat or muscle

**Wound Healing Timeline:**
- Days 1-5: Inflammation (redness, swelling normal)
- Days 5-21: New tissue formation (wound closes)
- Days 21-365: Remodeling (scar matures)
- Full healing can take a year for scar to fully mature

**Infection Risk Factors:**
- Dirty wound
- Not cleaned properly
- Foreign material in wound
- Diabetes
- Immune system problems
- Wound on foot (especially diabetes patients)
- Bite wounds (high infection risk)

**SSB Resources:**
- First aid kits (mentor, leadership, office)
- 24/7 SSB Helpline for medical guidance
- Incident Reporting system
- Urgent care partnerships
- Insurance coordination support
