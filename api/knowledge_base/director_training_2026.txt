Who is Summer Springboard?

Terra Education Travel
Started in 2004, Terra’s ﬁrst program was Global Leadership Adventures, an
international service-learning program for teens.
In 2012, the Discover Corps program was created to operate volunteer vacations
for adults, families, and university groups.
In the fall of 2016, we launched Summer Springboard.
In 2022, Beyond Sports ofﬁcially became part of the Terra Education family, after a
long standing partnership.
Terra Education is a certiﬁed Beneﬁt Corporation. B Corporations use business to create a social good.
(Others include Patagonia, 7th Generation, and Warby Parker)

Mission Statement
Our mission is to help students increase their
self-awareness so they can make decisions about
college selection, academic majors, and career
planning that reﬂect their personal vision for success.

8 Operating Principles
Our operating principles form the foundation for SSB culture and guide our behavior when we make decisions
and interact with each other.
1. Safety is always ﬁrst priority.
2. Everyone in the SSB organization is a leader.
3. Students are always watching and following our example.
4. We push ourselves outside our comfort zone.
5. Nothing goes exactly as planned.
6. We all must do things that are not in the job description.
7. We work together as a team.
8. We take our work seriously so we can have fun!

Goals for Directors Training
●

Summer Springboard aims to continue the tradition of providing
unique and compelling summer experiences for college-bound teens
as they look to grow, expand and impact their communities.

●

Our goal is to use our decades of experience in areas such as service
learning, education, international travel, media, and program
operations to create the most impactful domestic summer
enrichment programs for college-bound teens.

●

We are here at DT to learn how to deliver these enriching programs in
the safest, most fun, and most impactful way possible.

Essential Eligibility Criteria
1. Independent Self-Care Participants should be prepared to manage their personal care and medications with limited outside
independently.
2. Health & Psychological Stability To ensure that all participants can be supported safely and appropriately, students with a diagnosis or
condition that may require specific considerations may be asked to provide additional documentation or medical clearance and demonstrate
a period of independent stability for a minimum period of 12 months. This information helps guide the planning of reasonable
accommodations and assess that the program environment can provide appropriate support.
3. Physician & Guardian Support A participant’s physician may be required to confirm fitness for travel and participation. Guardians play
an important role in supporting disclosure and preparing students for the travel and group environment. *If health circumstances change
between enrollment and departure, we ask families to update us so we can reassess accommodations.
4. Openness & Adaptability Students should have a to engage with peers, adapt to cultural differences, and navigate new environments
with staff support. Respect, open communication, and a tolerance for discomfort and ambiguity are part of the learning experience.
5. Proactive Communication We encourage participants to communicate directly with SSB staff when they need clarification, support, or
adjustments. Staff are trained to respond and problem-solve collaboratively, but students must be able to take an active role in sharing their
needs.
6. Resilience to Environmental Stressors Students should expect to encounter; high heat and humidity, cold, altitude, insects, change in
diet, change in time zones, sharing spaces, roommates, going without a cell phone or other technology devices and long days with
structured schedules. While staff provide orientation and strategies to manage these stressors, participants should anticipate challenges and
be prepared to cope with them without continuous external support
7. Physical Abilities To participate fully in the program, students should be capable of engaging in program activities that may involve
walking distances greater than one mile, as the campus-based nature of the program involves moving between buildings and classes.
8. Instructional Abilities, Situational Awareness & Judgment Students should be able to understand and follow staff instructions
communicated in English, remain aware of their surroundings, and make responsible decisions. While staff provide supervision and
guidance, students share responsibilities in maintaining their own safety and contributing to the wellbeing of the group.
9. Independent Travel to/from Departure Point Participants must be able to arrange (or manage) travel between home and the destination
airport. (flights, arrival, etc. – unaccompanied minor services may be available with the airline)

Facilitating a Mentor Group - Facilitator
Questions
Following up on the scenario, it’s time to test mentors to get a sense of their thought
process during different situations.
Q: You have read the medical information for the students in your mentor group. You
know that one student has ASD (Autism Spectrum Disorder) and another student is
transgender. How would you address these topics during the ﬁrst mentor group
session (if at all)?
●

A: Welcome all students to the program. Do not disclose any information in a student’s medical
info to the full group, all information should be private and it is up to the student whether or
not they would like to share. Some students may not want to disclose an ASD diagnosis or that
they are trans. Medical information can be helpful to understand how to better support
students, and if there are any concerns you can bring it up to the student in private and let
them know that they can come to you for support.

Facilitating a Mentor Group - Facilitator
Questions
Q: You are preparing to leave campus for the Saturday City Exploration Day. All of your mentor
group students except one are present at the bus pickup location 10 minutes before
departure time as instructed. How do you go about locating the student for an on-time
departure?
●

●

A: Since there is still some time before departure, text the student ﬁrst via Reach to ask them
where they are and reconﬁrm the departure time and that they need to be at the bus pickup
location now. If the student does not respond after a few minutes, give them a call via your
personal phone. If the student is not picking up, try other ways of ﬁnding the student, such as
seeing if their roommate is present and knows where they are.
If you need to leave your group to go ﬁnd the student in the dorms, leave your group with
another mentor who can ensure that we don’t lose anyone.
○ Q: When you have the student and are getting on the bus, do you need to do another
headcount?
○ A: Yes! Do a ﬁnal count of your mentor group to make sure you are not missing anyone.
Someone may have gone to the bathroom when you were not there. Always do a ﬁnal
head count before leaving a location.

TRUE COLORS
In this exercise, we will:
Seek to discover the qualities and characteristics of our own particular personality style
or type;

●
●

Gain an understanding of other personality styles

Key Outcomes of this exercise:

●

True Colors is a metaphor
○

●
●
●

Metaphors are a way of describing something by comparing it to something else in this case colors! Metaphors help us picture things more clearly and usually make
our writing or talking more exciting.

Each person is a unique blend of the four colors or styles—a spectrum.
There are no bad or good colors.
There are wide individual variations within each color spectrum.

TRUE COLORS - PURPOSE
We can use this exercise to:
– Improve teamwork
– Identify commonalities
– Acknowledge individuality
– Appreciate the gifts of others
– Value methods of others
We do not use this exercise to:
– Label or stereotype
– Make excuses for bad behavior
– Change other people

TRUE COLORS ACTIVITY

Everyone has been given 2 pages with a list of personality characteristics and traits on it.
As a group, each of you will compare all 4 boxes in each row starting with the ﬁrst row.
Try hard not analyze each word; just get a sense of each box to begin scoring them by which
one you think MOST describes you to which one LEAST describes you.
Score each of the four boxes in each row from most to least as it describes you:

● 4 = Most, 3 = A Lot, 2 = Somewhat, 1 = Least.

Note: If any of the scores in the colored boxes are less than 5 or greater than 20, you have made
an error. Pls go back and ﬁx the scores.

Scores - what do they tell us?
People usually have characteristics from all four
color styles
– One or two colors are typically dominant
Highest
– Dominant color
– Familiar attributes
– Comfortable displaying these characteristics
– Happen automatically
Second highest
– Major inﬂuence on ﬁrst color
– May be interchangeable with your ﬁrst color

Third and fourth colors
– Unnatural
– Underdeveloped
– May admire in others
– May cause the most conﬂict in
others

cognitive variations. While neurodivergence comes
with unique challenges, it also brings valuable
strengths like creativity, problem-solving, and deep
focus.

What is Anaphylaxis?
●
●
●

Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction.
It typically occurs within minutes of exposure to an allergen (e.g., food, insect stings, medications).
It involves more than just a skin reaction — a combination of symptoms affecting multiple body systems.

➡ Skin + Something Else = Anaphylaxis
