## Duty of Care

### Definition

**Duty of Care** is the moral and legal obligation of organizations to their participants in maintaining wellbeing, security, and safety while traveling.

### Three Pillars of Duty of Care

#### 1. Preparation & Awareness
- Ensure staff and guides understand student needs and logistics
- Know where to find resources and when to act
- Review surveys and catch anomalies
- Complete all required training

#### 2. Communication & Oversight
- Identify trends and follow up
- Maintain clear HQ-to-field communication
- Act responsibly and model good judgment
- Avoid dangerous or irresponsible activities

#### 3. Responsibility & Leadership
- Lead by example to set the standard
- Take ownership of decisions
- Never leave situations unresolved
- Escalate when needed

### How Duty of Care Can Be Violated

**Not managing program needs:**
- Initial actions are important but providing ongoing support is critical
- Staff check-ins required
- Student or parent complaints must be addressed
- Student illness requires follow-up medical visits
- Near-misses or local partner performance issues must be documented

**Not communicating effectively:**
- Lapses in communication with stakeholders (parents, guardians, students, field staff)
- Failure to communicate with HQ teams
- Failure to communicate with local partners

**Failure to escalate and inform:**
- Not reporting incidents to appropriate level
- Keeping information from leadership
- Waiting too long to involve HQ

## Incident Response Framework

### The 3 C's of Incident Response

1. **CARE:** "I care about the situation, its urgency, and your wellbeing. It's my top priority."

2. **COMPASSION & EMPATHY:** Understand the perspective of someone in a foreign area without family.

3. **CONTROL:** Clearly communicate that you have resources and can provide help.

### Key Incident Response Concepts

#### Flexibility & Teamwork
- Every incident is unique
- Learn from prior events without assumptions
- **Collaborative mindset:**
  - Don't act alone
  - Ask: "Am I missing anything?"
  - Seek: "What do you need now?"

#### Gathering Information
- Collect all available data as currently known
- Document everything immediately
- **REMAIN NEUTRAL**
- Use objective language
- Don't make assumptions

#### Focus on Core Needs
- Human safety (first priority)
- Human needs: food, water, accommodation, transport
- Containment: isolating the event from spreading
- Effective communications

### 4-Step Incident Response Process

#### STEP 1: STABILIZE

**Ensure human safety, stay within scope of practice**

**Immediate actions:**
- Secure the scene
- Call for or seek emergency support needs
- Call 9-1-1 if life-threatening
- Separate involved parties if needed
- Account for all students
- Ensure basic needs met (safe location, water, etc.)

**Your role:** Keep people safe, not to investigate or determine fault at this stage.

#### STEP 2: CATEGORIZE

**Determine incident level:**

**Non-Urgent:**
- Updates or needs
- May or may not require response
- Can wait for business hours
- Examples: Student request for room change, minor supply need

**Urgent:**
- Problematic situations requiring a response
- Need attention within hours
- Examples: Student illness requiring doctor visit, behavioral issue, parent concern

**Emergency:**
- Real or perceived events with potential for severe injury or threat to life
- Immediate response required
- Examples: Medical emergency, student missing, violence, natural disaster

#### STEP 3: COMMUNICATE

**Establish facts:**
- Who, What, Where, When
- Actions taken thus far

**Determine assistance needs:**
- What help is needed and when
- Who needs to be notified (see escalation pathways below)
- What resources are required

**Escalate appropriately:**
- In some cases insurance needs notification within 48 hours
- Life-threatening = immediate escalation
- Sexual harassment/assault = immediate escalation
- Student missing = immediate escalation

#### STEP 4: DOCUMENT

**Ensure reporting completed:**
- Incident Report filed within 24 hours
- All facts documented
- Timeline clearly established
- All involved parties interviewed

**Planning and follow-up:**
- Set follow-up touchpoints
- Assign tasks for ongoing case management
- Close case when fully resolved
- Share lessons learned with team

## Case Management - Green/Yellow/Red System

### GREEN Cases

**Management:** Usually managed in the field/on campus

**Characteristics:**
- Risk: Low
- Duration: Brief
- Intensity: Low to moderate
- Frequency: Usually linked to a specific event
- Severity: Low

**Examples:**
- Student feeling homesick
- Minor roommate conflict
- Student forgets to check in on REACH
- Late to class once
- Minor dietary preference issue

**Response:**
- Handled by Campus Director and team
- Document in staff notes
- May send email to alerts@ for tracking
- No HQ involvement needed unless pattern develops

### YELLOW Cases

**Management:** Usually managed by field team on campus and supported by HQ team

**Characteristics:**
- Risk: Low-moderate
- Duration: Potentially chronic
- Intensity: Moderate
- Frequency: Daily/weekly
- Severity: Low-moderate

**Examples:**
- Student repeatedly late to class
- Ongoing roommate conflicts
- Student struggling academically
- Minor behavioral issues forming pattern
- Parent expressing repeated concerns

**Response:**
- Campus Director manages with PD support
- Email to alerts@ to create case
- Incident Report may be required
- HQ monitors and provides guidance
- Regular check-ins established
- May require parent communication

### RED Cases (Escalated/Crisis)

**Management:** Managed by HQ escalated/crisis team and supported by field staff/campus

**Characteristics:**
- Risk: Moderate-high
- Duration: Acute or chronic
- Intensity: Severe
- Frequency: Daily
- Severity: Moderate-high

**Examples:**
- Medical emergency
- Student dismissal
- Sexual harassment/assault allegation
- Student missing
- Mental health crisis
- Violence or threats
- Significant safety concern
- Natural disaster
- Media attention

**Response:**
- Immediate escalation to PD
- PD escalates to VP/Executive Directors
- HQ crisis team activated
- Campus staff follows HQ directives
- Detailed documentation required
- Legal/insurance notified if appropriate
- Media response plan if needed

### Campus Director to Program Director

**MUST escalate immediately for:**

- **Medical issues** (especially if urgent care visit or more is considered)
- **Any early departure**
- **Violation of Code of Conduct** (especially if potential need to send student home)
- **Any real or perceived sexual harassment/assault**
- **Any concerns for student or staff safety/wellbeing**
- **Any "near miss" experience** that may have to be communicated (medical and other)
- **Accumulation of minor issues** which together form a larger issue
- **Any meaningful risk to SSB standing/reputation**
- **Expressed dissatisfaction** of a student with program from student, parent, or other
- **Any obvious crisis situation** (student or staff medical emergency, armed robbery, kidnapping, assault, rape, natural disaster, PR issue)

**Process:**
1. **Call PD immediately** (don't wait for email)
2. Send email with key facts to PD
3. PD may escalate further based on severity
4. CD follows PD guidance
5. CD completes incident report within 24 hours
6. CD notifies conference rep and campus police if appropriate

### Program Director to VP to Executive Support

**Escalate to Executive Level for:**

**Sexual Harassment or Assault:**
- Any matters of this nature must be reported immediately
- Cannot wait for business hours

**Grave Programmatic Challenges & Near Misses:**
- Incidents with propensity to risk life, limb, or organizational reputation

**Clear and Urgent Crisis Situations:**
- Immediate response required for any critical incident

**Process:**
- PD calls VP immediately
- VP activates crisis team
- Executive Director (ED) Terra Education or ED Risk Management involved
- Crisis team coordinates response
- Field staff implements directives from HQ

### Golden Rule of Escalation

**"If in doubt, escalate it!!! We can always de-escalate!"**

**Better to:**
- Over-communicate and be told it wasn't needed
- Loop in leadership who can assess and decide

**Never:**
- Keep concerning information to yourself
- Wait to see if situation gets worse
- Assume someone else has escalated

## Alerts@ Email System

### What is Alerts@?

**Email:** alerts@summerspringboard.com

**Purpose:** Centralized system for tracking and managing incidents, concerns, and updates

**How it works:**
- Any email sent to alerts@ automatically creates a case in Salesforce
- Case visible to all HQ staff
- If you reply from within the Salesforce case, subsequent emails on that thread update the case (not create new ones)

### What Should Be Sent to Alerts@

**Send to alerts@ for:**

- Last-minute flight itinerary changes (delays, missed connections, cancellations)
- Student canceling last minute
- Student joining or changing program last minute
- Student medical information updates
- Expressed parent concern
- Student late to class (if pattern)
- Behavioral issues needing tracking
- Items that could turn into bigger issue
- Anything requiring follow-up or tracking
- Situations you want HQ aware of

**WHY:** Creates documentation trail, allows HQ to monitor patterns, ensures nothing falls through cracks.

**IMPORTANT:** To prevent duplicate cases, all communications about a specific case should be made through the same case on Salesforce.

**REMEMBER:** A case is created EVERY TIME an email is sent to alerts@. Check if case already exists before creating new email thread.

### How to Handle Alerts@ Emails

**If you are Campus Director or on-call staff:**

1. Read the alert
2. Determine who will handle
3. Reply all with single word "handling" and remove alerts@ from the email
4. This signals to rest of team that it's being addressed
5. Handle the issue
6. Update the case in Salesforce with resolution
7. Close case when fully resolved

**If you see alert and someone already replied "handling":**
- Do not also respond
- Trust that person is handling it
- If you have helpful information, you can reply within the case thread

**START HERE → Ask these questions:**

**Question 1: Is this about a STAFF MEMBER's performance?**
- YES, and it's minor/coaching level → **Observation Report** (GREEN)
- YES, and it's serious/repeated → **Incident Report** (YELLOW/RED)

**Question 3: Could this have legal/liability implications?**
- YES → **Incident Report** (always)
- NO, just want HQ aware → **alerts@ email**

**Still unsure?** → File an **Incident Report**. Better to over-document than under-document.

## Incident Reports

### When to Submit Incident Report

**Submit incident report within 24 hours for:**

**Medical Issues:**
- Any visit to urgent care, ER, or doctor
- Any injury requiring more than basic first aid
- Any illness requiring monitoring
- Any mental health concerns
- Any medication issues

**Code of Conduct Violations:**
- Any official verbal warning given
- All behavior contracts
- All dismissals
- Any concerning behavioral patterns

**Safety Concerns:**
- Near misses (almost accidents)
- Safety hazards discovered
- Campus security incidents
- Student whereabouts concerns

**Staff Issues:**
- Staff policy violations
- Staff performance concerns (yellow level and above)
- Staff injuries
- Staff conflicts requiring intervention

**Parent/Student Concerns:**
- Expressed dissatisfaction with program
- Formal complaints
- Requests for refunds or compensation
- Threats of legal action

**Crisis Situations:**
- Any emergency (ALWAYS CALL FIRST, then document)
- Natural disasters
- Campus emergencies (fire, lockdown, etc.)
- Media inquiries
- Police involvement

**General Rule:** If you're wondering whether to write an incident report, write it. Over-documentation is better than under-documentation.

### What Goes in an Incident Report

**Incident Narrative must include:**

**WHO:**
- Names of all students involved
- Names of all staff involved
- Names of all witnesses
- Names of parents contacted

**WHAT:**
- Objective description of what happened
- Use direct quotes when possible
- "Student said: 'I hate it here'" not "Student was upset"
- Stick to observable facts, not interpretations
- "Student was shouting" not "Student was crazy"

**WHEN:**
- Date and exact time incident occurred
- Timeline of events
- When staff were notified
- When parents were contacted
- When authorities involved (if applicable)

**WHERE:**
- Specific location (building, room number, location on campus)
- Include address if off-campus

**WHY:**
- Background context if relevant
- Any precipitating factors
- Student's explanation (if given)

**HOW:**
- How situation unfolded
- How it was discovered/reported
- How it was handled

**Additional Required Information:**

- What authorities have been involved? (police, campus security, fire dept, etc.)
- What facilities visited? (urgent care, hospital, etc.)
- Any treatment given?
- Current student disposition? (returned to program, sent home, in hospital, etc.)
- What has been shared with family?
- Will the case need monitoring, or can it be closed?
- What follow-up is needed and by whom?

### Writing Solid Incident Reports

**Objective Tone:**
- State facts, not opinions
- ✅ "Student was shouting and waved arms"
- ❌ "Student was out of control"

**Timeline:**
- Include exact times of incidents and notifications
- ✅ "At 10:45pm, mentor conducting bed checks heard noise from room 305"
- ❌ "Late at night, mentor heard noise"

**Action Taken:**
- Explicitly state what you did to resolve
- ✅ "Separated students, called parents at 11:15pm, placed both on behavior contracts"
- ❌ "Handled the situation appropriately"

**Identification:**
- Use student/staff full names, not just pronouns
- ✅ "Student Renae Pink was crying"
- ❌ "A student was crying"

**If it isn't written down, it didn't happen!**

## Case Management and Follow-Up

### Creating Cases

**Cases can be created three ways:**

1. **Manually:** SSB Salesforce user logs into Salesforce and creates case

2. **Automatically via alerts@:** Email to alerts@summerspringboard.com automatically creates case
   - If you respond from within Salesforce case, subsequent emails on that thread update the case (don't create new ones)

3. **Incident Report:** Submitting incident report creates case that requires follow-up

### Following Up on Cases

**Program Directors responsible for:**
- Checking cases daily (usually every morning)
- Reaching out to Campus Directors for case status updates
- Ensuring cases can be closed
- Monitoring patterns across multiple students/staff

**Campus Directors responsible for:**
- Responding to PD inquiries about case status
- Updating cases as situations develop
- Being go-between when mentor submits incident report
- Proposing when cases can be closed

**Examples of follow-up needed:**
- If student was sick yesterday, get update to see if better
- If flight was delayed, confirm all students arrived
- If parent was upset, confirm they've been satisfied
- If student on behavior contract, monitor compliance

### Closing Cases

**Cases can be closed when:**
- Issue fully resolved
- No further follow-up needed
- All parties satisfied
- Documentation complete

**If mentor writes "case closed" in incident report:**
- No follow-up required
- PD can close case directly

**If ongoing monitoring needed:**
- Case remains open
- Set follow-up tasks
- Assign to specific person
- Set due dates

### Good Systems for Managing Cases

**Program Director and Campus Director should:**
- Have daily check-in call/meeting during program
- Review all open cases
- Determine which can be closed
- Identify which need escalation
- Assign follow-up tasks
- Track patterns across students

**Example system:**
- Morning PD-CD call at 9am
- Review case list together
- CD provides updates
- PD closes resolved cases or assigns follow-up

## Mandated Reporting

### Legal Requirements

**All Summer Springboard staff are mandated reporters.**

**This means:**
- Legal obligation to report suspected child abuse or neglect
- Applies in all states
- Criminal penalties for failure to report
- Cannot delegate responsibility
- Cannot investigate yourself before reporting

### What Must Be Reported

**Report immediately if you have reasonable suspicion of:**

- Physical abuse (hitting, burning, excessive discipline)
- Sexual abuse (any sexual contact or exploitation)
- Emotional/psychological abuse (pattern of harm to child's emotional development)
- Neglect (failure to provide basic needs)
- Endangerment (placing child at risk of harm)

**"Reasonable suspicion" means:**
- You don't need proof
- You don't need to investigate
- If a reasonable person would suspect abuse, you must report
- When in doubt, report

### How to Report

**Step 1:** Immediately tell your Campus Director

**Step 2:** Campus Director immediately contacts:
- Program Director
- Risk Management Director (Maria Balmaseda: 347-210-4976)

**Step 3:** HQ coordinates with legal counsel and makes official report to:
- Local child protective services
- Law enforcement if appropriate

**Step 4:** Document everything
- What you observed or were told
- When and where
- Who was involved
- What you did
- Who you notified

**Timeline:** Report must be made immediately, typically within 24 hours of becoming aware.

### What NOT to Do

**Do NOT:**
- Investigate yourself
- Contact parents before reporting (unless HQ directs)
- Confront suspected abuser
- Promise student confidentiality (you must report)
- Wait to see if it happens again
- Ask leading questions of student
- Discuss with other staff beyond those who need to know

### Protection for Reporters

- Good-faith reports are protected by law
- Cannot be sued for making required report
- Cannot be fired or retaliated against
- Failure to report can result in criminal charges

## Special Incident Types

### Medical Emergencies

**Life-threatening (chest pain, difficulty breathing, severe bleeding, unresponsive, severe allergic reaction, head injury with loss of consciousness):**

1. Call 9-1-1 immediately
2. Provide first aid if trained and safe to do so
3. Call Campus Director immediately
4. Call parent immediately
5. Assign staff to stay with student
6. Assign staff to meet ambulance
7. Staff accompanies student to hospital
8. Campus Director notifies Program Director
9. Detailed incident report within 24 hours

**Non-life-threatening but requires doctor (persistent pain, possible fracture, high fever, vomiting, allergic reaction managed with EpiPen, concerning symptoms):**

1. Call Campus Director
2. Campus Director calls parent
3. Campus Director determines if urgent care or ER
4. Staff accompanies student
5. Campus Director notifies Program Director
6. Incident report within 24 hours
7. Follow-up on treatment plan

**Minor (small cuts, minor headaches, minor stomach aches, small bruises):**

1. Provide basic first aid
2. Document in daily notes
3. Monitor student
4. Escalate if doesn't improve

### Student Missing

**If student cannot be located:**

**Immediate actions (first 15 minutes):**
1. Check all likely locations (room, bathroom, common areas, dining hall, etc.)
2. Call/text student on REACH
3. Ask roommate and friends when last seen
4. Check REACH for last check-in location

**If not found after 15 minutes:**
1. Call Campus Director immediately (if not already involved)
2. Campus Director calls parents
3. Campus Director calls Program Director
4. Expand search (check entire campus, nearby areas)
5. Call campus security/police
6. Prepare to call 9-1-1

**If not found after 30 minutes:**
1. Call 9-1-1
2. Activate crisis response team
3. HQ involved at executive level
4. Follow police directives
5. Prepare public statement if needed

**Once found:**
1. Ensure student safe and evaluated medically if needed
2. Determine what happened
3. Parent notification and update
4. Address any code of conduct violations
5. Detailed incident report
6. Update all parties who were searching

### Sexual Harassment or Assault Allegations

**Any report of sexual harassment or assault requires immediate escalation.**

**Immediate actions:**
1. Thank student for reporting
2. Ensure student is physically safe
3. DO NOT investigate or ask detailed questions
4. Tell student you must report this and who you'll tell
5. Immediately notify Campus Director
6. Campus Director immediately calls Program Director
7. Program Director immediately escalates to VP/ED level

**Within first hour:**
1. Separate involved students
2. Ensure impacted student has support person
3. Contact parents of impacted student
4. Begin documentation
5. HQ determines if police should be called

**Do NOT:**
- Question student in detail (trained investigator will do this)
- Contact accused student's parents before HQ advises
- Allow students to confront each other
- Make promises about confidentiality
- Make judgments about credibility

**Follow HQ directives for:**
- Investigation process
- Parent communications
- Police involvement
- Student placement (room changes, supervision, etc.)
- Communications plan

### Death of Student or Staff

**If student or staff member dies:**

1. Confirm with medical professionals
2. Call Program Director immediately (BEFORE notifying anyone else)
3. Program Director activates crisis team and executive leadership
4. HQ coordinates all notifications:
   - Family notification (done by appropriate authorities/HQ, never by campus staff)
   - Staff notification
   - Student notification
   - Communications plan
5. Campus staff provides support to community
6. Follow all HQ directives
7. Grief counseling arranged
8. Memorial planning coordinated through HQ

**DO NOT:**
- Notify other students before HQ advises
- Post on social media
- Talk to media
- Notify other families before HQ advises
- Make assumptions about cause of death

### Natural Disasters or Campus Emergencies

**Fire, earthquake, active shooter, severe weather, etc.:**

1. Follow campus emergency protocols
2. Account for all students
3. Move students to safe location
4. Call Campus Director (if not with you)
5. Campus Director calls Program Director
6. Program Director escalates to HQ crisis team
7. Follow campus authority directives
8. Take attendance repeatedly
9. Communicate with HQ regularly
10. Wait for all-clear before resuming activities

**Parent communication:**
- HQ coordinates mass communication to all families
- Campus staff should not individual call unless HQ directs
- Updates provided regularly through official channels

### Media Inquiries

**If reporter contacts you:**

1. Say: "Thank you for reaching out. I'll have the appropriate person contact you. Can I have your name and number?"
2. Get: Name, organization, phone number, deadline, what they're asking about
3. Immediately call Campus Director
4. Campus Director immediately calls Program Director
5. DO NOT answer any questions
6. DO NOT say "no comment"
7. DO NOT speculate about what they might want

**HQ handles all media:**
- Coordinates messaging
- Determines who speaks (usually executive level)
- Approves all statements
- Monitors coverage

## Documentation Best Practices

### Objective vs. Subjective Language

**Use objective language:**
- ✅ "Student was crying and said 'I want to go home'"
- ❌ "Student was homesick"
- ✅ "Student's words were slurred and balance was unsteady"
- ❌ "Student was drunk"
- ✅ "Student pushed other student in the chest with both hands"
- ❌ "Student was violent"

**Include direct quotes:**
- Use quotation marks for exact words
- "Student said: 'I'm going to kick your ass'"
- Not: Student threatened other student

**Record observable facts:**
- What you saw
- What you heard
- What you smelled
- What you measured (temp, time, etc.)

**Avoid:**
- Assumptions
- Diagnoses
- Interpretations
- Opinions
- Judgments

### Timeline Documentation

**Use exact times:**
- ✅ "10:47pm - Mentor heard loud noise during bed checks"
- ✅ "10:52pm - Campus Director arrived at room"
- ✅ "11:15pm - Parents contacted"
- ❌ "Late at night, noise was heard"

**Create clear sequence:**
- Number events if helpful
- Show cause and effect
- Note any gaps in timeline
- Explain any delays

### Witness Information

**Record all witnesses:**
- Full names
- Roles (student, staff, instructor, bystander)
- What each person observed
- Contact information if not SSB participant

**Get statements:**
- Written when possible
- Signed and dated
- Separate witnesses before interviewing
- Don't let witnesses collaborate on story

### Photo/Video Evidence

**If appropriate and safe:**
- Photos of injuries (with consent)
- Photos of property damage
- Photos of substances or contraband (before disposal)
- Screenshots of relevant messages

**Do NOT:**
- Take photos that violate privacy
- Take photos of students in vulnerable moments
- Share photos beyond official incident documentation
- Post photos anywhere

### Chain of Custody for Evidence

**If evidence collected (contraband, substances, etc.):**

1. Document what was found
2. Photograph in place if possible
3. Seal in bag/container
4. Label with: what, where found, when, who found it
5. Give to Campus Director
6. Campus Director stores securely
7. Turn over to police if they request
8. Document transfer

## After-Action Reviews

### Purpose

After any significant incident, conduct after-action review to:
- Identify what went well
- Identify what could be improved
- Document lessons learned
- Update policies if needed
- Provide closure for staff

### Debrief with Participants (Students)

**Questions to ask:**
- What happened? (their perspective)
- What did you notice in yourself? (feelings, reactions)
- What support do you need now?
- What do we want to remember for next time?

**Goals:**
- Allow processing
- Provide support
- Gather information
- Plan for moving forward

### Debrief with Staff

**Questions to discuss:**
- What were the facts?
- What were our interpretations?
- What are our recommendations?
- What was front-loaded well?
- What was missing?
- Did our response match SSB's duty of care and risk tolerance?
- What follow-up is needed with leadership or host campus?

**Goals:**
- Learn from experience
- Improve protocols
- Support staff wellbeing
- Identify training needs
- Close loop on incident

### Lessons Learned Documentation

**After significant incidents, document:**
- What happened (brief summary)
- What worked well in response
- What could be improved
- Policy changes recommended
- Training needs identified
- Follow-up actions needed

**Share with:**
- HQ for policy review
- Other campuses if applicable
- Future campus teams
- Training curriculum development
